Module 1: Introduction to Information Security and Cybersecurity

Study Portfolio | Matheus Almeida e Silva

Introduction

The study of security begins with understanding its definition, basic principles, and the distinction between information security and cybersecurity.

Understanding Security

The word "security" comes from the Latin, meaning "free from care".

  1. Goal of Security: The "state of being free from danger".
  2. Process of Security: Defined as the "measures taken to ensure safety", or the "necessary steps to protect from harm".

Since complete security can never be fully achieved, the focus is often placed on the process rather than the goal.

Relationship Between Security and Convenience

It is crucial to understand that the relationship between security and convenience is inversely proportional. This means that as security is increased, convenience is decreased. Security is often described as sacrificing convenience for safety.

For example, a user who changes the screen timeout setting on their phone to 30 seconds to minimize risk (increased security) decreases convenience because they must now log in after 30 seconds of inactivity.

Fundamental Security Principles

There are two fundamental security concepts underpinning security practices:

1. Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA)

Modern security focuses on protecting information that provides value to people and enterprises. Three basic security protections must be extended over this information:

Concept Definition Example
Confidentiality Ensures that only approved individuals (authorized parties) can view or access sensitive information. Using software to encrypt a credit card number stored on a web server or using door locks to prevent access to physical servers.
Integrity Ensures that the information is correct and that no unauthorized person or malicious software has altered the data. Preventing an attacker from changing a purchase amount from $10,000.00 to $1.00.
Availability Ensures that the data is accessible to only authorized users when needed. Ensuring the total number of items ordered in an online purchase is available to a warehouse employee so the correct items can be shipped.

2. Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting (AAA)

The AAA principle (sometimes called "triple-A") involves controlling access to computer resources. This control process typically involves the following steps:

Action Description Computer Process
Identification The process of recognizing and distinguishing the user by reviewing credentials. User enters username.
Authentication The act of validating credentials as genuine. User provides password (allowing login).
Authorization Granting permission to perform a specific action or access specific data. User is granted access only to the specific services, devices, applications, and files needed for their job duties.
Accounting Creates a preserved record of who accessed the network, what resources they accessed, and when they disconnected. Information is recorded in a log file.

Security Controls

A security control is a safeguard (sometimes called a countermeasure) employed within an enterprise to protect the CIA of information. A control attempts to limit the exposure of an asset to a danger.

Controls are divided into four broad categories:

Table 1-2 Categories of Controls

Control Category Description Example
Managerial Controls that use administrative methods. An acceptable use policy specifying that users should not visit malicious websites.
Operational Controls implemented and executed by people. Conducting workshops to train users to identify and delete suspicious messages (security awareness training).
Technical Controls incorporated as part of hardware, software, or firmware. Hardware that blocks malicious content from entering the network.
Physical Controls that implement security in a defined structure and location. Installing a fence to prevent an unauthorized person from entering a building.

Additionally, there are six specific types of controls:

Table 1-3 Specific Control Types

Control Type Description Example
Deterrent Attempts to discourage security violations before they occur. Signs indicating that the area is under video surveillance.
Preventive Works to prevent the threat from contacting the vulnerability before the attack. Security awareness training for all users.
Directive Ensures a particular outcome is achieved, often through incentives. An incentive to employees who pass a training course.
Detective Identifies any threat that has reached the system during the attack. Installing motion detection sensors.
Compensating Provides an alternative to normal controls for some reason cannot be used during the attack. An infected computer is isolated on a different network.
Corrective Mitigates or lessens the damage caused after the incident. A virus is cleaned from an infected server.

Cybersecurity Versus Information Security

While the terms cybersecurity and information security are often used synonymously, they are technically different in scope.

  • Cybersecurity: Typically involves practices, processes, and technologies intended to protect devices, networks, and programs that process and store data in an electronic form.
  • Information Security: Protects "processed data" (information) that is essential in an enterprise business environment. This information may be in virtually any format, ranging from electronic files to paper documents.

Because business enterprises deal with information in a variety of formats, information security is often considered the most appropriate term in this setting.

Defining Information Security

Information security is formally defined as that which protects the integrity, confidentiality, and availability of information through products, people, and procedures on the devices that store, manipulate, and transmit the information.

The protection of information (which is processed, stored, and transmitted) is achieved through three layers:

Figure 1-2 Information Security Layers

  1. Policies and procedures
  2. People
  3. Products

(Policies and procedures enable people to understand how to use products to protect information). The goal of information security is to ensure protective measures are implemented, prevent system collapse during an attack, and allow for quick recovery.

Threat Actors and Their Motivations

A threat actor (or attacker) is an individual or entity responsible for attacks. Financial cybercrime often targets three categories: individual users, enterprises, and governments.

Attributes of Actors

The attributes of threat actors vary widely, including their level of sophistication/capability, resources/funding, and whether they are internal/external to the organization.

Categories and Motivations

Threat actors are classified into distinct categories, each with varying motivations:

Threat Actor Description and Skill Level Primary Motivation(s)
Unskilled Attackers Individuals who lack technical knowledge but use freely available automated attack software (scripts). Data exfiltration (unauthorized copying of data) or service disruption (obstructing business processes).
Shadow IT Employees who bypass corporate approval for technology purchases. Often ethical (sound moral principles, seeking efficiency), but it weakens security.
Organized Crime Highly centralized criminal enterprises that have moved into cyberattacks, which they consider less risky and more rewarding. Financial gain (earning revenue).
Insiders Employees, contractors, or business partners who pose a threat from a position of trust. Difficult to recognize as threats. Revenge or blackmail (coercion using threats).
Hacktivists Groups motivated by strong philosophical/political beliefs (ideology). Disruption/chaos (to make a political statement).
Nation-State Actors State-sponsored attackers employed by governments. Espionage (spying) or war (armed hostile combat).
Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) A new class of attack often associated with nation-state actors, using innovative tools to silently extract data over an extended period of time (persistent). Extraction of highly sensitive economic, proprietary, or national security information.
Competitors Launch attacks to steal classified information. Gain competitive advantage (e.g., stealing new product research or customer lists).
Brokers Individuals who discover weaknesses and sell this knowledge to the highest bidder (other attackers or governments) instead of reporting to the vendor. High-price financial gain for unknown weaknesses.
Cyberterrorists Attack a nation's network and computer infrastructure. Cause disruption and panic among citizens (e.g., attacking the electrical power grid).

How Attacks Occur: Vectors and Vulnerabilities

Threat actors target different digital platforms to exploit weaknesses; these platforms are called threat vectors or attack surfaces.

Threat Vectors and Attack Surfaces

Attack surfaces are categorized as mainstream (found in all technology settings) and specialized.

Mainstream Attack Surfaces (Software, Hardware, and Network)

Category Attack Surface Explanation
Software Vulnerable software Software containing security vulnerabilities; can be client-based or agentless.
Software File-based Attacks focusing on infecting individual files on a computer.
Software Image-based An image is a copy of all computer contents; a vulnerability permits an attack on the image.
Hardware Unsupported systems/applications Computer systems and applications that are no longer supported and do not receive security updates.
Hardware Removable devices Removable media (like a USB flash drive) can be infected on an unsecure computer and then infect a "clean" device.
Network Unsecure networks Unsecured wired and wireless networks that an attacker can breach, granting access to hundreds of connected devices.
Network Open service ports Unnecessary ports that are not disabled, allowing attackers access to devices and networks.
Network Default credentials Networks may have default administrator accounts with well-known passwords that attackers can target.

Specialized Threat Vectors (Communications and Supply Chain)

1. Communications (Message-based)

Tools where the identity of the other person may not always be validated, making them popular targets.

  • Email: Almost 94% of malware is delivered through email, tricking users into opening attachments or clicking malicious links.
  • Texts (SMS): Short Message Service (SMS) is used to send texts containing a link that installs malware.
  • Instant Messages (IM): Real-time messages (e.g., Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp) where threat actors pretend to be a reliable source.
  • Voice Calls: Often directed at older users who tend to trust a helpful voice, where attackers may pretend to be a bank to steal passwords.
2. Supply Chain

A network that moves a product from its creation to the end-user, including suppliers, manufacturers, vendors, and service providers. Each link can be a potential threat vector.

  • Supply Chain Infections: Malware can be injected into the product during manufacturing, storage, and distribution.
  • Hardware Infections: Considered especially dangerous when users receive brand-new infected devices.
  • Software Infections: Threat actors insert malicious code into the development pipeline, distributed as a malicious update.
  • Managed Service Providers (MSPs): Attackers target MSPs (who manage networks for small-to-medium enterprises, SMEs) to distribute malware to many clients downstream.
  • Open-Source Software: An alarming target where source code is freely available, allowing threat actors to add malicious code to a project downloaded by unsuspecting victims.

Categories of Vulnerabilities

A vulnerability (from the Latin meaning "wound") is the state of being exposed to the possibility of being attacked or harmed. Vulnerabilities are categorized as software, hardware, misconfigurations, and zero-day.

1. Software Vulnerabilities

Vulnerabilities are predominately found in software, with Operating System (OS) software being the chief culprit (OS-based vulnerabilities). Applications can also have vulnerabilities.

2. Hardware Vulnerabilities

Vulnerability Explanation Consequences/Risks
Difficulty Patching Firmware Firmware (software embedded in hardware) can be difficult to update to address code vulnerabilities, requiring specialized steps. Unpatched vulnerabilities become a target for attackers.
Legacy Platforms Older hardware platforms for which a more modern version is available but have not been updated, lacking recent security fixes. Prime targets for attacks.
End-of-Life (EOL) Hardware The end of the manufacturing lifespan, where vendor support ceases (including tech support and firmware updates). The primary risk is that vulnerabilities will not be patched. High cost of maintenance, loss of comprehensive security, and legal consequences (failure to comply with regulatory standards). EOL does not mean the device will no longer function.

3. Misconfigurations

Configuration settings that are not properly implemented result in misconfigurations and subsequent vulnerabilities.

Misconfiguration Explanation
Default settings Settings predetermined by the vendor for usability, not security.
Open ports and services Services configured to provide maximum access, allowing attackers to close those specific to the organization.
Unsecured root accounts A root account grants unfettered access to all resources.
Open permissions Excessive user permissions over restricted files (e.g., granting Write/Execute when only Read is needed).
Unsecure protocols Telecommunications protocols that do not provide adequate security (e.g., Telnet or SNMPv1).
Weak encryption Choosing a known vulnerable encryption mechanism or a key value that is too short.
Errors Human mistakes in selecting settings without considering security implications.

4. Zero-Day Vulnerabilities

A zero-day vulnerability is one for which there is no advanced warning (zero days of warning) because there was no prior knowledge of its existence. Attackers exploit it before the developer knows about it, meaning no security fixes are available. These are considered extremely serious.

Impacts of Successful Attacks

A successful attack always results in several negative impacts, primarily focusing on data.

Data Impacts

The consequences of a successful attack on data include:

Table 1-8 Consequences of Data Attack

Impact Description Example
Data Loss The destruction of data so that it cannot be recovered. Maliciously erasing patient data used for cancer research.
Data Exfiltration Stealing data to distribute it to other parties. Taking a list of current customers and selling it to a competitor.
Data Breach Stealing data to disclose it in an unauthorized fashion. Theft of credit card numbers to sell to other threat actors.
Identity Theft Taking personally identifiable information (PII) to impersonate someone. Stealing a Social Security number to secure a bank loan in the victim's name.

Overall Enterprise Effects

A successful attack can also have grave consequences for the organization:

  • Financial Loss (Availability Loss): Systems may become inaccessible, resulting in lost productivity and impacting normal tasks for generating income.
  • Reputation: The public perception of the enterprise is impacted. An organization that suffers a customer data theft faces a serious negative impression in the eyes of the public.

Information Security Resources

Defenders utilize a variety of external cybersecurity resources to help ward off attacks. These resources include frameworks, regulations, legislation, standards, benchmarks/secure configuration guides, and information sources.

1. Frameworks

An information security framework is a series of documented processes used to define policies and procedures for the implementation and management of security controls.

The frameworks from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) are among the most popular. NIST frameworks are divided into three basic parts:

  1. Framework Core: Defines the activities needed to attain different security results.
  2. Implementation Tiers: Help organizations identify their level of compliance.
  3. Profiles: Relate the status of the organization's cybersecurity measures to compliance roadmaps.

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) Version 1.1 is used to measure cybersecurity practices. It is composed of five functions:

Figure 1-5 NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) Functions

  1. Identify
  2. Protect
  3. Detect
  4. Respond
  5. Recover

The Framework Core is further subdivided into four elements:

Table 1-9 NIST Framework Core Elements

Element Name Description Example
Functions The most basic information security tasks. Identify, protect, detect, respond, and recover.
Categories Tasks to be carried out for each of the five functions. To protect (a function), organizations must implement software updates, install antivirus, and have access control policies.
Subcategories Tasks or challenges associated with each category. To implement software updates (a category), organizations must be sure that Windows computers have auto-updates turned on.
Information sources Documents or manuals that detail specific tasks for users and explain how to accomplish the tasks. A document detailing how auto-updates are enabled on Windows computers.

2. Regulations and Legislation

  • Regulations: Rules developed by professional organizations or government agencies. The process of adhering to them is called regulatory compliance. Organizations must follow multiple regulations: broadly applicable, industry-specific, U.S. state, and international.
  • Legislation: Specific national, territorial, and state laws enacted by governing bodies. The large number of entities passing multiple (and sometimes contradictory) laws often leads to a "hodge-podge" of legislation, which is not always a good cybersecurity resource.

3. Standards

A standard is a document approved through consensus by a recognized standardization body. It provides frameworks, rules, or guidelines for products or processes. Strictly speaking, compliance is not mandatory, but there may be restrictions for organizations that do not comply. An example is the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS), which provides a minimum degree of security for handling customer card information.

4. Benchmarks/Secure Configuration Guides

These are guidelines usually distributed by hardware manufacturers and software developers. They serve as instructions for configuring a device or software so that it is resilient to attacks. They are usually platform-/vendor-specific guides.

5. Information Sources

A variety of sources provide specialized in-depth information.

  • Requests for Comments (RFCs): Document "white papers" authored by technology specialists, engineers, and scientists.
  • Data Feeds: Continually maintained databases of the latest cybersecurity incidents. Common feeds include vulnerability feeds (latest vulnerabilities) and threat feeds (current threats and attacks).
  • Adversary Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTP): A database that records the behavior of threat actors and how they manage attacks.

The CompTIA Security+ Certification

The CompTIA Security+ is a vendor-neutral credential. It requires passing the current certification exam SY0-701. A successful candidate must demonstrate the knowledge and skills necessary to:

  • Identify attacks, threats, and vulnerabilities.
  • Design a strong security architecture.
  • Implement security controls.
  • Be knowledgeable of security operations and incident response.
  • Be well versed in Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) requirements.

Information Security Fundamentals Study Guide

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

Instructions: Click on a question to reveal the answer (2-3 complete sentences based on the provided source material).

1. Explain the relationship between security and convenience as described in the text.

The relationship between security and convenience is inversely proportional. This means that as security measures are increased, the convenience of using a system or device typically decreases. For example, setting a shorter screen timeout on a phone increases security but requires the user to log in more frequently, which is less convenient.

2. What are the three core components of the CIA triad, and what does each component protect against?

The three components are Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. Confidentiality ensures that only authorized parties can view sensitive information. Integrity ensures that information is correct and has not been altered by unauthorized persons or software. Availability ensures that data is accessible to authorized users when needed.

3. Describe the AAA framework for controlling access to computer resources.

The AAA framework stands for Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting. Authentication validates a user's credentials as genuine. Authorization grants the authenticated user permission to take specific actions or access certain resources. Accounting creates a preserved record of user actions, such as what resources were accessed and when.

4. What is a security control, and what are the four broad categories of controls?

A security control is a safeguard or countermeasure employed within an enterprise to protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA) of information. The four broad categories are Managerial (using administrative methods), Operational (implemented by people), Technical (incorporated into hardware/software), and Physical (implementing security in a defined location).

5. Compare and contrast Information Security with Cybersecurity.

Cybersecurity primarily involves practices and technologies intended to protect devices, networks, and programs that handle data in electronic form. Information security is a broader term that protects "processed data" or information essential to a business, which can exist in any format, from electronic files to paper documents.

6. Who are nation-state actors, and what is an Advanced Persistent Threat (APT)?

Nation-state actors are state-sponsored attackers employed by governments to launch cyberattacks against their foes, which may be foreign governments or their own citizens. An Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) is a class of attack commonly associated with nation-state actors, which uses innovative tools to silently extract highly sensitive data over an extended period.

7. Define "shadow IT" and explain the primary motivation behind it.

Shadow IT is the process of employees bypassing the official corporate approval process to purchase and use technology, such as unapproved software or hardware. The motivation is often ethical, stemming from frustration with slow internal processes, but it can create security vulnerabilities by introducing unvetted technology to the corporate network.

8. What is a supply chain attack surface, and why are these infections considered especially dangerous?

A supply chain attack surface is a network that moves a product from creation to the end-user, where each link in the chain presents a potential threat vector. These infections are dangerous because malware can be injected into a product during manufacturing, storage, or distribution, meaning users can receive brand-new devices that are already compromised without their knowledge.

9. Explain what a zero-day vulnerability is and why it is considered extremely serious.

A zero-day vulnerability is a flaw that is discovered and exploited by attackers before the software developer is aware of it and can create a patch. It is considered extremely serious because there are zero days of warning, leaving systems open to attack with no security fixes available.

10. Describe at least three potential negative impacts of a successful data attack on an enterprise.

A successful attack can result in data loss, the destruction of data so it cannot be recovered. It can also lead to data exfiltration, which is the theft of data to distribute to other parties, such as selling a customer list to a competitor. Furthermore, an attack can damage an enterprise's reputation, leading to a loss of customer trust and business.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Accounting The process of creating a preserved record of who accessed an enterprise network, what resources they accessed, and when they disconnected. It is the third component of the AAA framework.
Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) A class of attacks, commonly associated with nation-state actors, that uses innovative attack tools to silently extract data over an extended period of time.
Attack surface (threat vector) A digital platform that threat actors target for their exploits. Examples include vulnerable software, unsecure networks, and supply chains.
Authentication The act of ensuring a user's credentials are authentic. It is the second step in the access control process, after identification.
Authorization The act of granting permission for a user to take a particular action or access specific resources, devices, applications, or files.
Availability A core security principle ensuring that data is accessible to authorized users. It is the 'A' in the CIA triad.
Compensating control A type of security control that provides an alternative to normal controls that cannot be used for some reason. It is typically implemented during an attack.
Confidentiality A core security principle ensuring that only authorized parties can view sensitive information. It is the 'C' in the CIA triad.
Control A safeguard (or countermeasure) employed within an enterprise to protect the CIA of information by attempting to limit the exposure of an asset to a danger.
Corrective control A type of security control that mitigates or lessens the damage caused by an incident. It is implemented after an attack.
Cybersecurity A range of practices, processes, and technologies intended to protect devices, networks, and programs that process and store data in an electronic form.
Data breach The consequence of an attack where data is stolen to be disclosed in an unauthorized fashion, such as the theft of credit card numbers.
Data exfiltration The consequence of an attack involving the unauthorized copying or stealing of data with the intent to distribute it to other parties.
Detective control A type of security control that identifies any threat that has reached the system. It is implemented during an attack.
Deterrent control A type of security control that attempts to discourage security violations before they occur, such as a sign indicating video surveillance.
Directive control A type of security control that ensures a particular outcome is achieved, often by providing incentives.
End-of-Life (EOL) The point at which a hardware device reaches the end of its manufacturing lifespan and is no longer manufactured or supported by the vendor. EOL hardware can become a vulnerability due to a lack of security updates.
Firmware Software that is embedded into hardware, providing low-level controls and instructions. Patching firmware vulnerabilities can be difficult.
Hacktivists A group or individual strongly motivated by philosophical or political beliefs (ideology) to carry out attacks, often to "make a statement" or cause disruption and chaos.
Information security The protection of the integrity, confidentiality, and availability of information through products, people, and procedures on devices that store, manipulate, and transmit it. It applies to information in any format, including paper documents.
Insider threat A threat to an enterprise that comes from its own employees, contractors, or business partners who hold a position of trust.
Integrity A core security principle ensuring that information is correct and has not been altered by an unauthorized person or malicious software. It is the 'I' in the CIA triad.
Legacy platform An older hardware platform for which a more modern version is available but has not been updated or replaced, often leaving it without recent security fixes.
Managed Service Providers (MSPs) IT service providers who manage networks, computers, and cloud resources for other enterprises, typically small-to-medium businesses. They can be a target in supply chain attacks.
Managerial controls A broad category of security controls that use administrative methods, such as creating an acceptable use policy.
Misconfigurations Improper implementation of configuration settings on hardware or software platforms, which can result in vulnerabilities.
Nation-state actors State-sponsored attackers employed by governments to launch cyberattacks against their foes.
Operational controls A broad category of security controls implemented and executed by people, such as conducting security awareness workshops.
Organized crime A close-knit group of highly centralized enterprises set up for illegal activities. In recent years, they have moved into cyberattacks, motivated by financial gain.
Physical controls A broad category of security controls that implement security in a defined structure and location, such as installing a fence or door locks.
Preventive control A type of security control that works to prevent a threat from coming in contact with a vulnerability. It is implemented before an attack.
Security The necessary steps to protect from harm; often defined as the process of taking measures to ensure safety.
Shadow IT The process of bypassing corporate approval for technology purchases, often done by well-intentioned employees, which can weaken an enterprise's security.
Supply chain A network that moves a product from its creation to the end-user. Each link in the chain can be a potential threat vector.
Technical controls A broad category of security controls incorporated as part of hardware, software, or firmware, such as a hardware firewall.
Threat actor An individual or entity responsible for attacks (also called a malicious actor or attacker).
Unskilled attackers Individuals who perform attacks despite lacking technical knowledge, typically by using easy-to-use automated attack software downloaded from the internet.
Vulnerability The state of being exposed to the possibility of being attacked or harmed.
Zero-day vulnerability A vulnerability that is exploited by attackers before the software developer has been able to release a patch, providing zero days of warning.
