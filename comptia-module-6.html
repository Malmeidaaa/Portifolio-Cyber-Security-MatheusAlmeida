Module 6: Lightweight Cryptography, Mobile Security, and Embedded Systems

Exploring security in resource-constrained environments, mobile devices, and embedded systems.

Lightweight Cryptography for Tiny Devices

Information security on small, low-power devices poses a unique challenge because the principle of "one size fits all" does not apply to small device cryptography. Encryption and decryption are resource-intensive tasks, requiring significant processing and storage capacities.

Tiny sensors and actuators, such as implanted medical devices, stress detectors inside roads and bridges, and keyless entry fobs for vehicles, lack the diminutive size and limited power (usually powered by tiny, embedded batteries) required to use the same cryptographic software that a standard computer uses. Therefore, lightweight electronics require lightweight cryptographic algorithms.

The Ascon Standard and the NIST Competition

In 2018, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) launched a competitive program to crown a new cryptography standard for tiny devices. NIST began by working with a wide range of industries to understand the needs of these devices and published strict criteria for submissions.

The criteria required the specialized cryptography to provide, first and foremost, strong encryption for lightweight electronics. Additionally, NIST sought other important factors: performance and flexibility in terms of speed, size, and energy consumption.

A total of 57 submissions were initially received. NIST employed a multi-round public review process in which cryptographers examined and attempted to find weaknesses in each candidate. Ten finalists were identified and subjected to even greater scrutiny.

Finally, in early 2023, the winner was announced: Ascon.

Details on Ascon

The Ascon cryptographic algorithm was developed in 2014 by a team of cryptographers from institutions in Austria, the Netherlands, and Germany. Ascon had the additional advantage of being selected in 2019 as the primary choice for lightweight authenticated encryption in a separate competition organized by the CAESAR group (Competition for Authenticated Encryption: Security, Applicability, and Robustness).

Ascon is a "family" of seven different technologies. This family offers a range of functionality that gives designers different options for various tasks. Two tasks are among the most important in lightweight cryptography:

  • Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data (AEAD): AEAD protects the confidentiality of a message but also allows extra information to be included without being encrypted. This extra information may include the header of a message or a device’s IP address. AEAD can be used in applications such as vehicle-to-vehicle communications or in identification tags that track packages in warehouses.
  • Hashing: Creates a digital fingerprint of a message, which can be used in lightweight cryptography to determine whether a software update is appropriate or has downloaded correctly.

It is important to note that Ascon is not intended to replace standard cryptography on devices that do not have resource constraints, nor is it primarily designed for post-quantum encryption. The final NIST standard is expected to be formally released in late 2023.

Mobile Device Security

The current technological landscape is marked by a dizzying array of sensors, miniature devices, and mobile technologies. Although users have flocked to these devices, attackers have also been drawn to them, targeting valuable personal data or manipulating them to spy on occupants. Security on these devices is usually scant or even nonexistent, making them easy targets.

Mobile devices (like smartphones and tablets) and endpoint devices (which include any device connected to a network, from PCs to fitness trackers and automotive telematics units) have become a prime target for attackers, and a vulnerability on a single endpoint can penetrate the entire network.

1. Types of Mobile Devices

There are a variety of mobile devices, with different shapes, sizes, and functionalities.

Tablets

Tablets are portable computing devices first introduced in 2010. They are designed for user convenience, being thinner, lighter, and more intuitive to use than other computer types. They generally lack a built-in keyboard or mouse, relying instead on a touch screen and touch gestures for input.

  • Characteristics: Classified by screen size (5–8.5 inches and 8.5–10 inches are common), generally weigh less than 1.5 pounds (0.68 kg), and are less than 1/2 inch thick.
  • Capabilities: Modern tablets are powerful enough to be alternatives to standard laptops, with large storage capacities and powerful processors.
  • Popular OSs: Apple iPadOS, Google Android, and Microsoft Windows.

Smartphones

Smartphones evolved from "feature phones" (cellular phones that integrated only a single feature, like a camera or MP3 player).

  • Characteristics: Have a touchscreen display, one or more powerful cameras, and external storage capabilities.
  • Functionality: Unlike feature phones, smartphones have an Operating System (OS), which gives them a much broader range of functionality. Users can install apps for productivity, social networking, music, etc., and can access the Internet through different means.

Wearables

These consist of devices that can be worn by the user instead of carried, offering greater flexibility and mobility.

  • Smartwatches: Originally used to receive smartphone notifications. Today, they can manage media playback, serve as a contactless payment system, and even call emergency services.
  • Fitness Trackers: Originally designed to monitor physical activity (like step counting), they have evolved into sophisticated health-monitoring devices. They can provide continual heart rate monitoring, GPS tracking, oxygen consumption, repetition counting, and sleep monitoring.
  • Reading Technology: Many fitness trackers and smartwatches use two different colors of LED lights (green and red) on the underside to take readings on the human body (photoplethysmography or PPG).
    • Green LEDs: Used during exercise, as human blood absorbs green light. They are more accurate for rapid heart rates.
    • Red LEDs: Used when the wearer is not exercising. Human blood reflects red light. They require less power, saving battery life.

Portable Computers

Devices that closely mirror the functionality of standard desktop computers, running the same OS (Windows, macOS, Linux) and application software.

  • Laptop: Designed to replicate the abilities of a desktop computer, while being small enough for use on a lap.
  • Notebook: A smaller, lightweight version of a laptop, easy to carry inside a briefcase.
  • Convertible or 2-in-1: Can take on a variety of physical "poses" (laptop, tablet, stand, or tent) due to a flexible hinge.
  • Web-based Computers: Internet-focused, containing a limited version of an OS and a web browser. Traditional software applications cannot be installed, and user storage is limited, as they access online web apps and save user files on the web. Common OSs include Google Chrome OS and Microsoft Windows in S Mode.

2. Mobile Device Connectivity Methods

Connectivity Method Description
Cellular The network is divided into hexagonal cells. Devices connect to a cell transmitter, which connects to a Mobile Telecommunication Switching Office (MTSO). The current technology is 5G, which delivers higher speeds but has a smaller cell footprint.
Wi-Fi A Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) designed to replace or supplement a wired LAN.
Bluetooth Wireless technology that uses short-range radio frequency transmissions to provide rapid device pairings.
USB Connections Used for data transfer. The standard connection type on mobile devices is USB Type-C. The European Union regulated that by the end of 2024, all mobile phones, tablets, and cameras sold in the EU must be equipped with a USB Type-C charging port.

3. Enterprise Deployment Models

Due to the widespread use of mobile devices, many organizations have adopted enterprise deployment models.

Model Description
Bring Your Own Device (BYOD) Allows users to use their own personal mobile devices for business purposes.
Corporate Owned, Personally Enabled (COPE) The company purchases and owns the device, but the employee can use it for personal purposes.
Choose Your Own Device (CYOD) The employee chooses from a limited list of approved devices, pays the upfront cost, but the company owns the contract, potentially providing a small monthly stipend.
Corporate-Owned (CO) Devices purchased and owned by the enterprise.

Benefits for the Enterprise (BYOD, COPE, and CYOD): Management flexibility, less oversight, cost savings (employees purchase or receive a stipend), simplified IT infrastructure, and reduced strain on IT help desks.
User Benefits (BYOD, COPE, and CYOD): Choice of device and carrier, and convenience of carrying only a single device.

4. Mobile Device Risks and Vulnerabilities

The increasing reliance on mobile devices for accessing sensitive data (customer lists, banking details, etc.) has heightened the interest of threat actors. Almost half of organizations (45%) reported suffering a compromise due to a mobile device.

Mobile Device Vulnerabilities

  • Physical Security: Portability is the greatest vulnerability. Devices are routinely lost or stolen (70 million smartphones lost annually). A stolen device can serve as an entry point to view corporate data or infiltrate the corporate network.
  • Limited Updates:
    • Apple iOS: Apple commits to providing over-the-air (OTA) updates for at least four years.
    • Google Android: OTA updates vary considerably. Google devices receive updates for three years; other OEMs (Original Equipment Manufacturers) are required to provide OTAs for at least two years. Many OEMs hesitate to distribute updates after two years to differentiate themselves or due to lack of financial incentive. This results in vulnerable devices if used for long periods.
  • Location Tracking (Geolocation): While useful (navigation, reducing bank card fraud), geolocation increases the risk of targeted physical attacks. Attackers can determine the user's current location to steal the device or inflict harm.
    • GPS Tagging (Geo-tagging): Adds geographical identification data to media (like digital photos), potentially inadvertently identifying a specific private location.
  • Unauthorized Recording: Video cameras and microphones on mobile devices are frequent targets, allowing a threat actor to secretly spy and record using malware.
  • Connection Vulnerabilities: Vulnerabilities in mobile device connections can also be exploited by threat actors.

Accessing Untrusted Content

Accessing untrusted content can introduce malware into the device.

  • Unapproved Apps: Users are normally required to access official stores (Apple App Store or Google Play Store) to download apps.
    • Jailbreaking and Rooting: Users can circumvent the built-in limitations on their smartphone (jailbreaking on iOS; rooting on Android). This grants access to the underlying OS and file system with full permissions, essentially bypassing all security protections.
    • Sideloading: Apps are downloaded from an unofficial and unapproved third-party source. These "rogue" apps have not been vetted and often contain malware.
    • Digital Markets Act (DMA): The EU passed the DMA in 2022 (applicable in 2023), requiring Apple and similar providers to offer alternatives to their own app stores.
  • Messaging: Threat actors can send SMS (Short Message Service), MMS (Multimedia Messaging Service), or RCS (Rich Communication Services) texts containing links to untrusted content or specially crafted videos that introduce malware.
  • QR Codes (Quick Response Codes): Two-dimensional barcodes that can store website URLs, plaintext, phone numbers, etc.. An attacker can create a QR code with a malicious URL (e.g., disguised as a bank website) that directs the device's web browser to the attacker's imposter site or a site that immediately downloads malware.

5. Protecting Mobile Devices

Several steps can be taken to make mobile devices more resilient to attacks (called hardening mobile devices).

Device Configuration

Proper configuration includes strong authentication, containerization, and loss/theft services.

  1. Strong Authentication:
    • Passcode: The most secure option, but users often avoid it due to the time required and difficulty of entering a complex password on a small virtual keyboard.
    • PIN (Personal Identification Number): Composed only of numbers, ranging from 4 to 16 digits. Short PINs (four digits) provide limited security; 26.83% of all PINs could be guessed by attempting just the top 20 most frequent PINs (the most common being 1234).
    • Screen Lock: The device is configured to automatically lock after a set time of inactivity (5 seconds to 50 minutes). Features like Android's Smart Lock can keep the device unlocked in a secure setting (in the user's pocket, connected to a trusted device, or located in a specific place).
  2. Containerization: Segments business apps and data from personal apps and data, useful in models like BYOD, COPE, and CYOD.
    • Advantages: Helps companies avoid data ownership privacy issues and allows them to erase only corporate data when necessary, without touching personal data.
  3. Enabling Loss or Theft Services: Security features that can locate the device or limit the damage.
Loss/Theft Security Feature Function
Alarm Sounds a loud alarm on the device.
GPS Location Locates the device on a map.
Remote Lockout Locks the device with a strong passcode.
Thief Picture Takes a picture of the thief if the password is entered incorrectly.
Remote Wipe Erases sensitive data stored on the mobile device, ensuring no sensitive data is compromised.

Mobile Management Tools

Support tools facilitate the management of mobile devices in the enterprise.

Tool Focus Description and Functionalities
Mobile Device Management (MDM) Device Allows remote management of devices by an organization. Offers OTA updates, email/Wi-Fi configuration, approval/quarantine of new devices, detection of jailbroken/rooted devices, enforcement of geofencing (using GPS to define geographical boundaries for app use), and selective erasing of corporate data.
Mobile Application Management (MAM) Applications Covers application management, distributing and controlling access to apps. Provides a higher level of control over apps but less control over the device (MDM is the opposite).
Mobile Content Management (MCM) Content A system tuned to provide content management (tracking editing history, version control, indexing) to hundreds or thousands of mobile devices.
Unified Endpoint Management (UEM) Devices, Applications, and Content A group of software tools with a single management interface that supports all capabilities of MDM, MAM, and MCM.

Embedded and Specialized Systems

Not all computing systems are desktops or mobile devices designed for direct human input. Embedded systems integrate computing capabilities into larger systems for specific functions.

1. Types of Embedded and Specialized Devices

Hardware and Software for Device Creation

  • Raspberry Pi: A low-cost, credit-card-sized computer motherboard. It can perform almost any task of a standard computer and is used to control specialized devices.
  • Arduino: Designed primarily as a controller for other devices. It has an 8-bit microcontroller (instead of a 64-bit microprocessor like the Raspberry Pi), limited RAM, and runs programs compiled for its platform (mostly in C++). It consumes very little power.
  • FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array): A hardware "chip" or integrated circuit (IC) that can be programmed by the user to carry out one or more logical operations. Used in aerospace, defense, medical electronics, and cybersecurity systems.
  • SoC (System on a Chip): Combines all the required electronic circuits of the various computer components onto a single IC chip.
  • RTOS (Real-Time Operating System): An OS specifically designed for an SoC in an embedded or specialized system. It is tuned to accommodate very high volumes of data that must be immediately processed for critical decision making (called high availability).

Industrial Systems

  • Industrial Control Systems (ICSs): Collect, monitor, and process real-time data so that machines can directly control devices (such as valves and motors) without human intervention.
  • Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA): A larger system that manages multiple ICSs, crucial for industrial organizations to maintain efficiency and reduce downtime. (Note that multiple ICSs are managed by a SCADA system, not the reverse).

Specialized Systems

These include digital smart meters (replacing analog meters by offering remote reading, bidirectional measurement, and fraud prevention), medical systems (insulin pumps, MRI devices), aircraft, and vehicles. The progression of embedded systems in automobiles is notable, controlling everything from engine ignition timing (1970s) to active safety systems (ABS) and fully automated functions (such as self-parking and collision warnings).

Internet of Things (IoT)

IoT is defined as a global infrastructure that enables advanced services by interconnecting things (physical and virtual) based on information and communication technologies. Simply put, it is connecting any device to the Internet for the purpose of sending and receiving data to be acted upon.

  • Devices: Refers to devices not previously considered computing devices connected to a data network, such as thermostats, coffee makers, washing machines, electric toothbrushes, and light bulbs. It is estimated that the total number of IoT devices will reach 27 billion by 2025.

2. Security Considerations for Embedded Systems

These systems and devices generally have low resilience (toughness) to resist attacks.

Security Constraints

Several limitations make security a challenge for embedded systems and specialized devices.

Constraint Description
Resource vs. Security Limitation in providing strong cryptography due to the scarcity of resources like time (low latency desired) and energy.
Inability to Patch Devices may be in inaccessible locations or too simple to support complex updates.
Power Energy is typically limited due to embedded batteries.
Cost Adding security features can significantly increase production costs.
Compute Limited processing and storage capacity.
Availability Critical systems (like ICS/SCADA) require high availability, making security maintenance stops difficult.

Hardening ICS and SCADA Systems

Steps to strengthen the security of ICS and SCADA systems include:

  • Clearly define security roles and responsibilities.
  • Conduct physical security surveys at remote sites.
  • Disconnect unnecessary connections to the SCADA network.
  • Document network architecture and identify critical systems.
  • Implement clear policies and conduct training.
  • Test possible attack scenarios.
  • Evaluate and strengthen the security of all connections.
  • Remove or disable unnecessary services.
  • Implement intrusion detection systems and incident monitoring.

Legislation and Regulation

Due to the importance of protecting IoT devices, laws and regulations are being enacted.

  • Federal (US): The Internet of Things (IoT) Cybersecurity Improvement Act of 2020 requires federal agencies to increase cybersecurity for IoT devices.
  • European Union (EU): The Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) (2022) sets common security standards for connected devices and services.
  • State (US): Laws in California and Oregon (effective 2020) require connected devices to be equipped with "reasonable security features" appropriate for the device's nature and the information it collects. A reasonable feature may be a preprogrammed password unique to each device or the requirement for the user to create a new authentication method on first access.
  • Medical Regulation: The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) (late 2022) gained new powers to set minimum security standards for medical-device manufacturers. This includes requiring new devices to include a software bill of materials and the ability to accept patches and updates.

Endpoint Vulnerabilities, Attacks, and Defenses (Malware)

The modern term commonly used for network-connected hardware is endpoints, reflecting the vast range of devices, from PCs to fitness trackers and industrial sensors.

1. Malware Attacks

Malware (malicious software) is software designed to interfere with a computer's normal functions and commit unwanted actions. Estimates indicate that 560,000 new instances of malware are detected daily.

Malware can be classified based on its primary action, using common vernacular terms: kidnap, eavesdrop, masquerade, launch, and sidestep.

A. Kidnap

  • Ransomware: Malicious software designed to extort money from victims in exchange for their endpoint device being restored.
    • Blocking Ransomware: The earliest form (around 2010), which prevents or blocks normal computer usage, often pretending to be from law enforcement or a software vendor.
    • Locking Ransomware: A more malicious form that encrypts some or all files on the device (including network drives) so they cannot be opened. The device remains functional so the ransom can be more easily paid.
    • Blended Attacks: Attackers first steal data and then encrypt it. If the ransom is not paid, they threaten to publicly release the stolen information (extortion).
    • Seriousness: Ransomware is the most serious malware threat due to the low barrier to entry (renting software for $800/month), high frequency (two out of three organizations in 2021), and high impact (33% of companies forced to temporarily suspend operations).

B. Eavesdrop

Malware that secretly listens to its targets.

  • Keylogger: Silently captures and stores each keystroke a user types. It can be a software program (which can also take screen captures and turn on the webcam) or a small hardware device inserted between the keyboard and the USB port.
  • Spyware: Secretly monitors users and collects personal or sensitive information without their approval, using the computer's resources.

C. Masquerade

Malware that attempts to deceive the user by pretending to be something else.

  • Trojan: An executable program that masquerades as performing a benign activity (like a calendar program) but also does something malicious (like scanning the system for credit card numbers).
  • Remote Access Trojan (RAT): A special type of Trojan that gives the threat agent unauthorized remote access to the victim's computer via configured communication protocols.

D. Launch

Malware that infects a computer to then launch attacks on others.

  • Virus:
    • File-Based Virus: Malicious computer code that becomes part of a file. It attaches to the file (appender infection), inserts a "jump" instruction to its code, executes its malicious payload, and then reproduces itself by inserting its code into another file on the same computer. It relies on user action (like file transfer via email or USB) to spread to other devices.
    • Fileless Virus: Does not attach itself to a file. Instead, it takes advantage of native services and processes that are part of the OS to avoid detection. The exploited native services are called Living-Off-the-Land Binaries (LOLBins). The malicious code is loaded directly into RAM. Advantages include: not requiring a specific file type, automatic launch, and inability to be detected by anti-malware tools.
  • Worm: A malicious program that uses a computer network to replicate (sometimes called a "network virus"). It enters a computer via the network, exploits a vulnerability in an application or OS, and immediately searches for another computer on the network with the same vulnerability.
  • Bloatware: Software installed on a device without the user requesting it. It may contain malware or become an exploitable platform due to vulnerabilities.
  • Bot/Botnet: Software that allows an infected computer to be placed under the remote control of an attacker to launch attacks. The infected computer is a bot or zombie. When thousands of bots are gathered into a logical network, they create a botnet under the control of a bot herder.

E. Sidestep

Malware that helps evade detection.

  • Logic Bomb: Computer code added to a legitimate program that lies dormant and evades detection until a specific logical event triggers it.
  • Rootkit: Malware that can hide its presence and the presence of other malware by accessing lower layers of the OS or using undocumented functions to make alterations.
  • Backdoor: Provides access to a computer, program, or service that circumvents normal security protections.

2. Indicators of Attack (IoAs)

An Indicator of Attack (IoA) is a sign that an attack is currently underway. It differs from an Indicator of Compromise (IoC), which is evidence that an attack has already occurred.

Indicator Description
Account Lockout An inaccessible account indicates an attacker may have taken it over, locking out the legitimate user.
Concurrent Session Usage Legitimate user and attacker logged into the same account.
Blocked Content Data that is no longer accessible (can be an IoA).
Impossible Travel Accessing a resource from geographically impossible locations in a short time frame.
Resource Consumption System resources (memory, processing) suddenly depleted.
Resource Inaccessibility A large-scale attack can block access to system resources.
Out-of-cycle Logging Log records that do not correspond to actual events.
Published/Documented Evidence from external sources of a current attack.
Missing Logs Log files mysteriously deleted, indicating an ongoing attack.

Application Vulnerabilities and Attacks

This category of attacks targets software already installed, seeking vulnerabilities or manipulating it for compromise.

1. Application Vulnerabilities

  • Buffer Overflow Attack: Occurs when a process attempts to store data in RAM beyond the boundaries of a fixed-length storage buffer. The extra data overflows into adjacent memory locations, allowing an attacker to substitute the "return address" with an address pointing to their malware code. These attacks are called injections or memory injections.
  • Improper Exception and Error Handling: Based on poor programming practices where the software does not correctly check for exceptions. If the software fails to "trap" an error condition (e.g., after a very long input string), the program may crash and display an underlying OS prompt, giving the attacker system access.
  • NULL Pointer/Object Dereference: Occurs when an application attempts to dereference a pointer expected to be valid but which has a NULL value, leading to a crash.
  • Race Condition: Occurs when two concurrent threads of execution access a shared resource simultaneously, resulting in unintended consequences.
    • TOC/TOU (Time of Check to Time of Use): The race condition where a resource's state can change between the moment of checking and the moment of use, invalidating the check's result.

2. Web-Based Attacks

Application attacks directed at programs running on Internet web servers. The typical web infrastructure involves the client browser, web server, application servers, and database servers.

  • Directory Traversal: Takes advantage of a vulnerability so that a user can move from the restricted web server root directory to other restricted directories, viewing confidential files or executing commands.
  • Scripting Attacks:
    • Cross-Site Scripting (XSS): Exploits a website that accepts user input without validating it (sanitizing) and uses that input in a response. An attacker tricks a valid website into feeding a malicious script to another user's web browser, which then executes it.
  • Injection Attacks:
    • SQL Injection (SQLi): Inserts commands in Structured Query Language (SQL) to manipulate a database server. The attacker injects SQL statements (such as whatever' OR 'a'='a) as user input into a form (e.g., a username field) so that the database query is always true, allowing data extraction or manipulation.
  • Request Forgery:
    • Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF): Takes advantage of an authentication "token" a website sends to a user's web browser. If the authenticated user is tricked into loading another webpage, the new page inherits the victim's identity and privileges, allowing the attacker to perform an undesired function on the user's behalf.
    • Server-Site Request Forgery (SSRF): Exploits a trusting relationship between web servers.
  • Replay Attack: Copies data and then uses it later for an attack.

Endpoint Defenses and Hardening

1. Active Endpoint Protection

Antivirus (AV)

AV software can examine a computer for infections and monitor activity.

  • Signature-Based Monitoring (Static Analysis): The original approach, which compares known virus patterns against potentially infected files (string scanning, wildcard scanning, and mismatch scanning). The weakness is that the signature database must be constantly updated.
  • Heuristic Monitoring (Dynamic Analysis): A newer approach that uses a variety of techniques to spot the characteristics of a virus instead of looking for exact matches.

Web Browser Protections

  • Secure Cookies: A cookie is only sent to the server with an encrypted request (HTTPS protocol). This prevents an unauthorized person from intercepting the cookie during transmission. Cookies (which store user-specific information on the local computer) are used to make the stateless HTTP protocol mimic a stateful protocol.
  • HTTP Response Headers: Tell the browser how to behave when communicating with the website.
Header Description Protection
HTTP Strict Transport Security (HSTS) Forces the browser to communicate over HTTPS instead of HTTP. Encrypts transmissions to prevent interception.
Content Security Policy (CSP) Restricts the resources a user is allowed to load within the website. Protects against injection attacks.
Cross Site Scripting Protection (X-XSS) Prohibits a page from loading if it detects an XSS attack. Prevents XSS attacks.
X-Frame-Options Prevents attackers from "overlaying" their content on the webpage. Foils a threat actor's attempt to trick a user.

Monitoring and Response Systems

  • Host Intrusion Detection System (HIDS): Software running on the endpoint that detects when an attack has occurred (automated detection). Monitors system calls, file system access, and host input/output.
  • Host Intrusion Prevention System (HIPS): Not only monitors but also attempts to immediately block malicious attacks by following specific rules.
  • Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR): More robust than HIDS and HIPS. It aggregates data from multiple endpoints to a centralized database (allowing investigation of wider attacks) and performs sophisticated analytics to detect anomalies by comparing behavior against a baseline.

2. Endpoint Hardening

Hardening involves patch management and OS protections.

  • Patch Management: Installing software security updates (patching) is one of the most important steps. Attackers often craft attacks around vulnerabilities fixed by patches, knowing that many users are slow to apply them.
    • Patch Distribution: Automated patch management tools allow organizations to test patches before installation, save bandwidth by downloading from a local server, and approve/decline updates.
    • Patch Reception: Modern OSs (like Windows 11) are less lenient, forcing or delaying updates for a limited time (one to five weeks). Prompt installation of patches is advised.
  • Operating System (OS) Protections:
    • Disabling Unnecessary Ports and Protocols: A primary security configuration that closes unused ports and disables unnecessary services.
    • Application Allow Listing: Approves in advance only specific applications to run on the OS, ensuring that any unapproved item will not function. It is a powerful tool to combat attacks. Windows Defender offers an allow listing protection called "controlled folder access" against ransomware.
    • Sandbox: A "container" in which an application can be run so that it does not impact the underlying OS. Anything that occurs within the sandbox is not visible to other applications or the OS, and the contents are not saved upon closing.

Application Security

Application security is critical because insecure software can negate strict protections imposed on devices. Proactively preventing vulnerabilities begins during the application development and coding process.

1. Application Development Concepts

Application development requires several stages:

  1. Development: Establishing requirements and confirming that the application meets business needs before coding begins.
  2. Testing: Thoroughly testing the application for errors that could result in a security vulnerability.
  3. Staging: Testing to verify that the code functions as intended.
  4. Production: The application is released for use in its actual setting.

Application Development Lifecycle Models

  • Waterfall Model: Uses a sequential and rigid design process. Once a stage is finished, developers cannot go back without starting over. Quality assurance (QA) occurs only at the end, making discovered issues difficult to address.
  • Agile Model: Takes an incremental approach to overcome the waterfall model's disadvantages. Work is done in short "sprints" (weekly or monthly), allowing software issues to be discovered incrementally, and feedback and changes to be incorporated before the next sprint.

SecDevOps (DevSecOps)

A specific software methodology that follows the Agile model and heavily incorporates secure coding practices and techniques. It integrates secure development best practices into application development and deployment processes.

  • Key Characteristics: Elasticity (flexibility), scalability, and, most importantly, automation.
  • Automation: Enables automated courses of action to develop code as quickly and securely as possible. This includes:
    • Continuous Monitoring (real-time examination).
    • Continuous Validation (ongoing approvals of the code).
    • Continuous Integration (ensuring security features are incorporated at each stage).
    • Continuous Delivery and Continuous Deployment.
  • Other Concepts: Immutable Systems (once a value is employed, it is not modified; a new system must be created for changes) and Infrastructure as Code (managing hardware/software infrastructure using coding principles).

2. Secure Coding Techniques

Coding techniques aim to create secure applications and limit data exposure. Examples include:

Technique Purpose
Input Validation Checks user input to ensure only expected data is accepted, preventing injection attacks.
Error and Exception Handling Robust implementation to prevent programming errors from granting access to the underlying system.
Obfuscation Making code difficult to understand to hinder reverse engineering.
Normalization Modifying code to a format the application can understand.
Code Signing Digital verification of code integrity.

3. Code Testing

Testing should be performed earlier, during the implementation and verification phases.

  • Static Code Analysis: Occurs before the source code is compiled. It examines the software from a security perspective. Can be supplemented by manual peer reviews.
  • Dynamic Code Analysis / Runtime Verification: Performed after the source code is compiled, when all components are integrated and running. Uses tools that monitor application behavior for memory corruption and privilege issues.
  • Fuzzing: A common process in dynamic analysis that provides random input to a program in an attempt to trigger exceptions, such as memory corruption or crashes. The advantage is that it records the input that triggered the exception, allowing the problem to be tracked down.

Study Guide for Module 6

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

1. Why can't standard cryptography be used on tiny devices?

Standard cryptography requires significant processing power and storage capacity, which tiny devices (like sensors and medical implants) lack due to their small size and limited power sources (often small batteries).

2. What is the NIST standard for lightweight cryptography selected in 2023?

Ascon.

3. What are the two primary tasks of the Ascon family of technologies?

Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data (AEAD) and Hashing.

4. What is the difference between a notebook and a convertible (2-in-1) computer?

A notebook is a smaller, lightweight version of a laptop, while a convertible (2-in-1) has a flexible hinge that allows it to take on different poses (laptop, tablet, stand, tent).

5. Which mobile connectivity method uses a network of hexagonal cells?

Cellular.

6. Explain the "Corporate Owned, Personally Enabled" (COPE) deployment model.

In the COPE model, the organization purchases and owns the device, but the employee is allowed to use it for personal activities as well.

7. What is "sideloading" in the context of mobile apps?

Sideloading is the practice of downloading and installing apps from an unofficial and unapproved third-party source, bypassing the official app store.

8. What is the difference between MDM and MAM?

Mobile Device Management (MDM) focuses on managing the device itself (updates, configuration, remote wipe), while Mobile Application Management (MAM) focuses on distributing and controlling access to specific applications on the device.

9. What is an "embedded system"?

A computer system integrated into a larger system to perform specific functions, often without direct human input (e.g., controllers in a car engine, industrial control systems).

10. What is a "Fileless Virus"?

A type of malware that does not attach itself to a file but instead loads its malicious code directly into RAM, exploiting native OS services (LOLBins) to avoid detection by file-based scanners.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Ascon The NIST-selected standard for lightweight cryptography, designed for resource-constrained devices.
AEAD (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data) A cryptographic process that provides both confidentiality (encryption) and authenticity, allowing unencrypted data (associated data) to be attached to the message.
BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) An enterprise policy allowing employees to use their personal devices for work purposes.
Containerization A technology that isolates business apps and data from personal content on a mobile device.
Embedded System A computer system with a dedicated function within a larger mechanical or electrical system.
Geofencing Using GPS or RFID technology to create a virtual geographic boundary, enabling software to trigger a response when a mobile device enters or leaves a particular area.
Jailbreaking/Rooting The process of removing software restrictions imposed by the manufacturer on iOS (jailbreaking) or Android (rooting) devices to gain full administrative access.
Malware Software intentionally designed to cause damage to a computer, server, client, or computer network.
Ransomware A type of malware that threatens to publish the victim's data or perpetually block access to it unless a ransom is paid.
SecDevOps A software development philosophy that integrates security practices into the DevOps process (development and operations).
Side-loading Installing an application package on a mobile device from a source other than the official app store.
SoC (System on a Chip) An integrated circuit that integrates all or most components of a computer or other electronic system.
Zero-Day Vulnerability A software vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor and for which no patch exists.
