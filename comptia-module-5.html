Module 5: Endpoint Vulnerabilities, Attacks, and Defenses

Comprehensive guide to endpoint security, malware types, and defensive strategies.

I. Introduction and Endpoint Context

The Modern Concept of an Endpoint

The term endpoint is the currently accepted word used to refer to network-connected hardware devices. This shift reflects the evolution of technology, as computing devices today are far more than just traditional desktop computers.

Historical Terminology for Network-Connected Devices Approximate Period of Use
Host Forty years ago (with the rise of the TCP/IP protocol)
Client Thirty years ago (as servers became popular and clients made requests)
Workstation Twenty years ago (specialized computers for scientific or highly technical applications)
Endpoint Today (including smartphones, tablets, fitness trackers, ICS sensors, automotive telematics, and drones)

The proliferation of endpoints has led to an exponential increase in risks, making them a prime target for attackers. A vulnerability on a single endpoint can result in an attack that penetrates the network and infects all other connected devices.

The Ethical Dilemma: Bug Bounty vs. Attack and Cover-up

A high-profile incident has raised serious concerns among security professionals regarding the definition of an attack and how victims should react.

The case involved Joseph S., a former chief security officer at Uber, following an incident where threat actors breached Uber's security and downloaded about 57 million records containing private data.

The Attackers' Claim:

The attackers claimed they had discovered a vulnerability on the company's website and demanded "high compensation" for finding the flaw. They defined themselves as "white hat hackers"—ethical attackers who probe a system for weaknesses and privately provide information back to the organization about uncovered vulnerabilities. In contrast, "black hat hackers" violate computer security for personal gain or to inflict malicious damage.

Uber's Decision:

After much deliberation, an Uber attorney advised the team to treat the matter as a request for a bug bounty. A bug bounty is a common practice where large corporations pay security researchers who uncover and privately report bugs so they can be patched. Uber required the threat actors to enroll in the bug bounty program, paid $100,000 in Bitcoin, and required them to sign both a nondisclosure agreement (NDA) and a confidentiality agreement, attesting they had destroyed all stolen data.

The Legal Consequences:

Federal prosecutors, who were investigating Uber for a different incident, discovered what occurred and charged Joseph S. with criminal obstruction, alleging he orchestrated a cover-up to avoid required disclosures to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and various states (which all have mandatory breach notification laws). Prosecutors characterized the case as "cover-up, about payoff, and about lies". Joseph S. was later convicted of obstruction of justice in late 2022 and is appealing his case.

Many security professionals viewed this as a test case for potential criminal penalties they might face, arguing that decisions on responding to cyber incidents are complex and usually made by consensus with senior leadership, not unilaterally by the CSO.

II. Malware Attacks

Malware (malicious software) is a general term describing software designed to interfere with a computer's normal functions and commit unwanted, harmful actions. Malware types can be classified by examining the primary action they perform.

Malware Actions for Classification

The sources group malware actions into five categories: kidnap, eavesdrop, masquerade, launch, and sidestep.

1. Kidnap - Ransomware

Kidnapping, metaphorically, involves capturing a user's device and holding it "hostage" until a ransom is paid. Ransomware is malicious software designed to extort money from victims in exchange for restoring their endpoint device to its normal working state.

There are two general categories of ransomware:

Ransomware Category Description and Action
Blocking Ransomware (Legacy) The earliest form (widespread around 2010) that prevents or blocks the user from using their computer normally by manipulating the OS. Typically displays a message purporting to be from a reputable third party (like law enforcement) demanding a fine.
Locking Ransomware (Modern) A more malicious form that encrypts some or all files on the device so they cannot be opened. The device remains functional to facilitate easier ransom payment.

Locking ransomware has evolved to encrypt files not just on the local drive, but also on any network or attached storage device connected to that endpoint, including USB drives, network servers, and cloud-based data repositories.

Extortion and Blended Ransomware Attacks: Attackers now use blended attacks: they first steal all accessible data before locking the files. If the victim refuses to pay, threat actors threaten to publicly release the stolen information. Recently, attackers have added extortion, threatening to sell the data to the highest bidder unless a large ransom is paid.

Ransomware: The Most Serious Threat:

Ransomware is considered the most serious malware threat for several reasons:

  • Low barrier to entry: Launching an attack is relatively inexpensive (software rental is around $800/month) and does not require a high degree of skill.
  • Pervasive attacks: Occur frequently (two out of every three organizations experienced a successful attack in 2021).
  • High impact: Results are significant, forcing companies to lay off employees (37%), see senior-level managers resign (35%), or temporarily suspend business operations (33%).

FBI Stance on Paying Ransom: The FBI originally did not support paying ransoms, arguing it encourages criminals, but later softened its stance, understanding that executives must evaluate all options when faced with an inability to function.

2. Eavesdrop - Keyloggers and Spyware

This category of malware secretly listens to its targets.

Malware Type Description
Keylogger Silently captures and stores every keystroke a user types, allowing threat actors to search the captured text for sensitive information like passwords or credit card numbers.
Spyware Tracking software deployed without user consent or control. It secretly monitors users, collecting and distributing personal or sensitive information by utilizing the computer's resources and programs already installed.

Types of Keyloggers:

  1. Software Keyloggers: Programs installed remotely that capture sensitive information. They can also capture screen images and silently activate the computer’s web camera. Advantage: Does not require physical access; sends captured data back through the victim's internet connection.
  2. Hardware Keyloggers: Physical devices inserted between the keyboard connection and the USB port. They can easily go undetected as they resemble ordinary connectors and are beyond the reach of anti-malware scanning software. Disadvantage: The attacker must install and physically return later to remove the device and access stored information.

Technologies Used by Spyware:

Technology Description Impact
Automatic download software Used to download and install software without the user’s interaction Could install unauthorized applications
Passive tracking technologies Used to gather information about user activities without installing any software Could collect private information such as websites a user has visited
System-modifying software Modifies or changes user configurations (web browser home page, default media player, system functions) Changes configurations to settings that the user did not approve
Tracking software Used to monitor user behavior or gather sensitive information, sometimes including personally identifiable data Could collect personal information that can be widely shared or stolen, resulting in fraud or identity theft

3. Masquerade - Trojans

Malware in this category attempts to deceive the user and hide its true intentions by pretending to be something else.

  • Trojan (Trojan Horse): An executable program that masquerades as performing a benign activity but also does something malicious. Example: A downloaded calendar program that secretly installs malware to scan for credit card numbers.
  • Remote Access Trojan (RAT): A special type of Trojan that uses specially configured communication protocols to give the threat agent unauthorized, unrestricted remote access to the victim’s computer. This allows the attacker to monitor, change settings, copy files, and use the computer to access other networked devices.

4. Launch - Virus, Worm, Bloatware, Bot

This category includes malware that infects a computer specifically to launch attacks on other computers.

Malware Type Description Spread and Action
File-Based Virus Malicious computer code that becomes part of a file, similar to a biological virus. Reproduces itself only on the host computer. It must rely on user actions (such as transferring the infected file via email or USB) to spread to other devices. Infection methods include appender infection or evasive techniques like split infections or mutations.
Fileless Virus Does not attach to a file; instead, it uses native OS services and processes called Living-Off-the-Land Binaries (LOLBins) to avoid detection. The malicious code is loaded directly into the computer’s RAM (Random Access Memory). Advantages: does not require a specific file type, can launch automatically, and cannot be detected by anti-malware tools.
Worm Malicious program that uses a computer network to replicate (sometimes called a "network virus"). Designed to enter a computer through the network, exploit an OS or application vulnerability, and immediately search for other vulnerable computers. Today’s worms can carry payloads to delete files or allow remote control by an attacker.
Bloatware Software installed on a device without being requested by the user. Risks: may contain malware or may harbor a vulnerability that other malware can exploit. Can be preinstalled on new devices or installed unintentionally by overlooking default setup options (e.g., unwanted toolbars).
Bot (or Zombie) An infected robot computer placed under the remote control of an attacker for launching attacks. Hundreds or millions of bots form a botnet, controlled by a bot herder. Bots receive instructions through a command and control (C&C) structure, often via bot-herding websites, coded posts on Twitter, or social media notes.

Windows File Types That Can Be Infected:

File extension Description

5. Sidestep - Evasion of Detection

This category attempts to help malware sidestep or evade detection.

  • Logic Bomb: Computer code added to a legitimate program that lies dormant until a specific logical event triggers it. Once started, it deletes data or performs other malicious activities. They are difficult to detect before triggering because they are often embedded in large programs.
  • Rootkit: Malware that hides its presence (and the presence of other malware) by accessing lower layers of the OS or using undocumented functions to make alterations. This renders it undetectable by the OS or anti-malware scanning software.
  • Backdoor: Provides access to a computer, program, or service by circumventing normal security protections. Backdoors allow an attacker to return later and bypass security settings. They are sometimes left installed unintentionally by developers.

III. Application Vulnerabilities and Attacks

Application attacks target vulnerabilities in installed software, frequently leading to privilege escalation. Escalation can be horizontal (gaining rights of an account with similar privileges) or vertical (increasing elevation of rights).

Common Application Vulnerabilities

  1. Buffer Overflow Attack: Occurs when a process attempts to store data in RAM exceeding the boundaries of a fixed-length storage buffer. The excess data overflows into adjacent memory locations. The attacker can substitute the program's "return address" in memory with an address pointing to their malicious code. These attacks are known as injections or memory injections.
  2. Improper Exception and Error Handling: A vulnerability based on poor programming practices where an application fails to properly check for exceptions and errors during runtime. If software fails to "trap" an error condition (e.g., receiving an excessively long input string), it may crash and display an underlying OS prompt, giving the attacker system access.
    • NULL Pointer/Object Dereference: A type of improper handling where the application dereferences a pointer (obtains the address of a data item) that is expected to be valid but holds a NULL value, typically causing a program crash or exit.
  3. Race Condition: Occurs when two concurrent threads of execution access a shared resource simultaneously, leading to unintended consequences. A key example is the Time of Check (TOC) to Time of Use (TOU) race condition: the resource's state changes between the time the software checks it and the time it uses it, invalidating the check's result.

Web-Based Attacks

Web-based attacks are a common type of application attack directed at programs running on Internet web servers.

Web Application Infrastructure: The typical infrastructure involves a client (web browser) making an HTTP request to a Web Server, which connects to App Servers that, in turn, connect to Database Servers.

  1. Directory Traversal: Exploits a vulnerability to allow a user to move from the restricted "root" directory on the web server's file system to other restricted directories, enabling the viewing of confidential files or execution of commands.
  2. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS): Exploits a website that accepts user input without sanitizing (validating) it and uses that input in a response. An attacker tricks a valid website into feeding a malicious script to another user's web browser, which then executes it. XSS refers to an attack originating on one site (the web server) to impact another (the user's computer).
  3. SQL Injection (SQLi): One of the most common injection attacks, inserting Structured Query Language (SQL) statements to manipulate a database server. If user input is sent unfiltered to the database, malicious commands can be processed. For example, entering whatever’ or ‘a’=‘a can make the WHERE clause of a SQL query always true, resulting in the display of all user email addresses.

SQLi Statements and Potential Results:

SQL injection statement Result
whatever’ AND email IS NULL; -- Determine the names of different fields in the database
whatever’ AND 1=(SELECT COUNT(*) FROM tabname); — Discover the name of the table
whatever’ OR full name LIKE ‘%Mia%’ Find specific users
whatever’; DROP TABLE members; -- Erase the database table
whatever’; UPDATE members SET email = ‘attacker-email@evil.net’ WHERE email = ‘Mia@good.com’; Mail password to attacker’s email account
  1. Request Forgery: A request that has been fabricated (a forgery).
    • Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF): Takes advantage of an authentication "token" sent to the user's browser. If a user is authenticated and tricked into loading another webpage, the new page inherits the victim's identity and privileges, enabling the attacker to perform an undesired function on the victim's behalf.
    • Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF): Exploits a trusting relationship between web servers (as opposed to client-to-server trust). By modifying a target URL, an attacker can potentially extract sensitive information or inject untrusted input.

CSRF and SSRF Differences:

Attack name Attack target Purpose of attack
CSRF User Force target to take action for attacker while pretending to be authorized user
SSRF Web server Manipulate information or inject harmful data
  1. Replay Attack: Copies data (often digital identities) sent between two parties and later retransmits selected and edited portions to impersonate the legitimate user. Many such attacks occur between a user and an authentication server.

IV. Defending and Hardening Endpoint Devices

Defenses are split into actively protecting endpoints from attacks and hardening them for greater security.

A. Protecting Endpoints (Software and Monitoring)

Active protection is achieved through installed software, including antivirus, web browser protections, and monitoring systems.

  1. Antivirus (AV): Examines the computer for file-based virus infections, monitors activity, and scans new documents. If a virus is detected, options include cleaning, quarantining, or deleting the file.
    • Signature-based monitoring (Static analysis): The original method that scans files by matching known virus patterns (signatures). Weakness: Requires constant updates of the signature database.
    • Heuristic monitoring (Dynamic analysis): A newer approach that uses techniques to spot the characteristics of a virus instead of exact pattern matches. This can involve code emulation in a virtual environment to safely execute questionable program code.
  2. Web Browser Protections:
    • Secure Cookies: HTTP is a stateless protocol. A secure cookie is a protection mechanism that is only sent to the server with an encrypted request over the HTTPS protocol, preventing interception.
    • HTTP Response Headers: Instructions sent by the web server that tell the browser how to behave to enhance security.

HTTP Response Headers:

HTTP Response Header Description Protection
HTTP Strict Transport Security (HSTS) Forces browser to communicate over more secure HTTPS instead of HTTP Encrypts transmissions to prevent unauthorized user from intercepting
Content Security Policy (CSP) Restricts the resources a user is allowed to load within the website Protects against injection attacks
Cross Site Scripting Protection (X-XSS) Prohibits a page from loading if it detects a cross-site scripting attack Prevents XSS attacks
X-Frame-Options Prevents attackers from “overlaying” their content on the webpage Foils a threat actor’s attempt to trick a user into providing personal information
  1. Monitoring and Response Systems:
    • Host Intrusion Detection Systems (HIDS): Software application running on the endpoint that detects an attack has occurred (automated detection) by monitoring system calls, file system access, and host input/output.
    • Host Intrusion Prevention Systems (HIPS): Monitors endpoint activity and attempts to immediately block a malicious attack by following specific rules. Watches for events attempting to control or terminate programs, or install devices/drivers.
    • Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR): Considered more robust than HIDS and HIPS. It aggregates data from multiple endpoints to a centralized database for investigation. EDR tools perform sophisticated analytics to identify patterns and detect anomalies through baseline comparisons of normal behavior.

B. Hardening Endpoints (OS Level Protections)

Hardening involves patch management and operating system protections.

  1. Patch Management: Installing software security updates (patching) is the single most important step in securing a device. Patches address vulnerabilities uncovered after software release.
    • Patch Distribution: Organizations often use automated patch management tools (local servers) to test and manage patch distribution internally, rather than relying solely on the vendor's online update service. This saves bandwidth and allows administrators to approve, decline, or force updates.
    • Patch Reception: Modern OSs (e.g., Windows 11) limit user options to reject patches, though updates can be delayed for one to five weeks before they resume.
  2. Operating System Protections:
    • Disabling Unnecessary Ports and Protocols: A primary OS security configuration involves closing unused ports and disabling unnecessary protocols and services (like Windows ASP.NET State Service).
    • Application Allow Listing: Approving only specific applications to run on the OS in advance; anything not approved will not function. This is a powerful anti-attack tool. Windows Defender uses this technique for ransomware protection via "controlled folder access".
    • Sandbox: A "container" where an application runs without impacting the underlying OS. Anything occurring within the sandbox is not visible to the OS outside, and the contents are not saved when closed. Used for safely running suspicious programs.

V. Indicators of Attack (IoAs)

Signs of an attack are categorized as follows:

  • Indicator of Attack (IoA): A sign an attack is currently underway.
  • Indicator of Compromise (IoC): Evidence that an attack has already taken place.

Indicators of Attack (IoAs):

Indicator Description
Account lockout A user account is inaccessible through a normal login attempt, possibly indicating takeover by an attacker who is locking out the legitimate user
Concurrent session usage Both a legitimate user and an attacker are logged into the same account simultaneously
Blocked content Data that is no longer accessible can be an IoA
Impossible travel Accessing a resource that is geographically impossible (e.g., checking email in New York and downloading a file in Los Angeles five minutes later)
Resource consumption System resources (memory or processing capabilities) are suddenly depleted
Resource inaccessibility A large-scale attack can block access to system resources
Out-of-cycle logging Log records that do not correspond to actual events that have occurred
Published/documented Evidence from external sources indicating a current attack
Missing logs Log files that have mysteriously been deleted, indicating an ongoing attack

VI. Overview of Hands-On Projects and Case Studies

The sources detail several practical activities and research topics focused on endpoint security.

Activity Type Objective and Research/Practice Topics
Hands-On Projects 1. Microsoft Safety Scanner: Download and run the free tool to scan a computer for malware.
2. Windows Security Configuration: Explore Windows 11 security settings, including the setup of Controlled folder access for ransomware protection.
3. ConfigureDefender: Use a third-party tool to fine-tune Microsoft Defender settings.
4. VirusTotal Analysis: Analyze files and URLs for potential malware using the combined results of 70 AV scanners and blacklisting services.
5. Software Keylogger: Explore the features of a keylogger to understand how it captures data (keystrokes, screenshots, visited websites).
6. Ransomware Sites: Research decryption tools and prevention advice on sites like nomoreransom.org.
Case Projects 1. Trending Cyber/Joseph S.: Analyze the ethical and legal implications of the Uber incident (cover-up vs. bug bounty).
2. Biological and File-Based Viruses: Research similarities and methods for combating both types of viruses.
3. LOLBins (Living-Off-the-Land Binaries): Research fileless viruses, comparing them to file-based viruses and detailing defense mechanisms.
4. Infamous Logic Bombs: Research historical logic bombs, identifying responsible parties, damage caused, and prevention methods.
5. Indicators of Compromise (IoCs): Research and identify IoCs (evidence of a post-attack scenario).
6. External Software Components: Research attacks targeting Application Program Interfaces (APIs) and device drivers (e.g., shimming and refactoring).
7. Paying Ransom: Analyze the pros and cons of paying a ransom (e.g., speed of recovery, regulatory fines, funding future attacks).
8. Stopping Ransomware: Research proposals, such as regulating cryptocurrency exchanges, to combat ransomware crime.
9. Application Attacks Presentation: Prepare a comparison presentation on XSS, SQLi, CSRF, and SSRF, and report on software race conditions.

Study Guide for Module 5

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

1. What is the term currently used to describe network-connected hardware devices?

Endpoint.

2. What are the five categories of malware actions?

Kidnap, eavesdrop, masquerade, launch, and sidestep.

3. What is the difference between a virus and a worm?

A virus requires a host file and user action to spread, whereas a worm is a standalone program that uses the network to replicate and spread automatically.

4. What is a logic bomb?

Malicious code added to a legitimate program that lies dormant until a specific logical event triggers it.

5. What is the difference between horizontal and vertical privilege escalation?

Horizontal escalation involves gaining access to an account with similar privileges, while vertical escalation involves gaining higher-level privileges.

6. What is Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)?

An attack where malicious scripts are injected into trusted websites and executed by the victim's browser.

7. What is the purpose of a bug bounty program?

To incentivize security researchers to find and report vulnerabilities so they can be patched before attackers exploit them.

8. What is a rootkit?

Malware that hides its presence by accessing lower layers of the OS or using undocumented functions.

9. What is the difference between an Indicator of Attack (IoA) and an Indicator of Compromise (IoC)?

An IoA indicates an attack is currently underway, while an IoC indicates an attack has already occurred.

10. What is application allow listing?

A security practice where only approved applications are allowed to run on a system.

Glossary

Term Definition
