Pervasive Attack Surfaces and Controls: A Didactic Overview

The concepts of social engineering, physical security, and data controls are considered pervasive in information technology (IT) security, meaning they exist in or spread through every part of the domain. Unlike some IT topics linked narrowly to networking or mobile devices, these elements are universally important for securing information, running the full gamut across the field. Successfully defeating attempts to trick users, ensuring strong physical protection for assets, and implementing necessary controls for data confidentiality, integrity, and availability are fundamental requirements.

I. Social Engineering Attacks

Social engineering is one of the most effective types of attacks, relying on tricking a user into taking an action or providing information that benefits the attacker. This practice of deceiving victims for gain has existed for thousands of years, long before modern cyberattacks.

Social engineering is a means of eliciting information or convincing a user to take action that weakens security, almost always performed through deception and manipulation. Because these attacks exploit people, the attack surface is often described as human vectors. Cybersecurity attacks are frequently successful because they begin with highly successful social engineering, opening the door for subsequent steps. It is estimated that 98 percent of cybercriminals use social engineering in their attacks.

Threat actors, sometimes called "masters of the art of deception," rely heavily on human psychology—a person's conscious and unconscious feelings and thoughts—to persuade targets without them realizing they are being taken advantage of.

A. Principles of Human Manipulation

Threat actors create believable, often false scenarios specific to the target to obtain information or gain leverage to breach a system. The following principles of psychology are commonly exploited in social engineering attacks:

Principle Description Example (Attacker pretending to be CEO calling help desk)
Authority Directed by someone impersonating an authority figure or falsely citing their authority “I’m the CEO calling.”
Intimidation To frighten and coerce by threat “If you don’t reset my password, I will call your supervisor.”
Consensus Influenced by what others do “I called last week, and your colleague reset my password.”
Scarcity Something is in short supply “I can’t waste time here.”
Urgency Immediate action is needed “My meeting with the board starts in 5 minutes.”
Familiarity Victim is well known and well received “I remember reading a good evaluation on you.”
Trust Confidence “You know who I am.”

Another technique is prepending, which influences the subject before the event occurs by starting with the desired outcome. For example, combining prepending with the urgency principle: "You need to reset my password immediately because my meeting with the board starts in 5 minutes".

B. Personal Techniques to Gain Trust

When social engineering involves person-to-person contact, attackers use various personal techniques to gain trust:

  1. Provide a reason: Adding a rationalization, often using the word "because," increases the likelihood of the target providing information.
  2. Project confidence: A threat agent can generate less suspicion by calmly walking through a restricted area as if they know exactly where they are going, often greeting people.
  3. Use evasion and diversion: When challenged, an attacker might evade a question with a vague or irrelevant answer, feign innocence/confusion, or simply deny allegations until the target doubts their own suspicions. Sometimes, they resort to anger to cause the target to drop the challenge.
  4. Make them laugh: Humor is an excellent tool to develop a sense of trust and put people at ease.

C. Types of Social Engineering Attacks

Key types of social engineering attacks include phishing, impersonation, redirection, misinformation and disinformation, watering hole attacks, and data reconnaissance.

Phishing and its Variations

Phishing is one of the most common forms of social engineering, involving sending an email or displaying a web announcement that falsely claims to be from a legitimate source to trick the user into taking action. This term is a variation of "fishing," where bait is thrown out hoping some users will "bite". Phishing emails often request users to update personal data such as passwords or credit card numbers, directing them to an imposter website designed to steal the entered information. Phishing is considered one of the largest and most consequential cyber threats facing consumers and enterprises. Phishing is also used to validate email addresses by displaying an image retrieved from a website, confirming the email address is active and valid.

Variations include:

  • Spear Phishing: Targets specific users (rather than millions generically) with emails customized to the recipient, often including names and personal details to appear legitimate.
  • Whaling: A type of spear phishing that targets "big fish," such as wealthy individuals or senior executives who typically have large sums of money accessible.
  • Vishing (Voice Phishing): Uses telephone calls instead of email. The target often hears a recorded message pretending to be from their bank, instructing them to call an attacker-controlled number to enter sensitive information via the telephone keypad.
  • Smishing: Uses the short message service (SMS) to send fraudulent text messages. This is often combined with callback recorded phone messages asking the user to enter information for "verification".
Business Email Compromise (BEC)

A BEC is a particular type of phishing attack increasing in popularity that exploits the common business practice of electronically transferring funds or making payments. Attackers leverage the size and complexity of large enterprises by requesting funds from what appears to be a legitimate source, counting on the target complying without investigation. BEC attacks increased by 59 percent as of the third quarter of 2022. BECs are not limited to businesses; users can be victims, such as a request from a relative overseas needing money wired immediately.

BEC attack Description
Bogus invoice An attacker, pretending to be a legitimate supplier, sends a fake invoice demanding immediate payment for an overdue account.
Executive fraud A threat actor poses as a company executive, emailing Finance employees to immediately transfer funds for an unpublicized new company initiative, instructing them not to tell anyone.
Account compromise A Finance Department employee’s email account is compromised, and then vendors are sent emails demanding immediate payment for a fictitious service.
Impersonation

Impersonation is masquerading as a real or fictitious character and then acting out that role on a target. Common roles impersonated are managers, IT support, repairpersons, or trusted third parties, often chosen because users resist saying "no" to authority figures. Sometimes the goal of impersonation is specifically to obtain private information, a practice known as pretexting.

  • Brand Impersonation: The threat actor pretends to be a highly recognizable and well-known brand (like a major bank) to build immediate recognition and trust, tricking the target into clicking a link or opening an attachment.
Redirection

When phishing or impersonation fails, threat actors use redirection tactics to send users to fake look-alike sites, often filled with ads that generate revenue for the attacker.

  • Typo Squatting: Attackers purchase and register domain names that are spelled similarly to actual sites (e.g., goggle.com instead of google.com) to catch users who make typing errors when entering a URL (Uniform Resource Locator). Squatters prefer to mimic mainstream search engines/social media sites and financial/banking/online shopping sites.
  • Bitsquatting: A variation where threat actors register domain names that are only one bit different from the actual name (e.g., aeazon.com for amazon.com). This exploits the increased likelihood of a RAM memory error "flipping" a bit in DNS memory.
  • Pharming: Exploits the conversion of a URL (like www.cengage.com) into its corresponding IP address. A threat actor may install malware on the user's computer to perform the redirection, or infect a DNS server, directing large numbers of users to the fake site.
Misinformation and Disinformation
  • Misinformation is false or inaccurate information, regardless of the intent to mislead.
  • Disinformation is false or inaccurate misinformation that comes from a malicious intent and is knowingly false and intentionally spread.

An example of cyber disinformation is a hoax or false warning, often sent via email claiming to be from the IT Department and advising the recipient to erase specific files or change security configurations, which allows an attacker to compromise the system or leads the victim to call the attacker’s phone number for help.

Watering Hole Attack

Similar to animals gathering at a common watering hole, this attack is directed toward a smaller group of specific individuals, such as executives at a manufacturing company who frequently visit a common website (e.g., a parts supplier). The attacker identifies this common site and infects it with malware, which then makes its way onto the targeted group's computers.

Data Reconnaissance

Threat actors use various means to gather valuable information, sometimes without any direct interaction with the target.

  • Dumpster Diving: Digging through trash receptacles to find useful information such as calendars, memos, organizational charts, phone directories, and policy manuals. Purchasing used technology equipment from which sensitive information has not been "scrubbed" is similar to dumpster diving.
  • Google Dorking: Using advanced Google search techniques to find documents and data that unsuspecting victims have carelessly posted online.
  • Shoulder Surfing: Casually observing an individual entering secret information, such as an access code on a keypad. Attackers may use hidden webcams and smartphone cameras for this.

II. Physical Security Controls

Physical security controls are categorized as one of the four broad types of controls (along with managerial, operational, and technical) and are often the most overlooked. Preventing physical access to assets, such as a web server, is as important as preventing remote access. Physical security controls include perimeter defenses, preventing data leakage, and computer hardware security.

A. Perimeter Defenses

Perimeter defenses are used to restrict access and include barriers, security guards, sensors, security buffers, and locks.

Barriers

Passive barriers restrict unauthorized vehicles or individuals.

  • Fencing: A tall, permanent structure usually accompanied by signage and proper lighting. Standard chain-link fencing offers limited security as it can be easily cut or climbed.
  • Barricades: Designed mainly for directing large crowds and are typically easier to circumvent than fences.
  • Bollards: Short but sturdy vertical posts used as vehicular traffic barricades to prevent a car from "ramming" into a secure area.

Modern fencing often includes deterrents, such as those listed in the table below:

Technology Description Comments
Anticlimb paint Nontoxic petroleum gel-based paint that is thickly applied and does not harden, making climbing difficult. Typically used on poles, downpipes, wall tops, and railings above head height (8 feet or 2.4 meters).
Anticlimb collar Spiked collar extending horizontally up to 3 feet (1 meter) from the pole. Serves as both a practical and visual deterrent; used for protecting equipment mounted on poles or areas where climbing provides easy access over a fence.
Roller barrier Independently rotating large cups affixed to the top of a fence. Prevents intruders' hands from gripping the top to climb; often found around public grounds and schools where a nonaggressive barrier is important.
Rotating spikes Installed at the top of walls, gates, or fences; tri-wing spike collars rotate around a central spindle. Designed for high-security areas; can be painted to blend into fencing.

Security Guards

Human guards are an active security defense because they patrol, monitor, and can make split-second decisions, differentiating between an intruder and an innocent person.

  • Two-person integrity/control: Requiring multiple security guards for higher protection levels prevents one compromised guard (via bribery or coercion) from facilitating an attack.
  • Video Surveillance: Guards often monitor activity captured by cameras, transmitting signals to receivers (CCTV). High-end cameras can send alerts and start recording upon detecting movement or suspicious objects (e.g., a backpack). Drones (Unmanned Aerial Vehicles or UAVs) are increasingly used for monitoring.

Sensors

Sensors detect or measure a physical property and respond to it, supplementing security guards by generating alarms for unexpected actions.

  • Infrared (IR) Sensors: Detect IR radiation.
    • Active IR sensors both emit and detect IR (using an LED and receiver), acting as proximity sensors to determine how close an object is.
    • Passive IR sensors only detect IR radiation emitted as heat by living beings, making them ideal for motion-based detection.
  • Microwave Sensors: Use high-frequency radio waves, functioning like radar to detect changes in reflected waves. They are effective for monitoring large areas such as warehouses. They can also sense daylight levels to dim interior lights for energy savings.
  • Ultrasonic Sensors: Use ultrasound waves to measure the distance to a target object by timing the wave return. They are proximity sensors less susceptible to interference from smoke, gas, or airborne particles compared to IR sensors. In physical security, they can allow a person to be present but sound an alarm if they move too close to a door. The distance calculation uses the speed of sound.
  • Pressure Sensors: Detect if a person has entered a restricted area. Modern versions can differentiate between targets (pedestrian, car, truck) and determine the direction of travel. Underground pressure sensors use a controller box and buried plastic tubes.

Security Buffers

Buffers provide an additional layer of security between nonsecure and secure areas.

  • High Security: Access Control Vestibule (Mantrap): An automated buffer controlling two interlocking doors; only one door can be open at any time. Used in high-security areas like research labs or cash handling. Historically, a security officer checked credentials inside the small room (vestibule).
  • Medium Security: Reception Area: Allows visitors to enter an area where a receptionist checks credentials before they pass through an inner door. Precautions include ensuring the receptionist focuses solely on observing visitors, anchoring furnishings, and having the ability to electrically lock out suspicious persons.
  • Low Security: Waiting Room: A generic room that serves as a buffer, typically controlling traffic flow with a check-in window.

Locks

Locks restrict physical access. Key-based locks increase risk because keys can be lost, stolen, or duplicated.

  • Electronic Locks: A more secure option that uses buttons pressed in a proper sequence. They can be programmed to be valid only at specific times and maintain a record (log) of when the door was opened and by which code.
    • A problem with traditional electronic keypads is susceptibility to shoulder surfing or detecting fingerprint "smudges". Mitigation includes using a virtual screen with four circles where digits are randomly assigned and rotated, preventing a shoulder surfer from pressing the same circles repeatedly.
  • Smart Locks: Growing in popularity, these use a smartphone to send a code via wireless Bluetooth to open the door.

B. Preventing Data Leakage

Physical security also includes controls designed to prevent data from escaping.

  • Faraday Cage: Digital electronic devices emit electromagnetic fields, which can lead to Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) or allow unauthorized persons to detect and read the signals. A Faraday cage is a metallic enclosure that prevents the entry or escape of an electromagnetic field. Lightweight Faraday bags are used to shield portable devices, commonly used in crime scene investigations to prevent remote wiping of evidence.
  • Protected Distribution System (PDS): A system of cable conduits (hollow tubes carrying copper or fiber-optic cables) used to protect classified information transmitted between two secure areas. This is a standard created by the U.S. Department of Defense (DoD).
    • Hardened carrier PDS: Cables are installed in a conduit of special electrical metallic tubing with permanently sealed connections. If buried underground (e.g., between buildings), the carrier must be encased in concrete, and access points must be locked down. Requires regular visual inspection.
    • Alarmed carrier PDS: The system uses specialized optical fibers within the conduit to sense acoustic vibrations caused by intruders attempting to access the cables, triggering an alarm. Advantages include continuous monitoring and elimination of periodic visual inspections.

C. Computer Hardware Security

This physical security specifically protects mobile hardware like laptops, which are easily stolen.

  • Cable Locks: Most portable devices and expensive monitors have a steel bracket security slot built into the case. A cable lock can be inserted and secured to the device, with the cable then connected to an immovable object.
  • Safes and Vaults: Ruggedized steel boxes with locks used for storage. Safes can be small (for one laptop) or large, and can be prewired for electrical power and wired network connections, allowing devices to charge and receive updates while stored.

III. Data Controls

Protecting data is imperative, as it is considered the "lifeblood of technology". Protection strategies require understanding data classifications, types, breach consequences, and effective controls.

A. Data Classifications

To determine the appropriate level of protection, data is categorized into distinct classifications. The required protection level is influenced by the data's value, which can range from virtually priceless (like digital photos) to having little value (like old news headlines).

Data type Description Recommended handling
Confidential Highest level of data sensitivity. Should only be made available to users with the highest level of preapproved authentication.
Private Restricted data with a medium level of confidentiality. For users who have a need-to-know basis of the contents.
Sensitive Data that could cause catastrophic harm to the company if disclosed (e.g., technical specifications for a new product). Restricted to employees who have a business need to access the data and have been approved.
Critical Data classified according to availability needs; if unavailable, the function and mission would be severely impacted. Critical data must be rigorously protected.
Public No risk of release. For all public consumption; assumed public if no other label is attached.
Restricted Data that is not available to the public. Caution should be exercised before using this kind of information in emails.

When assigning a classification, the data's confidentiality should be considered along with its integrity and availability. Note that there is no universal agreement on data classifications, with different entities using three, four, five, or more types.

B. Types of Data

Data types categorize data based on their nature, and similar data types would fall into a single data classification.

  • Regulated Data: Data subjected to external stipulations concerning who can view and use it. An example is Protected Health Information (PHI), regulated by HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996).
  • Intellectual Property (IP) Data: An invention or work resulting from creativity. IP protection includes patent, trademark, copyright, or trade secret. Threat actors actively seek to steal IP research.
  • Trade Secret Data: Undisclosed enterprise data. It must have actual or potential independent economic value by virtue of not being generally known, have value to others who cannot legitimately obtain it, and be subject to reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy. Unlike patents, trade secret protection has no time limit, but it ceases to exist if any of the three required elements are lost.
  • Enterprise Information: Includes Legal information (factual information about the law, considered neutral) and Financial information (data on monetary transactions, such as credit card numbers or credit ratings).
  • Human- and Non-human-readable Data:
    • Human-readable data is what a person can read and interpret.
    • Non-human-readable data (machine-readable data) is interpreted by a device and is not readily understood by a person in its native state. Examples include JSON (JavaScript object notation) and XML (Extensible Markup Language).

C. Data Breach Consequences

Organizations suffering a data breach face significant consequences.

  1. Reputation Damage: Bad publicity often leads to a tarnished reputation, evidenced by loss of customers and a drop in stock price. Organizations are usually required to send data breach letters to users, magnifying the reputational harm.
  2. IP Theft: Loss of Intellectual Property owned by the organization or its customers.
  3. Fines: Financial penalties assessed under various federal and state laws (e.g., HIPAA, Sarbox, GLBA, PCI DSS). Organizations in the European Union face fines based on the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), with a second tier imposing fines up to 20 million euros or 4 percent of the firm’s worldwide annual revenue, whichever is higher.

D. Protecting Data: Considerations and Methods

Creating strong data controls involves several general considerations about the data.

General Data Considerations

  • Data State (Condition): Data can reside in three states:
    • Data in processing (Data in use): Data on which devices are performing actions (e.g., printing a report).
    • Data in transit (Data in motion): Actions that transmit data across a network (e.g., an email sent across the Internet).
    • Data at rest: Data stored on electronic media.
  • Location: Geolocation identifies the data's location using techniques encompassing latitude and longitude coordinates. GeoIP (Geolocation by Internet Protocol) relies specifically on the device's IP address.
  • Data Sovereignty: Country-specific government regulations that apply to data, usually determined by the country in which the data is collected or processed. Many countries (e.g., Russia, China, Germany) require citizens' data to be stored on physical servers within their borders.

Data Security Methods

Various techniques and technologies enhance data protection.

  • Data Minimization: Limiting the collection of personal information to only what is directly relevant and necessary for a specific task; collection should be adequate, relevant, and not excessive.
  • Data Masking: Creating a copy of the original data and using obfuscation (making unintelligible) on sensitive elements (like a Social Security number). Proper data masking replaces actual information that is not required and should be irreversible. It is one means of data sanitization (the process of cleaning data to provide privacy protection).
  • Tokenization: Obfuscating sensitive data elements (like account numbers) into a random string of characters (token). The original data and the token are stored in a token vault for retrieval if needed. If the original data can be restored, it is called pseudo-anonymization.
  • Restrictions: Limits imposed on data access.
    • Permission restrictions limit access only to individuals and devices with a legitimate business need.
    • Geographic restrictions limit access to specific locations (e.g., HIPAA data only accessible on the hospital campus).
  • Segmentation: Identifying data elements' classification, tagging them, and then separating the most sensitive data. This sensitive data is defined as the "protect surface," and extra security controls are applied around it.

To solidify understanding, consider the principles of physical security as being analogous to protecting a medieval castle:

  • Perimeter Defenses (Fencing, Bollards) are like the outer moat and walls, designed to passively stop invaders.
  • Security Guards and Video Surveillance are the archers on the battlements, providing an active defense and watchful eyes.
  • Security Buffers (Access Control Vestibules/Mantraps) are the gatehouse, a restricted, interlocking area where identity must be confirmed before proceeding deeper into the keep.
  • Data Controls are like protecting the crown jewels, ensuring they are hidden (Confidential), only viewed by trusted advisors (Private/Sensitive), and locked away (Tokenization/Masking), subject only to the King's laws (Data Sovereignty).

Study Guide: Pervasive Attack Surfaces and Controls

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of social engineering, physical security, and data controls. It includes a quiz to test your understanding, essay questions for deeper analysis, and a complete glossary of key terms from the module.

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

Instructions: Click on a question to reveal the answer.

1. What is social engineering, and what is considered its primary attack surface?

Social engineering is a method of eliciting information or convincing a user to take an action that weakens security, primarily through deception and manipulation. Because it exploits a person rather than a technology vulnerability, its attack surface is accomplished using "human vectors."

2. Explain the psychological principles of Authority and Intimidation as they are used in social engineering attacks.

The principle of Authority involves an attacker directing a target by impersonating an authority figure, such as a CEO, to make their request seem legitimate. Intimidation is used to frighten and coerce a target by threatening negative consequences, such as telling the target's supervisor if they do not comply with a demand.

3. Differentiate between Spear Phishing and Whaling.

Spear phishing is a targeted email attack that is customized to a specific user, often including their name and personal details to appear more legitimate. Whaling is a specific type of spear phishing that targets "big fish," such as wealthy individuals or senior executives, with the goal of accessing larger sums of money or more valuable information.

4. What is a Business Email Compromise (BEC), and what are two common forms it takes?

A Business Email Compromise (BEC) is a type of phishing attack that exploits the common business practice of making electronic payments by sending fraudulent requests for funds that appear legitimate. Two common forms are the Bogus Invoice, where an attacker pretends to be a supplier demanding payment, and Executive Fraud, where an attacker poses as an executive to order an employee to transfer funds.

5. Explain the difference between Typo Squatting and Bitsquatting.

Typo squatting involves registering domain names that are common misspellings of legitimate sites (e.g., goggle.com for google.com) to capture traffic from users who make typing errors. Bitsquatting is more technical, involving the registration of domain names that are one binary bit different from a legitimate domain, hoping to capture traffic from rare RAM memory errors that "flip" a bit in a character.

6. Describe two types of fencing deterrents used to enhance perimeter security.

One deterrent is anti-climb paint, a non-hardening, petroleum-based gel that makes surfaces difficult to climb. Another is the roller barrier, which consists of large, independently rotating cups affixed to the top of a fence that prevent an intruder from getting a handhold to climb over.

7. What is an access control vestibule and in what type of security environment is it typically used?

An access control vestibule, sometimes called a mantrap, is a security buffer with two interlocking doors where only one door can be open at a time. It is used in high-security areas like cash handling rooms or research labs to create a controlled space where credentials can be verified before granting access to the secure area.

8. Explain the function of a Faraday cage and an alarmed carrier Protected Distribution System (PDS) in preventing data leakage.

A Faraday cage is a metallic enclosure that blocks electromagnetic fields, preventing the unauthorized interception of signals (EMI) emitted by electronic devices. An alarmed carrier PDS protects data cables in a conduit containing specialized optical fibers; these fibers sense acoustic vibrations from any attempt to access the cables and trigger an alarm.

9. List and define the three states data can be in.

The three data states are data at rest, which is data stored on electronic media; data in transit (or in motion), which is data being transmitted across a network; and data in processing (or in use), which is data actively being used by a device for actions like generating a report.

10. Describe the data security methods of Data Masking and Tokenization.

Data masking creates a copy of original data but makes sensitive elements unintelligible (obfuscation), with no way to reverse the process back to the original state. Tokenization also obfuscates sensitive data, but it replaces the data with a random string of characters (a token) and stores the original data in a secure "token vault," allowing for retrieval if needed.

Smishing A phishing attack that uses the Short Message Service (SMS) to send fraudulent text messages.
Social Engineering A means of eliciting information or convincing a user to take an action that weakens security through deception and manipulation.
Spear Phishing A type of phishing attack that targets specific users with customized emails to make the message appear more legitimate.
Tokenization A data security method that obfuscates sensitive data by replacing it with a random string of characters (a token), while storing the original data in a secure token vault for later retrieval.
Trade Secret Data Undisclosed enterprise data that has economic value by not being generally known and is subject to reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy.
Typo Squatting The act of purchasing and registering domain names that are spelled similarly to actual sites to capture traffic from users who make typing errors.
Ultrasonic Sensor A sensor that uses high-frequency sound waves (ultrasound) to measure the distance to a target object, often used for proximity detection.
Vishing A phishing attack that uses a telephone call (voice phishing) to trick a target into revealing information.
Watering Hole Attack An attack directed at a small group of specific individuals by infecting a common website they are all known to frequent.
Whaling A type of spear phishing that specifically targets wealthy individuals or senior executives ("big fish").
