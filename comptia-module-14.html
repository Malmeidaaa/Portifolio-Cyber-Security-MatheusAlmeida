1. The Context of Technology and Sports (#TrendingCyber)

Technology and sports have a long history, but their integration has deepened drastically. Originally, technology was introduced clandestinely to gain a competitive advantage, such as the use of a radio receiver by the NFL's Cleveland Browns in 1956, which was subsequently banned by the league commissioner until 1994.

Currently, sports leagues collect huge volumes of real-time data:

  • NFL: Uses up to 30 ultra-wideband (UWB) receivers and radio frequency identification (RFID) tags on players, referees, and even the ball, collecting over 200 new data points on every play.
  • FIFA (2022 World Cup): Used 12 cameras with four-dimensional optical tracking, monitoring 29 points on players' bodies, assisting referees with offside calls and revealing valuable performance analytics.

Data analytics is changing the game itself (e.g., in baseball, home runs are prioritized over bunts, and in football, the shotgun formation is preferred over plays under center).

Privacy: This raises serious questions about athlete data privacy, including the use of data analytics to predict injuries in contract negotiations and whether nutrition and sleep data should be shared with bettors.

2. Oversight and Administration

Information security is divided into Oversight and Operations.

  • Oversight: The review and monitoring of processes, policies, plans, programs, or projects, watching them "from above" ("over"). The goal is to ensure actions achieve expected results, comply with policies, laws, and ethical standards, and represent good value for money.
  • Operations: The opposite of oversight, being the daily work "in the trenches" of configuring equipment, applying controls, and reacting to cyber incidents.

2.1. Governance

Governance refers to the structures, systems, and practices an organization implements to assign, oversee, and report.

The three key activities are:

  1. Assign: Decision-making responsibilities and setting strategic direction.
  2. Oversee: Monitoring service delivery, policy/plan implementation, and risk mitigation.
  3. Report: Reporting performance to drive continuous improvement and corrective actions (monitoring and review).

Governance Roles over Data and Systems

Role Description
Owner Person responsible for the information; determines security level and delegates duties.
Controller Principal party responsible for data collection (obtains consent, stores).
Processor Proxy acting on behalf of the controller; holds and processes data but makes no decisions on use.
Custodian Person assigned day-to-day tasks by the owner (periodically reviews security settings).
DPO Data Protection Officer. Manager who oversees data privacy compliance and manages risks.

Governance Mechanisms

Mechanism Definition Nature
Policy Formal statement of rules and requirements (e.g., AUP, SDLC). Mandatory
Procedure Detailed step-by-step instructions to comply with a policy. Mandatory
Standard Uniform use of technologies or configurations (e.g., PCI DSS). Mandatory
Guideline General guidance and advice. Voluntary

2.2. Compliance

Compliance is the process of ensuring an organization adheres to laws and regulations related to information security and user data privacy.

  • Key Regulations: GDPR (EU), PCI DSS (Payments), HIPAA (US Health).
  • Importance ("Carrot and Stick"): Provides "best practices" but imposes severe penalties (fines, sanctions, loss of license).

Compliance Monitoring

  • Internal Monitoring: Performed by the organization itself (dedicated team + automation). Generates internal reports.
  • External Monitoring: Performed by third parties (audit). Generates official attestation.

Data Privacy

  • Explicit Collection: User provides data for valid need.
  • Hidden Collection: Secret tracking (trackers in apps and IoT).

Legal Protections:

  • EU (GDPR): Right to be forgotten (erase data in ~30 days).
  • USA: No federal right to be forgotten; sector-specific regulations (HIPAA).

3. Security Operations

Security work is performed in the SOC (Security Operations Center). The modern SOC uses automation, orchestration, threat hunting, and AI.

3.1. Automation

Necessary due to data volume. Generates faster and scalable protection.

  • Benefits: Efficiency, baseline enforcement, scalability, staff retention (less burnout).
  • Risks: Complexity, cost, technical debt, single point of failure.
  • Use Cases: CI/CD, API Integration (iPaaS), Scripting, Guardrails (cloud), Automated Provisioning.

3.2. Orchestration (SOAR)

Unlike automation (single task), orchestration automates a complete workflow, connecting multiple tools.

SOAR: Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response.

3.3. Threat Hunting

Proactive, iterative, and human activity.

  • Premise: Assumes the network is already infected.
  • Process: Hypothesis -> Investigation -> Action.
  • Maturity: Level 1 (Initial) to Level 5 (Optimized/Continuous).

3.4. Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Gained popularity with ChatGPT (2022).

  • Data Analytics: Fixed process, relies on humans.
  • AI: Autonomous, iterative, dynamic.
  • Machine Learning (ML): Subset of AI. Learns from data (statistics) without explicit programming.

Riscos (Adversarial AI):

  • Compromise algorithms.
  • Taint Training Data.
  • Malicious use (Mutating malware).

Study Guide: Information Security Oversight and Operations

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

Instructions: Click on a question to reveal the answer.

1. What is information security governance and what are its three primary activities?

Governance refers to the structures, systems, and practices an organization uses for oversight and administration. Its three core activities are assigning decision-making responsibilities and strategic direction; overseeing the implementation of policies, plans, and risk mitigation; and reporting on performance to drive improvements.

2. Differentiate between a security policy and a security procedure.

A policy is a formal statement from a governing body that outlines specific requirements or rules that must be met to achieve the organization's strategic objectives. A procedure provides the detailed, mandatory, step-by-step instructions that an employee must follow to implement and comply with a given policy.

3. What is compliance monitoring, and what are the two main ways it can be performed?

Compliance monitoring refers to the quality assurance tests organizations use to determine how well their operations adhere to security laws, regulations, and standards. It can be performed internally by a dedicated compliance team, often using automation tools to generate reports. It can also be performed externally by a professional third party, which examines the organization's protections and creates an official report or attestation.

4. Explain the concept of threat hunting and its core assumption regarding the network's security status.

Threat hunting is a proactive and predominantly human-led activity to identify a cyber invasion that has evaded existing security controls. Its core assumption is that the network is already infected, meaning attackers are currently operating inside the network rather than trying to enter from the outside.

5. How does security orchestration differ from security automation?

Security automation involves setting a single security-related task to run on its own without human intervention. Security orchestration is a broader concept that automates an entire process or workflow by combining and coordinating multiple individual automated tasks across different platforms, including hardware, software, and services.

6. Describe the relationship between data analytics, artificial intelligence (AI), and machine learning (ML).

Data analytics is the process of examining large data sets to draw conclusions, where researchers manually create algorithms to find patterns. AI is a subset of data analytics that is autonomous, using the algorithms to create its own predictive models. ML is a further subset of AI that uses statistical techniques to "learn" from data and progressively improve its performance without being explicitly reprogrammed.

7. What is an acceptable-use policy (AUP), and who does it typically cover?

An acceptable-use policy is a formal written statement that defines the actions users are permitted to perform while accessing an organization's systems and networking equipment. The policy's audience is not limited to employees but also includes vendors, contractors, and visitors, each of whom may have different privileges.

8. Identify two potential risks or negative considerations when implementing advanced security automation.

Implementing security automation introduces several risks, including increased complexity in managing interconnected appliances and higher initial and ongoing support costs. Other risks include creating technical debt if problems are not addressed immediately and the potential for a single point of failure if there is an over-reliance on the automated system.

9. Explain the "right to be forgotten" principle as it relates to data protection in the European Union.

The "right to be forgotten" is a fundamental principle of EU data protection that gives data subjects (any living individual whose data is collected) the legal right to have their private data erased by a data controller without undue delay, which is typically interpreted to be about 30 days.

10. What is adversarial artificial intelligence, and provide one example of how it can be used?

Adversarial artificial intelligence refers to threat actors attacking AI systems or using AI for malicious purposes. One example is when attackers taint the training data used by a machine learning system, causing it to produce false negatives and effectively cloak the attackers' malicious activities from detection. Another example is using AI to develop mutating malware that can change its structure to evade signature-based defenses.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Acceptable-Use Policy (AUP) A policy that defines the actions users may perform while accessing systems and networking equipment, covering employees, vendors, contractors, and visitors.
Accountability A principle of good governance; the obligation of an individual, group, or organization to answer for a responsibility that has been conferred.
Adversarial Artificial Intelligence The practice of threat actors attacking AI systems or taking advantage of AI for malicious purposes, such as tainting training data or using AI to create mutating malware.
Application Programming Interface (API) A link provided by an OS, web browser, or other platform that allows a developer access to resources at a high level, enabling faster code creation.
Artificial Intelligence (AI) A subset of data analytics where technologies can understand and act by themselves based on acquired information. AI systems are autonomous, iterative, and dynamic.
Attestation The verification of a statement's truth or authenticity, such as an external compliance report serving as an official attestation of compliance monitoring.
Automation The use of technology to perform a task or process with minimal human assistance. In security, it streamlines and speeds up processes to provide timely insights and faster protection.
Board An internal governance body composed of directors who approve strategic organizational goals and policies.
Compliance The process of ensuring that an organization adheres to laws and regulations related to information security and user data privacy.
Compliance Monitoring Quality assurance tests performed by organizations to determine how well their business operations meet security regulations and standards. It can be internal or external.
Controller A data governance role; the principal party responsible for collecting data, acquiring user consent, and managing access.
Custodian/Steward A data governance role; an individual to whom day-to-day actions have been assigned by the owner, such as reviewing security settings and maintaining access records.
Data Analytics A fixed process that examines large data sets to draw conclusions, where researchers manually create algorithms to learn patterns and correlations from historical data.
Data Privacy Officer (DPO) A data governance role; a manager who oversees data privacy compliance and manages data risk, ensuring the enterprise complies with privacy laws and policies.
Data Subjects In the context of the EU, any living individual whose personal data is collected, processed, and stored by an organization.
Due Care The act of taking reasonable steps to secure and protect an organization's assets, reputation, and finances.
Due Diligence The act of identifying and mitigating risks brought on by third parties.
Employee Offboarding The procedure entailing actions to be taken when an employee leaves an enterprise, such as disabling accounts and backing up files.
Employee Onboarding The procedure encompassing the tasks associated with hiring a new employee, such as signing an NDA and configuring accounts.
Governance The structures, systems, and practices an organization has in place to assign decision-making responsibilities, oversee implementation, and report on performance.
Guardrails (Security) Automations that constantly watch cloud deployments, find deviations from desired baselines, and can automatically remediate issues without slowing development.
Guideline A governance mechanism that provides general, voluntary guidance and support for policies, standards, or procedures.
Incident Response Playbook A type of procedure that lists specific, mandatory actions to take in response to different threats.
Integrity A principle of good governance; acting in a way that is impartial and ethical, reflected through compliance with laws and high standards of professionalism.
Leadership A principle of good governance; "setting the tone" to encourage personnel to embrace good governance practices.
Machine Learning (ML) A subset of AI that uses statistical techniques to give computer systems the ability to “learn” or progressively improve performance using data, rather than being explicitly programmed.
Noncompliance The failure to adhere to compliance standards, which can result in penalties such as fines, sanctions, reputational damage, or loss of license.
Orchestration (Security) The automation and combination of many different individual tasks into a single, functional, automated process or workflow, crossing multiple platforms.
Oversight The act of reviewing and monitoring processes, policies, or projects from above to ensure they achieve expected results and comply with standards, without direct involvement in operations.
Owner A data governance role; the person responsible for the information who determines the level of security needed and delegates duties.
Policy A formal statement from a governing body that outlines specific requirements or rules that must be met to achieve an organization's strategic objectives.
Procedure A governance mechanism that provides detailed, mandatory steps a user must follow to comply with a policy.
Processor A data governance role; a proxy (person or agency) who acts on behalf of the data controller to hold and process data but does not make decisions about its use.
Provisioning The process of creating and setting up an IT infrastructure, including managing user and system access to resources.
Right to be Forgotten A fundamental EU data protection principle giving data subjects the legal right to have their private data erased by a data controller without undue delay.
Security Operations Center (SOC) A function or team within an organization responsible for proactive monitoring, incident response and recovery, remediation, and other security tasks.
SOAR Platform A Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response platform that combines tools to synthesize and automate security operations, threat intelligence, and incident response.
Standard A governance mechanism that specifies the uniform use of specific technologies or settings for secure configurations; can be internal or from a third party like PCI DSS.
Stewardship A principle of good governance; the act of responsibly looking after resources on behalf of the organization.
Threat Hunting A proactive, iterative, and predominantly human-led activity to identify a cyber invasion that has evaded existing security controls, assuming the network is already compromised.
Transparency A principle of good governance; achieved when decisions and actions are open, allowing stakeholders access to full, accurate, and clear information.
