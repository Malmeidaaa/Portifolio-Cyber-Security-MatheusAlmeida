Module 3: Cryptography Fundamentals

The Fundamentals of Cryptography and Information Security

The Fundamentals of Cryptography and Information Security

I. Defining Cryptography and Its Historical Context

Cryptography is fundamentally the science of protecting information by transforming it into an unreadable format. At its core, it is the practice of secure communication in the presence of adversaries. The main goal of cryptography is to ensure that only the intended recipient can read and process the information.

Core Terminology

When using cryptography, specific terminology is applied:

  1. Plaintext: The original, readable data that is input for encryption or the output of decryption.
  2. Ciphertext: The scrambled, unreadable output data resulting from encryption.
  3. Encryption: The process of changing the original text (plaintext) into a scrambled message.
  4. Decryption: The reverse process of changing the ciphertext back to its original plaintext form.
  5. Cleartext: Unencrypted data that is not intended to be encrypted (it is "in the clear").
  6. Key: A mathematical value entered into a cryptographic algorithm to produce the ciphertext. The key is the critical factor that must be kept secret at all costs, even though the algorithm itself is often public and well-known.

Plaintext data is input into a cryptographic algorithm (also called a cipher), which consists of procedures based on a mathematical formula. Just as a key is inserted into a door lock to lock the door, a unique mathematical key is input into the encryption algorithm to "lock" the data by creating the ciphertext.

Cryptography vs. Steganography

The history of hiding messages has roots dating back to the ancient Greeks, who initially focused on hiding the existence of a message rather than its meaning.

  • Steganography: Hides the existence of the message or information itself (from Greek meaning "covered writing"). Examples include writing messages in ink on wooden tablets covered with wax, hiding messages in a tattoo, or concealing them in metadata of a digital file. Steganography is a form of obfuscation, which means making something obscure.
  • Cryptography: Hides the meaning of the message (from Greek meaning "hidden writing"). This involves transforming or scrambling information so that unauthorized parties cannot determine its contents.

Steganography is sometimes used in conjunction with encryption, where the data is first encrypted and then hidden, requiring an attacker to first find the data and then decrypt it.

Early Cryptographic Methods

Since simply trying to hide a message (steganography) was often ineffective once discovered, the ancient Greeks turned to making messages intelligible only to the desired recipient, pioneering two methods still used today:

  1. Transposition: Each letter of the message is rearranged (e.g., 'ran' becomes 'arn'). For short messages, this can be easily broken, but for longer messages, it becomes much more difficult for an outsider—though also more difficult for the recipient.
  2. Substitution: One letter is substituted for another letter. Examples include ROT13, which rotates the alphabet 13 steps (e.g., 'security' becomes 'frphevgl'), or substituting numbers for letters. A more complex substitution is the XOR cipher, based on the binary operation Exclusive OR.

II. Foundational Security Concepts

Cryptography is essential to information security, protecting data across all its states: data at rest, data in use, and data in transit. The field of cybersecurity is built upon core principles, often summarized by the CIA Triad, plus two additional critical concepts.

The CIA Triad and Extended Principles

The main principles of cryptography align with these foundational security concepts:

  • Confidentiality: Preventing unauthorized access to information. It ensures that only authorized individuals can view the data. Techniques like encryption and access controls are used to maintain confidentiality.
  • Integrity: Ensuring that information has not been altered, deleted, or corrupted in any unauthorized way, maintaining its accuracy and reliability. Cryptography ensures integrity because ciphertext requires a key to be changed. Digital signatures and hashing are key to maintaining integrity.
  • Authentication: Verifying the identity of the sender and receiver. Cryptography can verify the genuineness of the user, preventing messages from imposters.
  • Non-Repudiation: Proving that a message was sent or received by a specific individual. This prevents an individual from fraudulently denying they performed an action.
  • Availability: Guarantees that systems and data are accessible to authorized users when needed. (While not a direct function of encryption, selecting inappropriate cryptographic solutions can impact availability through performance issues).
  • Obfuscation: Making something obscure or unclear. Encryption provides a degree of obfuscation, though its security depends on the secret key. Obfuscation alone (security through obscurity) is considered a flawed general information security protection strategy.

The protections offered by cryptography are summarized below:

Characteristic Description Protection
Confidentiality Ensures that only authorized parties can view the information. Encrypted information can only be viewed by those who have been provided the key.
Integrity Ensures that the information is correct and no unauthorized person or malicious software has altered the data. Encrypted information cannot be changed except by authorized users who have the key.
Authentication Provides proof of the genuineness of the user. Proof that the sender was legitimate and not an imposter can be obtained.
Nonrepudiation Proves that a user performed an action. Individuals are prevented from fraudulently denying that they were involved in a transaction.
Obfuscation Makes something obscure or unclear. By making it obscure, the original information cannot be determined.

III. Cryptographic Algorithms

Cryptographic algorithms are organized into three broad categories: Hash algorithms, Symmetric cryptographic algorithms, and Asymmetric cryptographic algorithms.

1. Hash Algorithms

A hash algorithm is a one-way function that takes an input (or 'message') and produces a fixed-size string of characters called a hash value or digest (also called a message digest or hash). The process is called hashing. It is often described as a digital fingerprint. Hashing is a one-way process because it is computationally infeasible (or impossible) to reverse it and get the original data from the hash value. Hash algorithms are primarily used to ensure data integrity and for comparison purposes.

A secure hashing algorithm must have the following characteristics:

  • Fixed size: A digest of a short data set must produce the same size digest as a long data set.
  • Unique: Two different sets of data cannot produce the same digest; changing a single letter in one data set should produce an entirely different digest.
  • Original: It should not be possible to produce a data set that has a desired or predefined hash.
  • Secure: The resulting hash cannot be reversed to determine the original plaintext.

Common Hash Algorithms:

  • Message Digest (MD): MD5 was the most widely used version, but serious weaknesses have been identified, and it is no longer considered suitable for use.
  • Secure Hash Algorithm (SHA): SHA-1 is no longer suitable for use. SHA-2 has six variations, with SHA-256, SHA-384, and SHA-512 being common (the number indicates the digest length in bits) and are currently considered secure. SHA-3 was announced in 2015 and was designed to be dissimilar to previous hash algorithms to prevent threat actors from compromising them based on earlier weaknesses.
  • RipeMD (RACE Integrity Primitives Evaluation Message Digest): Features two independent parallel chains of computation whose results are combined. Versions include RipeMD-160, RipeMD-256, and RipeMD-320.
  • Whirlpool: Uses a block cipher and returns a 512-bit message digest for messages less than 2256 bits. The authors have stated it is not and will never be patented and may be used free of charge.

2. Symmetric Cryptographic Algorithms

Symmetric encryption uses a single, shared secret key for both encrypting and decrypting data. This is also known as private key cryptography. The sender uses the key to "lock" the message, and the receiver uses the exact same key to "unlock" it.

  • Characteristics: This method is very fast and efficient, making it ideal for encrypting large amounts of data.
  • Primary Weakness: The biggest challenge is securely sharing the key between the two parties. Since the key cannot be sent electronically unencrypted, secure distribution and maintenance of the single key among multiple geographically scattered users pose significant challenges.
  • Key Security: The element that must be kept secret is the key itself.

Common Symmetric Algorithms (Strong Algorithms):

  • Advanced Encryption Standard (AES): Performs three steps on every 128-bit block of plaintext. The number of internal rounds depends on the key size (e.g., AES-256 uses 13 rounds for a 256-bit key). To date, no attacks have been successful against AES.
  • Blowfish and Twofish: Blowfish is a block cipher that operates on 64-bit blocks with key lengths from 32 to 448 bits. Twofish is a later derivation considered strong, though less widely used than Blowfish.

Note: Older symmetric algorithms like Data Encryption Standard (DES), Triple Data Encryption Standard (3DES), and Rivest Cipher (RC) have been deprecated and should no longer be used.

3. Asymmetric Cryptographic Algorithms

Asymmetric encryption, also known as public-key cryptography, uses a pair of mathematically linked keys: a public key and a private key. This method solves the key-sharing problem inherent in symmetric encryption.

  • Key Function: The public key can be shared freely and is known to everyone. The private key must remain secret and is known only to the individual it belongs to.
  • Encryption/Decryption: Data encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the corresponding private key, and vice-versa.
  • Characteristics: This method is slower than symmetric encryption.
  • Primary Uses: Authentication, digital signatures, and securely exchanging a symmetric key.
  • Key Security: The element that must be kept secret is the private key.

Common Asymmetric Algorithms:

  • RSA: Published in 1977, RSA’s security is based on factoring, specifically the difficulty for modern computers to quickly factor the large prime numbers ($p$ and $q$) that make up the value $n$ (where $n = p \times q$). The public key is the pair $(n, e)$, and the private key is $(n, d)$.
  • Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC): ECC is a newer form of asymmetric encryption not based on factoring. It uses sloping curves (a set of points satisfying a specific mathematical equation) instead of large prime numbers. ECC is gaining popularity because it offers comparable security to RSA with smaller key sizes, leading to faster computations and lower power consumption, making it ideal for mobile devices. ECC is used by the U.S. government, the Tor project, and for verifying Bitcoin ownership.

Digital Signatures

Asymmetric cryptography is crucial for creating digital signatures, which provide electronic verification of the sender. A digital signature serves as proof, similar to a handwritten signature, but offers additional benefits:

  • Verify the sender: Confirms the identity of the originator.
  • Prevent the sender from disowning the message (Nonrepudiation): The signer cannot fraudulently claim the signature was forged.
  • Prove the integrity of the message: Proves the message has not been altered since it was signed.

The process for creating and verifying a digital signature relies on the keys working in both directions:

  1. The sender creates a digest (hash) of the document.
  2. The sender encrypts the digest with their private key—this encrypted digest is the digital signature.
  3. The sender transmits both the original document and the digital signature.
  4. The receiver uses the sender's public key to decrypt the digital signature, revealing the original digest. If decryption fails, the message did not come from the legitimate sender.
  5. The receiver hashes the received document using the same algorithm and compares the newly generated hash to the decrypted digest. If they match, the message integrity is proven.

The Digital Signature Algorithm (DSA) is a U.S. government standard for digital signatures.

Key Exchange

Key exchange is necessary for securely sending and receiving keys, especially symmetric private keys, within normal communication channels.

  • Diffie-Hellman (DH): Requires two parties (Alice and Bob) to agree on a large prime number and a related integer, which can be public. Through mathematical computations and the exchange of intermediate values, they can separately create the same shared key.
  • Diffie-Hellman Ephemeral (DHE): Uses temporary keys (ephemeral keys) that are used only once and then discarded, unlike DH, which uses the same keys each time.
  • Elliptic Curve Diffie–Hellman (ECDH): Uses elliptic curve cryptography instead of prime numbers for computation.
  • Perfect Forward Secrecy: Public key systems that generate random public keys that are different for each session. If a secret key is compromised, it cannot reveal the contents of more than one message.

IV. Algorithm Variations and Enterprise Security Strategies

Variations of Algorithms

Cryptographic algorithms can vary based on the device used (cipher machines vs. computers) and the amount of data processed (stream vs. block).

  • Cipher Machines vs. Computers: Prior to electronic computers, mechanical devices like the cipher disk (developed in 1470) and the Enigma machine (used by Germany during WWII) were used to encrypt data.
  • One-Time Pad (OTP): A secure cryptographic algorithm that is entirely hand-calculated. It combines plaintext with a long, random key (pad). OTP is the only known encryption method that cannot be broken mathematically, provided the pad is used only once and then destroyed.

  • Stream vs. Block:
    • Stream cipher: Takes one character and replaces it with one character. Stream ciphers are generally less secure because the engine that generates the stream does not vary, only the plaintext changes.
    • Block cipher: Manipulates an entire block of plaintext (typically 8 to 16 bytes) at one time. Block ciphers are considered more secure because the output is more random, as the cipher is reset to its original state after each block is processed.
  • Sponge Function: A newer type of algorithm that takes an input string of any length and returns a string of any requested variable length.

Data Protection Strategies

Protecting data requires different strategies depending on the data's state and the type of control implemented.

Data at Rest vs. Data in Transit
State Definition Primary Threat Protection Strategy
Data at Rest Data stored on a device (e.g., hard drive, server, cloud storage). Unauthorized access to the physical storage medium. Full disk encryption (FDE), making data unreadable without a decryption key.
Data in Transit Data moving across a network (e.g., internet or local network). Network interception. Transport encryption, using protocols like TLS/SSL/HTTPS to create a secure, encrypted tunnel.
Control Types (Defense-in-Depth)

Mitigation techniques reduce the risk and impact of a cyberattack and are often multi-layered. These three types of controls work together to form a "defense-in-depth" strategy:

Control Type Purpose/Analogy Examples
Preventative Designed to stop an attack before it happens (like a locked door). Firewalls, antivirus software, regular security patching, employee training.
Detective Aims to identify an ongoing attack or past breach (like an alarm system). Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS), Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems, network monitoring tools.
Corrective Used after an attack to minimize damage and restore operations (like a cleanup crew). Data backups, disaster recovery plans, incident response procedures.

Relying on just one type of control leaves the system vulnerable at different stages of an attack.

Indicators of Malicious Activity (IoCs)

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) are forensic data signaling a potential security breach. Common indicators of malicious activity include:

  • Unusual Network Traffic: Large, sudden spikes in data leaving the network, connections to known malicious IP addresses, or traffic on unusual ports.
  • Anomalous Account Behavior: An employee's account logging in at odd hours, from a different geographical location, or attempting to access unauthorized files.
  • Unauthorized File Modifications: Critical system files are altered, encrypted (as in a ransomware attack), or renamed. New, unfamiliar files appear in unexpected locations.
  • Unexpected System or Application Behavior: Applications crashing, system services stopping, or automated scripts running on a machine without a clear purpose.

V. Implementation and Uses of Cryptography

Cryptography is a fundamental technology in modern life used across various applications.

Common Uses of Cryptography

  • Secure Communication: Protocols like HTTPS and SSL/TLS secure web browsing, email, and messaging.
  • Digital Signatures: Used to verify the authenticity and integrity of digital documents.
  • Secure Payments: Protecting online transactions and credit card information.
  • Secure Storage: Safeguarding data at rest, such as password protection and full disk encryption.
  • Cryptocurrencies: Blockchain technology uses cryptographic principles to secure transactions and verify ownership.
  • Virtual Private Networks (VPNs): Creating a secure, encrypted tunnel for internet traffic.

1. Software Encryption

Software-based cryptography can be applied at the file, disk, or database level.

  • File and File System Cryptography: Encrypting individual files (file-level encryption) or groups of files (file system cryptography). Modern OSs provide native encryption support, such as Microsoft’s Encrypting File System (EFS) and Apple’s FileVault. These are transparent to the user; authorized users open files which are decrypted as data is read from the disk and encrypted when saved. Third-party tools like VeraCrypt and GnuPG also exist.
  • Disk Encryption: Applying cryptography to entire disks or sections.
    • Full-Disk Encryption (FDE): Protects the entire drive, including the operating system (OS) and temporary files. BitLocker is an example of FDE software included in Microsoft Windows.
    • Volume-level encryption: Applied to a volume, which is an accessible section of a drive with an associated file system.
    • Partition-level encryption: May be applied if the partition (a logical division of a hard drive) is formatted and contains a file system.
  • Database Encryption: Applying cryptography to an organized collection of structured information (database-level encryption).
    • The two main methods are the plug-in method (attaching an encryption module to the Database Management System/DBMS) or transparent data encryption (TDE), which executes encryption and decryption within the database engine itself. TDE is easier to manage.
    • Encryption can occur at the database file level, table level, or column level. Encrypting each row (record-level encryption) or individual data element (cell) requires careful planning and imposes a significant "performance tax".

2. Hardware Encryption

Hardware encryption is generally considered a more secure option than software encryption because it cannot be exploited by attacks targeting software vulnerabilities.

  • USB Devices: Cryptographic features can be built directly into the hardware of a USB device, automatically encrypting all data written to it. Some devices offer remote control and tracking or can initiate a self-destruct sequence if stolen.
  • Self-Encrypting Drives (SEDs): Drives that automatically protect all data written to them. If the authentication process between the SED and the host device fails on startup, the SED can deny access or perform a cryptographic erase of the decryption keys.
  • Hardware Security Module (HSM): A removable external cryptographic device (USB, expansion card, or network server). An HSM includes an onboard random number generator, key storage, and accelerated symmetric and asymmetric encryption, performing all processes in hardware to prevent compromise by malware.
  • Trusted Execution Environment (TEE): A secure cryptoprocessor (motherboard chip) internal to the computer, protecting the confidentiality and integrity of code and data stored on it.
  • Trusted Platform Module (TPM): An international standard for cryptoprocessors that provides cryptographic services. A TPM includes a random number generator, full support for asymmetric encryption, and can test key components during startup to prevent booting if system files have been altered. Apple and Android devices use similar technology called a secure enclave.

3. Blockchain Technology

A blockchain is a shared, immutable ledger that facilitates recording transactions and tracking assets in a business network. It is a system where transaction records are maintained across several computers linked in a peer-to-peer network. Blockchain relies heavily on cryptographic hash algorithms, primarily SHA-256, to make it computationally infeasible to tamper with transactions without network consensus.

This system is designed to replace inefficient, duplicated ledgers maintained by separate entities (suppliers, retailers, banks) with a single, tamper-evident ledger.

There are different types of blockchains:

  1. Public Blockchain (Open Public Ledger): A network anyone can join, commonly used for exchanging cryptocurrencies.
  2. Private Blockchain: Operates in a closed network, best suited for internal enterprise use.
  3. Federated Blockchain (Consortium Blockchain): Used when multiple organizations require both public and private aspects, involving more than one organization providing controlled access to preselected nodes.

VI. Cryptographic Limitations and Attacks

Cryptography is not an absolute protection and continually faces constraints and attacks.

Cryptographic Limitations and Constraints

The importance of using appropriate cryptographic solutions is paramount. Using an inappropriate solution can compromise confidentiality, cause data integrity failures (e.g., due to collision attacks), or lead to performance issues impacting availability.

A major constraint is the resource versus security constraint. Cryptographic algorithms require time and energy, which are scarce resources in low-power devices (like IoT sensors, which draw very little energy) or applications requiring ultra-fast response times. This has led to the development of lightweight cryptography.

Other significant constraints include:

  • Key Management: The greatest challenge is the secure generation, distribution, and storage of cryptographic keys. If a key is compromised, the entire security of the system is at risk.
  • Human Factor: The human element remains the weakest link; users choosing weak passwords, falling for phishing, or mishandling private keys can bypass strong encryption.
  • Performance: Asymmetric encryption, in particular, requires significant computational power and can be too slow for real-time applications involving large data volumes.
Limitation Explanation
Speed The speed of encryption/decryption can be unacceptable in certain instances, depending on hardware and software factors.
Size The resulting size of an encrypted file can be up to one-third larger than the plaintext version.
Weak keys Some ciphers can produce a weak key that causes the cipher to behave unpredictably or compromise overall security.
Key length A short key length (number of bits) results in weaker security.
Longevity As computers become more powerful, the useful lifetime of ciphers may diminish.
Predictability A weak random number generator may create predictable output.
Reuse Reusing the same key for each encryption provides a larger data footprint for an attacker to use in attempting to break the encryption.
Entropy Low entropy (measure of randomness of a data-generating function) gives the ability to predict future-generated values.
Computational overhead Sensors and IoT devices often lack the capacity to accommodate the computational overhead required for cryptography.

Attacks on Cryptography

Attacks are generally categorized based on whether they target the key, the algorithm, or the implementation.

General Attacks:
  • Brute-Force Attack: An attacker systematically tries every possible key until the correct one is found. The key length determines the feasibility of this attack.
  • Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attack: An attacker secretly intercepts and relays communications between two parties who believe they are communicating directly.
  • Side-Channel Attacks: An attacker analyzes physical characteristics of a computing system, such as power consumption, timing, or electromagnetic radiation, to extract information about the cryptographic process.
  • Cryptanalysis: The study of methods for obtaining the plaintext of a message without access to the key, often involving mathematical and statistical techniques to find algorithm vulnerabilities.
Algorithm and Implementation Attacks:
  • Known Ciphertext Attack (Ciphertext-only attack): An attacker uses sophisticated statistical tools to analyze large sets of captured ciphertext in an attempt to discover a pattern that may reveal the plaintext or key.
  • Downgrade Attack: An attacker forces the system to abandon its current, higher security mode and revert ("fall back") to an older, less secure operational mode (often due to backward compatibility features), allowing the attacker to target the weaker mode.
  • Misconfiguration Implementation: Most cryptographic breaches result from incorrect choices or misconfigurations of options, such as selecting weak, deprecated algorithms like DES or SHA-1, or choosing a weaker available algorithm (like SHA-256 over SHA3-512).
  • Collision Attack: An attempt to find two input strings for a hash function that produce the same hash result (a collision). This violates the foundation of a secure hash algorithm. The statistical phenomenon known as the birthday attack makes it easier to find any two inputs that hash to the same value, rather than finding an input that collides with a specific predefined hash.

The Threat of Quantum Computing

Quantum computing relies on quantum physics using atomic-scale units called qubits. Unlike traditional bits (0 or 1), a qubit can be both 0 and 1 simultaneously, allowing for parallel calculations and making quantum computers significantly faster and more efficient than today’s machines.

  • Risk to Cryptography: Quantum computing poses a severe risk, particularly to asymmetric cryptography. Because asymmetric security relies on the difficulty of factoring large prime numbers, a quantum computer could perform this factoring rapidly, rendering virtually all current asymmetric cryptographic algorithms useless.
  • Mitigation: Researchers are developing "quantum-safe" encryption proposals, known as post-quantum cryptography, which would be secure against quantum computer attacks.
Analogy for Cryptography: If information security is a highly secured vault protecting your valuable data, then cryptography acts like the complex, advanced combination lock on the door. Symmetric encryption is like a single physical key that everyone must share, making its distribution difficult and risky. Asymmetric encryption is like having a mailbox (the public key) for receiving messages that anyone can use, but only you possess the unique, private key required to open the box and read the contents. Meanwhile, hashing is like a tamper-evident seal placed on the outside of a package; it doesn't hide the package, but if even a tiny piece of the contents is changed, the seal is immediately broken, alerting the recipient to the lack of integrity.

Study Guide

Short-Answer Quiz

1. What is the fundamental difference between cryptography and steganography?

Steganography hides the very existence of a message or information, such as writing a message in ink on a wooden tablet and then covering it with wax. In contrast, cryptography does not hide the message itself but rather hides its meaning by transforming it into an unreadable format. Essentially, steganography hides the message, while cryptography hides the meaning of the message.

2. Explain the primary challenge associated with symmetric encryption and how asymmetric encryption addresses it.

The primary challenge of symmetric encryption is the secure distribution and maintenance of the single, shared key among all parties. If the key is intercepted during transmission, the entire system is compromised. Asymmetric (or public key) cryptography solves this by using a pair of keys; the public key can be distributed freely without risk, while the private key remains secret, thus eliminating the key-sharing problem.

3. What are the four main characteristics of a secure hash algorithm?

A secure hash algorithm must have four key characteristics. It must produce a fixed size digest regardless of the input data's size. The digest must be unique, meaning two different data sets cannot produce the same digest. It must be original, making it impossible to produce a data set that has a predefined hash. Finally, it must be secure, meaning the hash cannot be reversed to determine the original plaintext.

4. Describe the process of creating a digital signature and what it verifies.

To create a digital signature, the sender first generates a hash digest of the message. The sender then encrypts this digest with their own private key. A digital signature serves to verify the sender's identity, prevent the sender from disowning the message (non-repudiation), and prove that the message has not been altered since it was signed (integrity).

5. What is a downgrade attack, and why is it a threat to security?

A downgrade attack is an attack where an adversary forces a system to abandon its current, higher-security mode of operation and "fall back" to an older, less secure mode. This is often possible due to the need for backward compatibility. This attack is a threat because it allows the attacker to exploit known vulnerabilities in the weaker, outdated security protocols.

6. Compare and contrast data at rest and data in transit, including the primary strategy for protecting each.

Data at rest is data stored on a device like a hard drive or server, where the primary threat is unauthorized physical access. This data is best protected by full disk encryption (FDE). Data in transit is data actively moving across a network, where the main threat is interception. This is protected by transport encryption protocols like TLS, which create a secure, encrypted tunnel for the data.

7. What is a Trusted Execution Environment (TEE), and what role does a Trusted Platform Module (TPM) play?

A Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) is a secure cryptoprocessor, an internal motherboard chip that cannot be tampered with, which protects the confidentiality and integrity of code and data stored on it. The Trusted Platform Module (TPM) is an international standard for such a cryptoprocessor. A TPM provides cryptographic services like random number generation, key generation, and can test system components at boot to ensure they haven't been altered.

8. Explain the concept of a collision attack and how the "birthday paradox" relates to it.

A collision attack is an attempt to find two different input strings that produce the exact same hash result, which would undermine data integrity. The "birthday paradox" is a statistical phenomenon that makes this attack easier than a brute-force approach. It states that it is much easier to find any two people in a group who share a birthday than it is to find someone who shares your specific birthday, which translates to it being easier to find any two inputs that collide than to find an input that collides with a specific hash.

9. What is a blockchain, and what cryptographic algorithm is fundamental to its operation?

A blockchain is a shared, immutable, distributed ledger used to record transactions and track assets across a peer-to-peer network of computers. It is designed so that transactions can be recorded only once and cannot be altered without the consensus of all parties. Blockchain relies heavily on cryptographic hash algorithms, most notably SHA-256, to securely record its transactions.

10. Identify two key limitations that make it difficult to implement strong cryptography on low-power devices.

Implementing strong cryptography on low-power devices like sensors is difficult due to the "resource versus security constraint." First, cryptographic algorithms require significant computational power and energy to perform their calculations, both of which are in short supply on these devices. Second, many applications for these devices require extremely fast response times, but the computational overhead of strong encryption can introduce unacceptable delays or latency.

Glossary of Key Terms

Algorithm

A set of procedures, typically based on a mathematical formula, used in cryptography to encrypt or decrypt data.

Asymmetric Cryptographic Algorithm

Also known as public key cryptography, it uses a pair of mathematically related keys (a public key and a private key) for encryption and decryption.

Authentication

The process of verifying the identity of a sender, receiver, or user. Cryptography can provide proof of the genuineness of the user.

Birthday Attack

A type of cryptographic attack based on the statistical probability of finding a collision in a hash function. It exploits the "birthday paradox," which shows it's easier to find any two inputs that produce the same hash than to find an input that matches a specific hash.

Blockchain

A shared, immutable, distributed ledger that facilitates recording transactions and tracking assets in a business network, relying on cryptographic hashing to ensure integrity.

Block Cipher

A cryptographic algorithm that manipulates an entire block of plaintext (e.g., 8 to 16 bytes) at one time. It is considered more secure than a stream cipher.

Ciphertext

The scrambled, unreadable, and encrypted output of a cryptographic algorithm.

Cleartext

Unencrypted data that is not intended to be encrypted.

Collision

An event where two different input strings produce the same hash result (digest) from a hash function.

Confidentiality

A core principle of information security ensuring that information is kept secret and private, preventing unauthorized access.

Cryptography

The science and practice of protecting information by transforming it into an unreadable format (ciphertext) to ensure secure communication in the presence of adversaries.

Decryption

The process of changing scrambled ciphertext back into its original, readable plaintext form using a cryptographic algorithm and key.

Digital Signature

An electronic verification of a sender, created by encrypting a message's hash digest with the sender's private key. It ensures authenticity, integrity, and non-repudiation.

Downgrade Attack

An attack where an adversary forces a system to abandon a modern, high-security mode of operation and fall back to an older, less secure mode, which can then be exploited.

Encryption

The process of changing original plaintext into scrambled ciphertext using a cryptographic algorithm and a key.

Full Disk Encryption (FDE)

A method of protecting all data on an entire hard drive, including the operating system and temporary files, by encrypting the whole disk.

Hardware Security Module (HSM)

A removable external cryptographic device that includes an onboard random number generator and key storage, performing all cryptographic operations in hardware to protect against software-based attacks.

Hashing

The process of using a one-way hash algorithm to create a unique, fixed-size "digital fingerprint" (a digest or hash) of a set of data, primarily used to ensure data integrity.

Integrity

A core principle of information security ensuring that data is trustworthy and has not been altered, deleted, or corrupted in an unauthorized manner.

Key

A mathematical value entered into a cryptographic algorithm to produce ciphertext (for encryption) or plaintext (for decryption).

Key Exchange

The process of securely sharing cryptographic keys between two parties. Methods include Diffie-Hellman (DH) and Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH).

Non-Repudiation

The process of proving that a user performed a specific action, ensuring that they cannot later deny having sent or received a message.

Obfuscation

The action of making something obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. Encryption is a form of obfuscation based on a secret key.

Plaintext

The original, readable, and unencrypted data that is either the input for encryption or the output of decryption.

Post-Quantum Cryptography

Cryptographic algorithms that are designed to be secure against attacks from a quantum computer.

Private Key

In asymmetric cryptography, the key that is kept secret by its owner and is used to decrypt messages encrypted with the corresponding public key.

Public Key

In asymmetric cryptography, the key that can be freely distributed to anyone and is used to encrypt messages intended for the owner of the corresponding private key.

Quantum Computing

A type of computing that uses quantum-physical phenomena, such as qubits that can be both 0 and 1 simultaneously, to perform calculations much faster than classical computers.

Steganography

The practice of hiding the existence of a message or information within a seemingly harmless file or object.

Stream Cipher

A cryptographic algorithm that encrypts data one character at a time. It is generally considered less secure than a block cipher.

Symmetric Cryptographic Algorithm

Also known as private key cryptography, it uses a single, shared secret key for both encrypting and decrypting data.

Trusted Platform Module (TPM)

An international standard for a secure cryptoprocessor (a chip on a motherboard) that provides cryptographic services and can ensure system integrity at boot.
