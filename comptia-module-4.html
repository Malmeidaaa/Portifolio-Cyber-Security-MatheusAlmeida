I. Introduction and Foundations of Cryptography

Cryptography is crucial for data protection. Although most end-users only utilize what occurs by default, encrypting and decrypting data is considered essential, and often required by regulations for enterprises. Corporate cryptography involves high complexity, especially concerning key management (e.g., how to retrieve Alice's encrypted data if she is suddenly ill?).

The objectives of Module 4, Advanced Cryptography, are to:

  1. Define digital certificates.
  2. Describe the components of Public Key Infrastructure (PKI).
  3. List the secure communication and transport protocols.
  4. Explain how to implement cryptography.

The #TrendingCyber Scenario: The Strength of Cryptography

The strength of cryptographic security is exemplified by a user ("The Guy" or TG) who forgot the key to his Bitcoin wallet, worth over $300,000. TG had stored the password to his Bitcoin wallet in a password-protected zip file but forgot the zip password, forcing him to seek help to break the file encryption.

Mike S., a security professional, and his colleague Nash were hired to crack the zip encryption. Mike knew he could not break into the Bitcoin wallet itself, as it is too well protected by cryptography. However, TG used zip's proprietary encryption algorithm instead of the widely used Advanced Encryption Standard (AES). Using the time stamp from TG's old laptop, Mike could narrow down the search space from 10 sextillion (1 followed by 21 zeros) down to "only" 10 quintillion (1 followed by 18 zeros).

Mike and Nash used a network of personal computers with Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) to accelerate the attack. Although they initially failed after 10 days, correcting an error in the GPU processing allowed them to find the correct key within one day. Since then, Mike has received numerous requests, including one concerning $4 million in Bitcoin, although he stated breaking the Bitcoin wallet directly is exceedingly difficult.

Core Pillars of Data Protection (The CIA Triad)

Using appropriate cryptographic solutions is fundamental to modern security. Cryptography provides the essential tools to achieve the following goals, often referred to as the "CIA Triad":

Pillar Security Goal Explanation and Primary Tool Example
Confidentiality (Secrecy) ü§´ Keeping information secret from unauthorized parties. Encryption scrambles plaintext into unreadable ciphertext. Only someone with the correct key can unscramble it. Encryption (like TLS/SSL) protects credit card information sent to an online store from hackers who intercept the data.
Integrity (Trustworthiness) ‚úÖ Ensuring data has not been altered or tampered with in transit. Hashing algorithms (like SHA-256) create a unique, fixed-size "fingerprint" of the data. Any change in data instantly changes the hash. Calculating the hash value of a downloaded software file to ensure it is the authentic, unaltered version.
Authentication & Non-Repudiation (Proof of Origin) ‚úç Verification of the sender‚Äôs identity (Authentication) and proof that the sender truly sent the message, preventing later denial (Non-Repudiation). Digital signatures utilize asymmetric cryptography to accomplish this. A digital signature on an email or contract verifies the sender and confirms the content has not been changed since signing.

The Importance of Using Appropriate Cryptographic Solutions

Simply using cryptography is insufficient; the solution must be appropriate for the task.

  1. Right Tool for the Job: You must use the correct tool for the specific objective. Encryption should be used for secrecy, hashing for integrity, and digital signatures for authenticity. Hashing algorithms are one-way processes and cannot be used for confidentiality, as the original data cannot be recovered.
  2. Strength vs. Performance: The cryptographic strength must align with the data sensitivity and device capabilities. Using weak, outdated encryption (like DES) for financial records is dangerously insecure, while using military-grade encryption on a low-power smart lightbulb would be slow and drain resources.
  3. Correct Implementation: A strong cryptographic algorithm can be rendered useless by poor implementation, such as using predictable keys or insecure modes of operation. Security experts advise: "Don't roll your own crypto".

II. Digital Certificates

Digital certificates are one of the most common applications of public key cryptography.

Defining Digital Certificates

Asymmetric cryptography uses public and private keys; the public key is shared, and the private key is confidential. While asymmetric cryptography protects confidentiality, determining the sender's true identity is challenging. A digital signature, created by encrypting a hash digest with the private key, provides a degree of proof by verifying the owner of the private key. However, this is vulnerable to an imposter attack.

The Imposter Public Key Problem:
Mallory could intercept a message from Bob, create her own set of keys using Bob‚Äôs identity, and send a new message with her imposter digital signature. Alice would retrieve Mallory's imposter public key, believing it belongs to Bob, and be tricked.

The Solution:
The solution requires a trusted third party (similar to a government issuing a passport) to verify Bob's identity.

A digital certificate is the technology used to associate a user‚Äôs identity to a public key and has been digitally signed by a trusted third party. This third party verifies the owner and confirms the public key belongs to that owner.

A digital certificate is essentially a verified container for a public key. It typically contains the owner's name/alias, the owner's public key, the name and digital signature of the issuer, the serial number, and the expiration date. Digital certificates can authenticate users, websites, organizations, devices, servers, emails, and applications.

Managing Digital Certificates

Effective management involves Certificate Authorities (CAs) and tools for controlling the certificate lifecycle.

Certificate Authorities (CAs)

The formal process for obtaining a digital certificate is known as Certificate Signing Request (CSR) generation.

  1. The user first generates their public and private keys.
  2. The user completes a request (CSR) with necessary information (name, email) and electronically signs it by affixing their public key.
  3. The request is sent to a Registration Authority (RA), which is responsible for verifying the credentials and authenticity of the applicant.
  4. Once verified, the request is transferred to an Intermediate Certificate Authority (Intermediate CA). Intermediate CAs are subordinate entities that process requests and issue digital certificates on behalf of the main Certificate Authority (CA), which is the party responsible for the certificates.

Applicant Authentication Methods used by the RA include:

  • Email: The simplest form, sufficient for basic email communication, but insufficient for higher security verifications.
  • Documents: Requiring specific documentation such as a birth certificate or an employee badge with a photograph.
  • In person: Requiring the applicant to apply in person and provide a government-issued passport or driver‚Äôs license to prove existence and identity.

Certificate Management and Revocation

Strong certificate management includes a Certificate Repository (CR) and methods for revocation. A CR is a publicly accessible centralized directory of digital certificates used to view the status of a certificate.

Digital certificates can be revoked before their expiration date for benign reasons (e.g., details changed) or dangerous circumstances (e.g., theft of a private key or theft of digital certificates from a CA). CAs must publish lists of revoked certificates in a timely fashion.

Two means exist to check the revocation status:

  1. Certificate Revocation List (CRL): A list of certificate serial numbers that have been revoked. Many CAs maintain an online CRL, and local devices maintain a local CRL updated via OS or browser software.
  2. Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP): A "request-response" protocol that performs a real-time lookup of a certificate‚Äôs status. The web browser sends information to a trusted entity, the OCSP Responder, which provides immediate revocation information.

A variation is OCSP stapling. To avoid high traffic volume from real-time lookups by every client, web servers send queries to the OCSP Responder at regular intervals to receive a signed, time-stamped response. During the client handshake, the server includes (staples) this previously received OCSP response, which the browser evaluates.

Types of Digital Certificates

Digital certificates are grouped into root certificates, domain certificates, and hardware and software certificates.

1. Root Digital Certificates and Certificate Chaining

Digital certificate verification and organization rely on certificate chaining. This creates a path between the few trusted "root" CAs and the many intermediate CAs. Trust is traced back to the highest level of CA, known as the root of trust.

  • Root Digital Certificate: The beginning point of the chain. It is created and verified by a CA and is self-signed because it does not rely on any higher-level authority for authentication.
  • Intermediate Certificates: Issued by Intermediate CAs, these link the root certificate to the user digital certificate, which is the endpoint of the chain.

Approved root digital certificates and intermediate certificates are distributed in three primary ways:

  1. Through updates to the Operating System (OS).
  2. Through updates to the web browser.
  3. Through pinning, where the digital certificate is hard-coded (pinned) within the application or program using it.

Root CA Security: Protecting the integrity of the root CA is essential because a breach could taint all intermediate CAs and issued certificates. A common method is to keep the root CA offline (offline CA) and powered down, only bringing it online for specific, infrequent tasks like issuing certificates that authorize intermediate CAs.

2. Domain Digital Certificates

Most digital certificates are web server digital certificates. They serve two primary functions: ensuring the web server's authenticity to the client and ensuring the authenticity of the cryptographic connection.

The secure connection is established through a key exchange (or handshake).

  1. The browser sends a ClientHello message listing supported cryptographic algorithms.
  2. The server responds with a ServerHello, indicating the chosen algorithm and sending its digital certificate.
  3. The browser verifies the certificate, extracts the server's public key, generates a random value (the pre-master secret), encrypts it with the public key, and sends it back (ClientKeyExchange).
  4. The server decrypts the message to obtain the pre-master secret. Both the server and browser then create the identical master secret, which is used to generate session keys (symmetric keys) for encrypting and decrypting information exchanged during the session.

Types of Domain Digital Certificates:

  • Domain Validation (DV): Entry-level certificates that authenticate that an organization has the right to use a specific domain name, usually by verifying control over the domain. They indicate nothing about the trustworthiness of the individuals behind the site.
  • Extended Validation (EV): Requires more extensive verification of the legitimacy of the business by the Intermediate CA. This includes verifying the legal existence, physical address, and operational presence of the website owner.
  • Wildcard Digital Certificate: Used to validate a main domain along with all subdomains (e.g., *.example.com covers www.example.com and mail.example.com).
  • Subject Alternative Name (SAN): Addresses the limitation of wildcard certificates by allowing different values (additional host names, common names) to be associated with a single certificate. A SAN certificate also permits covering multiple IP addresses.

3. Hardware and Software Digital Certificates

  • Machine/Computer Digital Certificate: Used to verify the identity of a device (e.g., a printer) in a network transaction. Many network devices can create their own self-signed machine digital certificates.
  • Code Signing Digital Certificate: Used by software developers to digitally sign a program, proving the software comes from the signed entity and has not been altered.
  • Email Digital Certificate: Allows a user to digitally sign and encrypt email messages. They can also authenticate the authors of documents like Microsoft Word or Adobe PDF files.

Digital Certificate Attributes and Formats

The standard format for digital certificates is X.509 Version 3. X.509 certificates contain several required attributes used for secure connection negotiation:

  • Certificate validity period.
  • End-host identity information.
  • Encryption keys that will be used.
  • Signature of the issuing CA.
  • Common Name (CN): The name of the device protected by the certificate (e.g., www.example.com or *.example.com).

III. Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

PKI addresses the challenge of managing digital certificates and asymmetric cryptography "at scale" (e.g., in an enterprise with thousands of users).

What Is Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)?

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) is the underlying infrastructure that serves as a key management system for controlling public keys, private keys, and digital certificates across large user populations.

PKI is defined as the set of software, hardware, processes, procedures, and policies needed to create, manage, distribute, use, store, and revoke digital certificates. The goal of PKI is to establish the identity of people, devices, and services to control access, protect data, and provide accountability. PKI is digital certificate management at scale.

Trust Models

Trust, defined as reliance on another person or entity, is a principal foundation of PKI. A trust model defines the type of relationship that exists.

  1. Direct Trust: A relationship between two individuals because one knows the other.
  2. Third-party Trust: Individuals trust each other because they both trust a common third party (e.g., trusting a judge in a courtroom; or trusting a CA in PKI).
  3. Web of Trust: A less secure model based on direct trust where users sign and exchange certificates with all other users, relying on mutual trust.

PKI models using a CA:

PKI Trust Model Description Limitations and Benefits
Hierarchical Trust Model Assigns a single hierarchy with one master CA, called the root, which signs all digital certificate authorities with a single key. Limitation: High risk; if the root's single private key is compromised, all digital certificates become worthless. Can create a significant backlog.
Distributed Trust Model Features multiple CAs that sign digital certificates. Benefit: Eliminates single point of failure; loss of one key only compromises certificates signed by that CA; distributes the workload. This is the basis for most certificates on the Internet.
Bridge Trust Model Similar to distributed, but one Bridge CA acts as a facilitator to interconnect all other CAs (hierarchical or distributed models). The Bridge CA does not issue certificates but acts as a hub, allowing different models to be linked together.

Managing PKI

PKI management involves establishing formal policies and practices.

  • Certificate Policy (CP): A published set of rules governing PKI operation, providing recommended baseline security requirements for the use of CAs, intermediate CAs, and other PKI components.
  • Certificate Practice Statement (CPS): A more technical document detailing how the CA uses and manages certificates, covering specifics like user registration, issuance, revocation, and private key protection. X.509 certificates often contain a field linking to the associated CP.

Certificate Life Cycle

A certificate's life cycle is typically divided into four stages:

  1. Creation: The certificate is issued after the user's identity is positively verified, and the CA applies its signing key.
  2. Suspension: The certificate‚Äôs validity is temporarily suspended (e.g., if an employee is on leave). A suspended key can be later reinstated.
  3. Revocation: The certificate is permanently invalidated before its normal expiration date (e.g., due to a lost or compromised private key). The CA updates its CRL. Revoked keys cannot be reinstated.
  4. Expiration: The certificate can no longer be used after its set expiration date, requiring the user to obtain a new certificate.

Key Management

Improper certificate and key management is a common vulnerability. Since keys are the foundation of PKI, they require careful management.

Key Storage and Usage

  • Public Keys: Stored by embedding them within digital certificates.
  • Private Keys: Can be stored on the user‚Äôs local system (software-based storage), which is vulnerable to OS attacks, or more securely on smart cards or tokens (hardware storage). Keys must never be shared in plaintext, copies should not be made, and expired keys must be destroyed.
  • Key Usage: Dual key pairs can be created for enhanced security: one pair for encryption (where the public key can be backed up) and a second pair used only for digital signatures (where the public key is never backed up).

Key Handling Procedures

Key management procedures include:

  • Escrow: Key escrow is the process where keys are managed by a third party (like a trusted CA). The private key is split, encrypted, and stored in separate locations, allowing the user to retrieve and combine the halves for decryption if needed.
  • Expiration/Cryptoperiod: Keys must have an expiration date (cryptoperiod) to prevent an attacker from decrypting messages indefinitely if the key is stolen.
  • Renewal: An existing key can be renewed instead of expiring, but continuous renewal makes them more vulnerable.
  • Revocation: Permanent invalidation of a key before expiration (e.g., employee termination). The CA must be immediately notified.
  • Recovery: Techniques for recovering lost or damaged keys.
    • Key Recovery Agent (KRA): A highly trusted person designated to recover lost digital certificates.
    • M-of-N Control: The encrypted private key is divided into N parts and distributed. A smaller subset, the M group, must meet and agree to piece the key together for recovery. The distribution to multiple users (N group) ensures key recovery is possible even if one member is absent.
  • Destruction: Removal of all private and public keys along with the user‚Äôs identification information from the CA. Information from revoked or expired keys remains for audit purposes, but destruction removes it.

IV. Secure Communication and Transport Protocols

Cryptographic algorithms are used to protect data in transit, which is known as transport/communication encryption. These protocols often rely on tunneling.

Tunneling and Encapsulation

Protocols typically rely on "encapsulating" or enveloping the data to be transmitted inside something else.

  • Encapsulation is the process of enclosing the encrypted data with an additional, unencrypted header. This is essential because if the entire packet were encrypted, network routers could not read the header and forward the packet to its destination.
  • Tunneling refers to the entire process of encapsulation, transmission, and "de-encapsulation" to transport data securely across a network.

Primary Secure Protocols: TLS and IPSec

The two primary protocols utilizing tunneling are Transport Layer Security (TLS) and IP Security (IPSec).

1. Transport Layer Security (TLS)

  • Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) was an early protocol developed by Netscape, but due to vulnerabilities, it is deprecated and has been replaced by Transport Layer Security (TLS).
  • The current version, TLS v1.3, provides a higher degree of protection. It removes support for older hashing algorithms (MD5 and SHA-224), requires Perfect Forward Secrecy for key exchange, and encrypts handshake messages after the ServerHello exchange.
  • A cipher suite is a named combination of the encryption, authentication, and Message Authentication Code (MAC) algorithms used with TLS, negotiated during the initial handshake.

2. IP Security (IPSec)

Internet Protocol Security (IPSec) is a comprehensive suite of protocols for securing Internet Protocol (IP) communications. IPSec encrypts and authenticates each IP packet of a session between devices.

IPSec is a transparent security protocol‚Äîit is transparent to applications (which do not need modification), users (who do not need special training), and local software.

IPSec Protection Areas (Protocols):

  • Authentication: Achieved by the Authentication Header (AH) protocol, ensuring packets were sent from the source.
  • Confidentiality: Achieved by the Encapsulating Security Payload (ESP) protocol, which supports authentication and data encryption.
  • Key Management: Achieved by Internet Security Association and Key Management Protocol/Oakley (ISAKMP/Oakley), which generates keys and authenticates the user using techniques like digital certificates.

IPSec Encryption Modes:

  • Transport Mode: Encrypts only the data portion (payload) of each packet, leaving the header unencrypted. Used when a device needs to see the source/destination addresses for routing.
  • Tunnel Mode: Encrypts both the header and the data portion. The original packet is treated as the payload of a new packet with added headers. Generally used for network-to-network communication.

IPSec Architectures and Implementation:

  • Devices: Installing IPSec on all devices provides the highest "end-to-end" security, although it requires more work on large networks. Alternatively, installing it only on routers secures traffic outside the enterprise.
  • Integration into TCP/IP: IPv6 has integrated IPSec features, making it the best option. IPv4 requires changes to the IP implementation on each device.
  • Architectures:
    • Bump in the Stack (BITS): IPSec acts as a separate architectural layer between the IP layer and the Data Link layer, intercepting data as it moves down the protocol stack. BITS adds IPSec to legacy hosts.
    • Bump in the Wire (BITW): Adds a hardware device that provides IPSec services by intercepting outgoing data and adding protection. BITW adds IPSec to legacy network equipment.

Other Secure Protocols

  • Hypertext Transport Protocol Secure (HTTPS): The secure version of HTTP, sent over TLS (or SSL). HTTPS uses port 443 instead of HTTP's port 80 and is indicated by https://.
  • Secure Shell (SSH): An encrypted alternative to the Telnet protocol. It is a Linux/UNIX-based command interface used for securely accessing a remote computer. Both the client and server are authenticated using a digital certificate.
  • Secure/Multipurpose Internet Mail Extensions (S/MIME): A protocol for securing email messages. It allows users to send messages that are both encrypted and digitally signed.
  • Secure Real-time Transport Protocol (SRTP): A secure extension that protects transmissions using the Real-Time Transport Protocol (RTP). It provides security features like message authentication and confidentiality for Voice over IP (VoIP) communications.

V. Implementing Cryptography

Correct implementation of cryptography is necessary to prevent vulnerabilities. Implementation involves understanding key strength, secret algorithms, and block cipher modes of operation.

Key Strength

A cryptographic key is a value used as input to an algorithm to transform plaintext into ciphertext. Three primary characteristics determine the key's resiliency to attacks (key strength):

  1. Randomness: The key must be random with no predictable pattern to thwart attacker attempts to uncover it.
  2. Cryptoperiod: The length of time for which a key is authorized for use. A limited cryptoperiod protects the ciphertext from extended cryptanalysis and limits exposure time if compromised.
  3. Key Length: Shorter keys are easier to break. The key space is all possible values for a specific key. The formula for determining key space in symmetric algorithms is character-setkey-length. Increasing key length significantly impacts security.

Secret Algorithms

While keys (except public keys) must be kept secret, the same does not apply to algorithms. Historically, attempts to keep algorithms secret have failed. For cryptography to be useful, it needs to be widespread, making secrecy difficult.

The Kerckhoffs‚Äôs Principles (1883) state that systems should not require secrecy. Today, this principle is applied by separating algorithms from keys: algorithms are public, while keys are private.

Block Cipher Modes of Operation

A block cipher manipulates an entire block of plaintext at one time. The plaintext is divided into blocks of specific lengths, and each block is encrypted independently. A block cipher mode of operation specifies how these blocks should be handled.

Common modes include:

  • Electronic Code Book (ECB): The most basic mode; each block is encrypted separately. If two plaintext blocks are identical, they are encrypted into identical ciphertext blocks. This repetition is a vulnerability, and ECB is not considered suitable for use.
  • Cipher Block Chaining (CBC): A common mode where each ciphertext block is "fed back" and XORed with the next plaintext block before being encrypted. This makes the ciphertext dependent on the previous block, making it much harder to break than ECB.
  • Counter (CTR): Requires both the sender and receiver to access a counter that computes a new value for each ciphertext block exchange. The weakness lies in requiring a synchronous counter.
  • Galois/Counter (GCM): This mode both encrypts the plaintext and computes a Message Authentication Code (MAC), providing both privacy (encryption) and integrity (MAC). It uses a counter and includes Additional Authentication Data (AAD) in the transmission.

Study Guide for Advanced Cryptography

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

1. What fundamental weakness of a digital signature does a digital certificate address?

A digital signature can only prove that the sender's private key was used to encrypt the signature digest, but it cannot definitively confirm the true identity of the sender. An imposter could create their own key pair under the sender's name and trick a recipient. A digital certificate solves this by having a trusted third party verify the owner's identity and digitally sign their public key, associating the identity with the key.

2. Describe the process of a Certificate Signing Request (CSR) generation.

A user who wants a digital certificate first creates a public and private key pair. They then complete a request, affix their public key, and send it to a Registration Authority (RA) to verify their credentials. Once verified, the request is transferred to an Intermediate Certificate Authority (CA), which processes the request and issues the digital certificate.

3. Explain the difference between a Certificate Revocation List (CRL) and the Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP).

A CRL is a list of certificate serial numbers that have been revoked, which is maintained by a CA and can be queried or pushed to devices via updates. In contrast, OCSP is a request-response protocol that performs a real-time lookup of a specific certificate's status by sending a query to a trusted OCSP Responder.

4. What is certificate chaining and what is the role of a root digital certificate in this process?

Certificate chaining is the process of linking several certificates together to establish trust. The chain begins with a root digital certificate, which is created, verified, and self-signed by a high-level Certificate Authority (CA). The trust flows from this root certificate through one or more intermediate certificates down to the end-user's certificate, establishing a verifiable "root of trust."

5. What is Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) and what is its primary goal?

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) is the comprehensive framework of software, hardware, policies, and procedures needed to manage digital certificates and public/private keys on a large scale. Its primary goal is to establish the identity of people, devices, and services in order to control access to resources, protect data, and provide accountability.

6. Briefly describe the hierarchical trust model for PKI and list one of its limitations.

The hierarchical trust model assigns a single master Certificate Authority (CA), called the root, which signs all digital certificates for an organization with a single key. A major limitation is that if this single CA's private key were compromised, all digital certificates it issued would become worthless. It can also create a significant backlog, as a single CA must verify and sign all certificates.

7. Explain the concept of key escrow as a key handling procedure.

Key escrow is a key management process where keys are managed by a trusted third party. The private key is split into parts, and each part is encrypted and stored separately by the third party. This allows an authorized user to retrieve the parts, combine them, and recover the key if it is lost, relieving the end-user from the risk of permanent key loss.

8. What is the primary difference between IPSec's transport mode and tunnel mode?

IPSec's transport mode encrypts only the data portion (payload) of each IP packet, leaving the header unencrypted, which is typically used when a device needs to see routing information. The more secure tunnel mode encrypts both the header and the data portion by encapsulating the entire original packet inside a new packet, a method generally used for network-to-network communication.

9. List and briefly define the three primary characteristics that determine a key's strength.

The three characteristics of key strength are randomness, ensuring the key has no predictable pattern; cryptoperiod, the authorized length of time a key can be used; and key length, as longer keys create a larger key space and are harder to break through brute-force attacks.

10. What is the main vulnerability of the Electronic Code Book (ECB) block cipher mode of operation?

The main vulnerability of the ECB mode is that identical plaintext blocks are encrypted into identical ciphertext blocks. An attacker can exploit this repetition to analyze patterns, modify the message by reshuffling blocks, or substitute one encrypted block for another.

Cipher Suite A named combination of encryption, authentication, and message authentication code (MAC) algorithms used with protocols like TLS during the initial connection handshake.
Code Signing The use of a digital certificate by a software developer to digitally sign a program. This proves the software's origin and that it has not been altered or compromised.
Cryptoperiod The length of time for which a cryptographic key is authorized for use. Limiting the cryptoperiod helps protect against extended cryptanalysis if a key is compromised.
Digital Certificate A technology used to associate a user‚Äôs identity to a public key. It is a container for the public key that has been digitally signed by a trusted third party, verifying the owner's identity.
Digital Signature An electronic verification of a sender. It is created by generating a digest (hash) of a document and then encrypting that digest with the sender's private key.
Extended Validation (EV) Certificate A type of domain digital certificate that requires extensive verification of the legitimacy of the business, including its legal existence, physical address, and operational presence.
Internet Protocol Security (IPSec) A suite of protocols for securing Internet Protocol (IP) communications by encrypting and authenticating each IP packet of a session. It operates at OSI Layer 3.
Key Escrow A process in which keys are managed by a trusted third party. The private key is split, and each half is encrypted and stored separately, allowing for recovery by the user if the key is lost.
Key Management System The framework and services for controlling public keys, private keys, and digital certificates, often as part of a larger PKI.
M-of-N Control A key recovery technique where a private key is encrypted, divided into N parts, and distributed among a group of individuals (the N group). A smaller subset (the M group) must agree to combine their parts to recover the key.
Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP) A "request-response" protocol that performs a real-time lookup of a certificate‚Äôs status by sending its information to a trusted entity called an OCSP Responder.
OCSP Stapling A variation of OCSP where a web server queries the OCSP Responder at regular intervals and receives a signed, time-stamped response. The server then "staples" this response to the TLS/SSL handshake, improving efficiency.
Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) The set of hardware, software, processes, policies, and procedures needed to create, manage, distribute, use, store, and revoke digital certificates across large populations. It is essentially digital certificate management at scale.
Root of Trust The concept that the trust of a certificate can be traced back to the highest level of CA. This is established through certificate chaining starting with a root digital certificate.
Self-Signed A characteristic of a root digital certificate, which is signed by its own creator (the CA) because there is no higher-level authority to verify it.
Subject Alternative Name (SAN) Certificate A certificate that allows multiple host names (sites, IP addresses, common names) to be protected by a single digital certificate.
Third Party In cryptography, a trusted entity, different from the sender or receiver, that verifies the identity of an individual and their public key. Certificate Authorities are trusted third parties.
Transport Layer Security (TLS) A secure communication and transport protocol that is the modern replacement for SSL. It is used to create an encrypted data path between a client and a server, such as in HTTPS.
Transport/Communication Encryption The use of cryptographic algorithms to protect data while it is in transit across a network.
Tunneling A technique for transporting data securely across a network. It involves encapsulating encrypted data with an additional, unencrypted header so that it can be routed through a public network while its contents remain protected.
Wildcard Digital Certificate A certificate used to validate a main domain along with all of its first-level subdomains (e.g., *.example.com would cover www.example.com and mail.example.com).
X.509 The standard format for digital certificates, which specifies the attributes (fields) that a certificate must contain, such as validity period, issuer signature, and common name.
