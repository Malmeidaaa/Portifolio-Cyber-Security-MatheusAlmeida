Incident Preparation and Investigation

The ability of an organization to maintain its operations and services in the face of disruptive events such as natural disasters (hurricanes, floods, earthquakes), human-made disasters (sabotage, industrial mishaps, chemical spills, transportation accidents), and successful cyberattacks is crucial for its survival. Unfortunately, preparation for such events is often severely lacking, as more than half of small businesses reportedly have no plan to react to a cyberattack. This preparation necessitates two major steps: advanced preparation and post-incident investigation to learn from mistakes and address vulnerabilities.

I. Preparation for a Cyber Incident

Preparing for a cyber event is absolutely essential and primarily involves business continuity planning and incident response planning.

A. Business Continuity Planning (BCP)

Business continuity is defined as the ability of an organization to maintain its operations and services despite a disruptive event or major disaster. Business continuity planning is the process an organization undertakes in advance to determine a plan of action to protect itself in the event of a disaster.

The outcome of this planning is a Business Continuity Plan (BCP), a strategic document that provides alternative modes of operation for business activities that, if interrupted, could result in a significant loss to the enterprise. The BCP process involves identifying exposure to threats, creating preventive and recovery procedures, and then testing those procedures.

A BCP is designed to ensure an organization can achieve continuity of operations and is fundamentally recovery planning. A BCP should include the following elements:

  • High availability (HA): The ability to withstand all outages (planned and unplanned, environmental and internal) while providing continuous processing for critical applications.
  • Scalability: The capability to cover increased capacity as organizations grow and expand.
  • Diversity: Inclusion of different technologies, third-party vendors, controls, and cryptographic solutions.
  • On-prem and cloud: Flexibility to address resources moved from on-premises to the cloud without needing frequent rewrites of the plan.

Related to BCP is the federal initiative known as Continuity of Operation Planning (COOP), which encourages organizations to address how critical operations will continue under a broad range of negative circumstances using an "all-hazards approach".

Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

A crucial tool for creating a BCP is the Business Impact Analysis (BIA). A BIA identifies business processes and functions and then quantifies the impact a loss of these functions may have on business operations, covering impacts on property, finance, safety, reputation, and life.

The BIA is designed to identify critically important processes:

  • Mission-essential function: The core purpose of the enterprise (e.g., "Deliver healthcare services to individuals and their families" for a hospital).
  • Critical systems: Systems that support the mission-essential function (e.g., maintaining an emergency room facility).
  • Single point of failure: A component or entity in a system that, if it fails, will disable the entire system. Minimizing these single failure points results in a system that can function for an extended period with little downtime.

A BIA also often contains a privacy impact assessment to identify and mitigate privacy risks, including an examination of what Personally Identifiable Information (PII) is being collected and how it is protected. Availability, which is often expressed as a percentage of uptime in a year, is listed below:

Percentage Availability Corresponding Downtime
99.9% (Three Nines) 8.76 hours/year
99.99% (Four Nines) 52.6 minutes/year
99.999% (Five Nines) 5.26 minutes/year
Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)

The Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) is a subset of the BCP that focuses specifically on restoring IT functions and services following an event that causes a significant disruption. The DRP is a detailed, written document that must be updated regularly.

A common topic covered in a DRP is the restoration order—the sequence in which different systems are reinstated. Factors determining this order include:

  1. Dependencies: Systems that rely on others (e.g., the network must be restored before applications that rely on it).
  2. Importance of processes: Critical systems supporting the mission-essential function or requiring high availability.
  3. Alternative business practices: How long a manual workaround process can temporarily substitute for normal business activities without causing major problems.

B. Incident Response Planning

Since successful information security attacks are inevitable, advanced preparation is necessary. An incident response plan is a set of written instructions for reacting to a security incident. This plan prevents enterprises from being unable to quickly identify the attack, contain its spread, recover, and learn from the attack.

The elements of the incident response plan are the action steps to be taken when an incident occurs, known as the incident response process.

Phase Action Step Definition
Preparation Advanced planning for a cyber event
Detection Quickly identifying an attack
Containment Stopping the spread of an attack
Eradication Eliminating the cause of an incident
Recovery Recovering from an attack
Lessons Learned Performing detailed analysis to increase security and improve future response efforts

An incident response plan must also define:

  • Definitions: Clear descriptions of the types and categories of documented incident definitions, outlining what is and is not an incident requiring a response.
  • Incident response teams: Teams responsible for responding to security incidents, including technical specialists, public relations employees, and managers.
  • Reporting requirements: Instructions indicating to whom information should be distributed and when the security event has escalated to require specific actions (Stakeholder management).
Performing Testing Exercises

It is important to test an incident response plan by conducting simulated testing exercises to make necessary adjustments.

Testing Exercise Type Description
Tabletop An exercise where a scenario is discussed, often informally for about 30 minutes
Walkthrough A simulated testing exercise
Simulation A type of simulated testing exercise for the plan
Fail over A type of simulated testing exercise
Parallel Processing A type of simulated testing exercise
Studying Attack Frameworks

Organizations can study how attacks occur using exploitation frameworks, which serve as models of the thinking and actions of threat actors.

  • MITRE ATT&CK: A knowledge base of categorized attacker techniques focused on how attackers interact with systems, arranged into tactics.
  • The Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis: A framework for examining network intrusion events based on four core interconnected elements: adversary, infrastructure, capability, and victim.
  • Cyber Kill Chain: Outlines the systematic, end-to-end steps of an attack, illustrating that disrupting any one step (especially the early steps) will interrupt the entire attack process.

II. Resilience Through Redundancy

Resilience, or elasticity and flexibility, provided by equipment redundancy can aid in incident preparation and recovery during a cyberattack. Redundancy in information security typically involves duplicated servers, drives, networks, power, sites, clouds, and data. The goal of redundancy is to reduce the mean time to recovery.

A. Capacity Planning

Capacity planning is the process of forecasting the need for future resources by analyzing current maximum capacity against future requirements. This planning aims to prevent both overprovisioning (having too many underutilized resources) and underprovisioning (having too few resources).

Capacity planning involves calculating:

  1. People capacity planning: Calculating future human resources (employees) needed.
  2. Technology capacity planning: Predicting the number of devices needed.
  3. Infrastructure capacity planning: Predicting the size of the network needed.

Intentional excess capacity includes platform diversity, which is the use of multiple different devices to host or serve an application or service.

B. Servers

Since servers are critical to network infrastructure, they require high availability. Server redundancy often incorporates multiple servers that appear to users and applications as a single computing resource.

  • Clustering: The combination of two or more devices (servers) to appear as a single unit. A server cluster is interconnected via a public cluster connection (clients see them as one) and a private cluster connection (servers exchange data).
  • Asymmetric server cluster: A standby server exists solely to take over if another server fails; the standby performs no other useful work.
  • Symmetric server cluster: Every server performs useful work; if one fails, the remaining servers continue their normal work plus the failed server's work.

Virtualization has reduced the need for large numbers of physical server clusters for disaster recovery because a virtualized image can be quickly moved to another physical server.

C. Drives

The two primary types of drives are Hard Disk Drives (HDDs), which use spinning platters, and Solid-State Drives (SSDs), which store data on chips. SSDs are more resistant to failure, more reliable, and fail at a lower rate than HDDs over their first four years of service. Most servers utilize SSDs for booting (due to speed and reliability) and HDDs for data storage (due to lower cost).

Since drives cannot typically be repaired, the metric mean time to repair (MTTR), which is the average time needed to restore a failed component, is often meaningless. Organizations instead rely on mean time between failures (MTBF), which refers to the average amount of time until a component fails, cannot be repaired, and must be replaced. This metric helps determine the necessary stockpile of spare drives for quick replacement.

RAID

RAID (Redundant Array of Independent Drives) is a technology that uses multiple hard drives for increased reliability and performance. RAID can be implemented either through software (at the OS level) or hardware (requiring a specialized controller).

The most common levels of RAID are Levels 0, 1, 5, 6, and 10.

  • RAID Level 0 (Striping): Divides data into sections (stripes) alternated across drives. It is not fault-tolerant; if one drive fails, all data is lost. It is good for noncritical storage requiring high speed.
  • RAID Level 1 (Mirroring): Connects multiple drives to the same controller, making them exact duplicates of the primary drive. If the primary drive fails, the others take over with no data loss. It is best for mission-critical storage. Disk duplexing is a variation that uses separate controller cards for each disk to protect against single controller failure.
  • RAID Level 5 (Distributed Parity): Distributes parity data (error checking) across all drives. It combines efficient storage with excellent security and decent performance, suitable for file and application servers.
  • Nested RAID (e.g., RAID 10): Combines two other RAID levels (e.g., RAID 0 and Level 1).
Caution: RAID, except for Level 0, offers protection from a single drive failure, but it is not intended to replace data backups; rather, it provides an immediate, live copy of data in case of failure.
SAN Multipath

A Storage Area Network (SAN) is a dedicated network storage facility that consolidates different storage facilities to appear to the server as a single pool of locally attached devices. Multipath is a technique that creates more than one physical path between devices and a SAN. If one path is interrupted, multipath redirects the connection to another path.

D. Networks

Redundant networks wait in the background and use a replication scheme to keep their copy of live network information current, ensuring services are always accessible. If a disaster occurs, the redundant network automatically launches transparently to users.

Redundancy is achieved through various components:

  • NIC Teaming: Configuring up to 32 physical Network Interface Card (NIC) adapters on a server into one or more software-based virtual network adapters for redundancy and faster performance.
  • Failover ports: Switches and routers may offer a primary active port and a standby failover network port for physical redundancy.
  • Load balancers: Provide network redundancy by blocking traffic to non-functioning servers.
  • SDN controllers: Virtual Software-Defined Network (SDN) controllers can increase reliability by setting up multiple paths between origin and destination, preventing impact from the outage of a single link.

E. Power

Maintaining electrical power is essential for redundancy.

  • Dual power supply: Critical devices like servers can be fitted with dual power supplies so that if one fails, the other takes over.
  • Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS): A device that maintains power to equipment in case of an interruption in the primary electrical source. A UPS can communicate with the network OS to ensure an orderly shutdown by notifying administrators and users, preventing new logins, disconnecting users, and shutting down the server.

The two primary types of UPSs are:

UPS Type Description
Off-line UPS The equipment uses standard primary power; the UPS quickly (within milliseconds) begins supplying power only when the primary source is interrupted.
On-line UPS The UPS is always running off its battery while the main power runs the battery charger. It cleans the electrical power before it reaches the server and can serve as a surge protector.

Since a UPS supplies power for only a limited time, organizations often use a generator (powered by diesel, natural gas, or propane) to create backup power. Commercial generators are permanently installed and include automatic transfer switches that instantly detect power loss and switch to the backup generator.

F. Sites

Organizations maintain redundant recovery sites to move operations temporarily if a major disaster damages the primary facility.

The three basic types of redundant sites are:

  • Hot site: Essentially a duplicate of the production site. It has all necessary equipment, office space, and a live telecommunications link. Data backups can be quickly moved, or synchronization may occur automatically, allowing operations to be moved typically within an hour.
  • Cold site: Provides office space, but the customer must provide and install all equipment. It is less expensive but requires more time to achieve full operation.
  • Warm site: Has all equipment installed but lacks active Internet/telecommunications facilities and does not have current backups of data. This is less expensive than a hot site, but recovery time can be half a day or more.

When creating alternate sites, geographic dispersal is important. Sites should be distributed across a larger area rather than being clustered in a limited area to mitigate the impact of large-scale environmental or human-made disasters.

G. Clouds

Cloud computing is increasingly used for recovery, as users can often directly access backed-up applications and data through the cloud infrastructure. Cloud resilience requires two main considerations:

  1. Geographic dispersal: Duplicating processes across one or more geographical areas, known as high availability across zones.
  2. Multi-cloud systems: Spreading cloud computing across multiple cloud providers. This allows an organization to tolerate a critical issue that could occur with a single cloud provider.

H. Data

Data resilience is achieved by copying data. The methods for copying data depend on two critical calculations:

  • Recovery Point Objective (RPO): The maximum length of time an organization can tolerate between data copies. This is the "age" of the data they wish to be able to restore.
  • Recovery Time Objective (RTO): The length of time required to recover the data that has been copied.

Data copies fall into two main types:

Scheduled Event: Backup

A backup is a single scheduled event where data is copied and stored. The frequency is generally once per day. Backups can be stored onsite (immediately available, but vulnerable to site disaster) or offsite (away from the production facility, but raises security and access delay concerns).

Continuous: Replication

Replication is the continuous copying of data, serving as an alternative to a single scheduled event. Replication techniques include:

  • Snapshot: Repeatedly takes a “picture” of the state of the data, allowing restoration from a specific point in time.
  • Journaling: Makes a copy of the data whenever a change to the data occurs.

Replication often results in a very low RTO, sometimes to the extent that users do not realize data was restored. The differences between backups and replication are summarized below:

Feature Backup (Scheduled Event) Replication (Continuous)
Frequency Single scheduled event (e.g., once daily) Continuous copy (e.g., via journaling or snapshots)
RPO Usually measured in hours or days Usually measured in minutes or seconds
RTO Often higher, depending on media access Often very low; users may not realize restoration occurred
Method Data copied and stored Data continuously copied upon change

III. Incident Investigation

Following a cybersecurity incident, a full investigation is required to pinpoint how it occurred, prevent future incidents, and meet regulatory compliance reporting requirements. Often, investigations only examine superficial causes, failing to determine exactly what happened and why.

Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is the process of discovering the origin (root) cause of the security event. Incident investigation involves analyzing data sources and performing a digital forensics investigation.

A. Data Sources

Several data sources provide clues for uncovering how an incident occurred.

Log Files

A log is a record of events that occur. Security logs are particularly important for incident investigation as they can reveal the type of attack and how existing security defenses were circumvented.

Logs that typically provide the most beneficial security data for an investigation include:

Log Type Description
Firewall logs Security data useful for investigation
Intrusion detection systems (IDS)/intrusion prevention systems (IPS) logs Security data useful for investigation
Application logs Security data useful for investigation
Endpoint logs Security data useful for investigation
OS-specific security logs Security data useful for investigation
Network logs Security data useful for investigation
Metadata logs Contains data that describes information about other data (e.g., file type, authorship, date/time created)

Log management (transmitting, collecting, analyzing, and disposing of log data) faces several challenges:

  1. Multiple devices generate logs: Nearly every network device creates logs, and each device may interpret an event differently, creating a confusing mix of data.
  2. Very large volume of data: Devices generate huge amounts of data, including non-security-related events, making filtering overwhelming.
  3. Different log formats: Different devices record information in different formats, making combining multiple logs a major challenge.
Other Data Sources

Other useful sources include:

  • Vulnerability scans.
  • Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) products, which consolidate real-time security monitoring with analysis and reporting. A SIEM dashboard provides alerts, trends, sensitivity, and correlation data collected from its sensors.
  • Network Internet Protocol (IP) monitors: Provide insight by creating automated reports on activity.
    • sFlow is a packet capture sampling protocol that generates information based on capturing packets.

B. Digital Forensics

Digital forensics is the application of science to questions of interest to the legal profession, focusing on technology. It involves the retrieval of difficult-to-obtain data, which is usually hidden, altered, or deleted by the perpetrator. A digital forensics specialist searches for evidence pertaining to cybercrime or damage.

Digital forensics is often confused with e-discovery, which is the electronic counterpart of manually sifting through documents during legal discovery. E-discovery involves uploading electronic documents (emails, spreadsheets, PDFs) to a platform where software quickly searches for information based on key terms.

Forensics Procedures

When responding to an incident that requires digital forensics, five basic steps are followed:

  1. Secure the Scene: Immediate action is necessary to prevent digital evidence from being overwritten or contaminated. This involves documenting the physical surroundings, identifying and tagging all cables, and taking custody of the device and peripherals.
  2. Preserve the Evidence: Tasked with ensuring important proof is not corrupted or destroyed. Placing evidence in sealed bags with tamper-evident seals and identifying tags helps mitigate nonrepudiation. Once data is identified, it may be placed under a legal hold, meaning it cannot be modified, deleted, or destroyed.
  3. Document Chain of Custody: A strict chain of custody must be maintained from the beginning of the work. This detailed document (provenance) ensures the evidence was always under strict control and no unauthorized person had the opportunity to corrupt it. Gaps in custody can lead to severe legal consequences.
  4. Examine for Evidence: Specialized tools are used to gather evidence (acquisition). Software tools capture the system image (or a snapshot) of the current state of settings and data. A mirror-image backup (bit-stream backup) is an evidence-grade backup that replicates all sectors of a drive, including hidden data storage areas and the swap/pagefile. Common software suites include EnCase and FTK Imager. Specialized hardware tools, like mobile device forensics tools, extract data such as Call Detail Records, GPS data, app data, SMS texts, photos, and videos.
  5. Generate a Report: Upon completion, a detailed written description of the acquisition and analysis of the evidence is required (reporting).
Order of Volatility

When examining devices for evidence (artifacts), a specific order of volatility must be followed to preserve the most fragile data first. The first two levels are considered the most volatile because they change very quickly.

Order Examples Description
1 Registers and CPU cache Extremely volatile and change constantly
2 Routing tables, ARP cache, process table, kernel statistics, RAM Data can change quickly while the system is operating; kernel statistics move between cache and main memory; RAM is lost if power is lost
3 Temporary file systems Not subject to the degree of rapid changes as the prior elements
4 Hard drive Data is relatively stable
5 Remote logging and monitoring data More volatile than hard drive data, but hard drive data is considered more valuable and is preserved first
6 Physical configuration and network topology Not considered volatile and do not have a significant impact on an investigation
7 Archival media Data that has been preserved in archival form is not volatile

IV. Cautionary Cybersecurity Trend

A significant and alarming trend in cybersecurity involves the retrieval of sensitive data from hardware devices that have been decommissioned and sold on the secondary market. Studies consistently show that a majority of secondhand computing equipment (hard drives, USB drives, phones) have incomplete or no data wiping before resale, often containing sensitive personal data such as contact information, intimate photos, medical history, and financial data.

A recent example involves used routers sold on the secondary market, a category of hardware previously not extensively examined. Security researchers purchased 18 used core routers from major vendors (Cisco, Fortinet, Juniper Networks). Using only standard router operating system commands and utilities—not specialized forensic tools—they found sensitive corporate data on nine of them.

The types of corporate secrets exposed were extensive:

  • VPN credentials or hashed root passwords (100% of examined routers).
  • Router-to-router authentication keys (89%).
  • Itemized connection details for specific applications (89%).
  • Credentials for connecting to other networks as a trusted party (44%).
  • Data allowing third-party connections to the network (33%).
  • Customer data (22%).

This exposed data included firewall rules, network locations, and information on remote cloud applications. Threat actors could use this internal information to plan and execute attacks by impersonating network or internal hosts, or by guessing general security levels across the environment.

Researchers recommended that hardware vendors:

  1. Provide free, full, and complete instructions for securely wiping devices, even for unsupported models.
  2. Ensure secure-wipe instructions use similar language and terms across product lines and the industry.
  3. In future designs, only use easily removable media (like SSDs or CF cards) to store sensitive configuration information, allowing the media to be removed prior to decommissioning.

Study Guide for Incident Preparation and Investigation

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

1. What is the fundamental difference between a Business Continuity Plan (BCP) and a Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)?

A Business Continuity Plan (BCP) is a high-level strategic document focused on maintaining an organization's overall operations and services during a disruptive event. A Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) is a subset of the BCP that specifically details the technical processes for restoring IT functions and services following a significant disruption. The DRP is tactical, while the BCP is strategic.

2. Explain the purpose of a Business Impact Analysis (BIA) and identify two of its primary goals.

A Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is a tool used to identify business processes and functions and to quantify the potential impact of their loss. Its primary goals are to determine the mission-essential functions of the enterprise and to identify critical systems and any single points of failure that would disable an entire system if they were to fail.

3. Describe the security risks associated with decommissioned network hardware, as highlighted by the ESET researchers' study.

The ESET study found that a significant number of decommissioned routers sold on the secondary market were not properly wiped of sensitive corporate data. This data included customer information, network credentials, VPN keys, and firewall rules, which a threat actor could use to gain inside information and plan a highly effective cyberattack.

4. What are the Recovery Point Objective (RPO) and Recovery Time Objective (RTO), and how do they relate to data copying strategies?

The Recovery Point Objective (RPO) is the maximum length of time an organization can tolerate between data copies, essentially defining the acceptable "age" of the data to be restored. The Recovery Time Objective (RTO) is the length of time it will take to actually recover and restore the data after an incident has occurred.

5. List and briefly describe the five main steps involved in a digital forensics procedure.

The five basic steps are: 1) Secure the Scene to prevent evidence from being overwritten or contaminated; 2) Preserve the Evidence to ensure proof is not corrupted; 3) Document the Chain of Custody to track who handled the evidence at all times; 4) Examine for Evidence using specialized tools; and 5) Generate a Report detailing the acquisition and analysis.

6. Explain the concept of a server cluster and differentiate between asymmetric and symmetric clustering.

A server cluster is the combination of two or more interconnected servers that appear as a single unit to provide high availability. In an asymmetric server cluster, a standby server exists only to take over if the primary server fails. In a symmetric server cluster, every server performs useful work, and if one fails, the others take on its workload in addition to their own.

7. What are three key challenges associated with log management in a large enterprise environment?

Three key challenges of log management include the vast number of devices generating logs, the very large volume of data produced, and the fact that different devices use different log formats. These factors make it difficult to consolidate, filter, and analyze log data effectively during an investigation.

8. Define the "chain of custody" and explain its importance in an incident investigation.

A chain of custody is a detailed document that tracks where evidence was at all times, from the beginning of an investigation. It documents who handled the systems, for how long, and how they were managed, ensuring that the evidence was always under strict control and no unauthorized person had the opportunity to corrupt it. This documentation is critical for the evidence to be admissible in a court of law.

9. Compare the three types of redundant recovery sites: hot site, cold site, and warm site.

A hot site is a fully equipped duplicate of the production site, allowing for immediate resumption of operations. A cold site provides only office space, requiring the organization to bring in and install all equipment. A warm site is a compromise, containing all the necessary equipment but lacking active internet connections or current data backups.

10. Differentiate between the two main techniques for data replication: snapshot and journaling.

A snapshot is a replication technique that takes a "picture" of the state of the data at repeated intervals, allowing restoration to a specific point in time. Journaling is a continuous process that makes a copy of the data whenever a change occurs, providing a more up-to-the-minute replica.

Replication: The process of continuously copying data as an alternative to single scheduled backups.
Root-Cause Analysis (RCA): The process of discovering the origin (root) cause of a security event.
Server Cluster: The combination of two or more servers that are interconnected to appear as one.
Single Point of Failure: A component or entity in a system that, if it no longer functions, will disable the entire system.
Snapshot: A data replication technique that takes a "picture" of the state of the data repeatedly.
Solid-State Drive (SSD): A data storage device that stores data on chips instead of magnetic platters, offering greater reliability and speed than HDDs.
Storage Area Network (SAN): A dedicated network storage facility that provides access to data storage over a high-speed network.
Symmetric Server Cluster: A server cluster in which every server performs useful work, and if one server fails, the remaining servers continue to perform their normal work as well as that of the failed server.
System Image: A snapshot of the current state of system elements, containing all current settings and data.
Tabletop Exercise: An incident response testing exercise involving a discussion of a scenario in an informal environment to identify weaknesses in a plan.
Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS): A device that maintains power to equipment in case of an interruption in the primary electrical power source.
Warm Site: A redundant recovery site that has all equipment installed but does not have active Internet or telecommunications facilities and does not have current backups of data.
