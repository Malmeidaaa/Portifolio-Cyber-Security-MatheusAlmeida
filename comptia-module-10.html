Module 10: Wireless Network Attacks and Defenses

Study Portfolio | Matheus Almeida e Silva

1. Challenges of Technological Diversity and Security Focus

Wireless technology, especially Wireless Local Area Networks (WLANs) or Wi-Fi, has undergone astonishing growth over the last 25 years. Initially introduced in 1997 with data transmissions at a mere 2 million bits per second (Mbps), Wi-Fi is rapidly advancing, with the forthcoming Wi-Fi 7 (IEEE 802.11be) version designed to transmit at an impressive 46,210 Mbps (or 46 Gbps).

This technological evolution has had a profound global impact. Wi-Fi has become ubiquitous—expected in almost every public setting, from coffee shops to sports stadiums—and is an essential driver of the modern economy.

Intel's Long-Term Retention Lab

To proactively address the issue of managing technology diversity for security research, Intel established the Long-Term Retention Lab in Costa Rica.

  • Function: This facility was created to store both old and new technology devices, making them available for remote security testing.
  • Capacity and Scope: Fully functional by 2019, the warehouse currently stores around 3,000 hardware and software items, dating back over 10 years.
  • Security: Access is strictly controlled, the location is secret, and new employees must be approved by senior management.
  • Testing: Engineers worldwide can request specific device configurations for testing (receiving over 1,000 requests monthly).

2. Common Wireless Technologies and Attacks

Wireless networks, including Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and Near Field Communication (NFC), significantly extend the attack surface beyond physical borders, allowing remote exploitation.

A. Cellular Networks

Cellular networks are the most widespread wireless networks. They are operated, owned, maintained, and managed by telecommunication service providers. Crucially, end users are not responsible for configuring or securing these cellular networks.

B. Bluetooth Attacks

Bluetooth is a wireless Personal Area Network (PAN) technology that uses short-range Radio Frequency (RF) transmissions.

  • Bluejacking: An attack that sends unsolicited messages (text, images, or sounds) to Bluetooth-enabled devices. It is usually considered more annoying than harmful because no data is stolen.
  • Bluesnarfing: An attack that accesses unauthorized, confidential information (emails, calendars, contact lists, photos, or videos) from a wireless device by connecting without the owner’s knowledge or permission.

C. Near Field Communication (NFC)

NFC is a set of standards used to establish communication between devices brought into very close proximity (within 4 centimeters).

Vulnerability Explanation Defense
Eavesdropping Unencrypted NFC communication can be intercepted. Users must be aware of surroundings.
Data theft Attackers can "bump" a portable reader to a smartphone. Turn off NFC in large crowds.
MITM Attacker intercepts NFC communications and forges response. Configure pairing so one sends, one receives.

D. RFID Attacks

RFID tags are susceptible to attacks such as RFID Cloning, which involves capturing data transmitted via RFID and transferring it to another tag.

3. Wireless Local Area Networks (WLAN)

A WLAN (Wi-Fi) is designed to replace or supplement a wired LAN.

Enterprise AP Types

  • Fat APs (Autonomous): Independent and self-contained.
  • Thin APs (Lightweight): Lack internal management; configuration is centralized in a wireless switch.
  • Controller APs: Thin APs managed by a dedicated Wireless LAN Controller (WLC).
  • Captive Portal APs: Used in public areas to force users to agree to an AUP or login.

Common Wireless Threat Vectors

Attack Type Description
Evil Twin Fake AP with identical SSID to trick users into connecting.
Rogue AP Unauthorized AP installed on a network that bypasses security controls.
Deauthentication Attacker sends spoofed frames to force clients to disconnect.
War Driving Physically driving around to locate vulnerable networks.

4. Vulnerabilities in Legacy WLAN Security

  • WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy): Original protocol, highly insecure due to short IV length and detectable patterns.
  • WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup): PIN method is vulnerable to brute-force attacks (under 4 hours).
  • MAC Address Filtering: Vulnerable because MAC addresses are exchanged unencrypted and can be spoofed.
  • WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access): Addressed WEP deficiencies but still has weaknesses.

5. Modern Wireless Security Solutions

A. WPA2

Uses AES-CCMP for encryption. WPA2-Enterprise uses IEEE 802.1x for port-based authentication involving a Supplicant, Authenticator, and Authentication Server (RADIUS).

B. WPA3

Key improvements:

  • SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals): Secures the handshake against dictionary attacks.
  • PMF (Protected Management Frames): Mitigates disassociation attacks.
  • OWE (Opportunistic Wireless Encryption): Encrypts open networks.

Study Guide: Wireless Network Attacks and Defenses

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

1. What are the key differences between the Bluetooth attacks known as Bluejacking and Bluesnarfing?

Bluejacking is an attack that sends unsolicited messages, images, or sounds to Bluetooth-enabled devices and is considered more annoying than harmful as no data is stolen. In contrast, Bluesnarfing is a more malicious attack that accesses and copies unauthorized information—such as emails, contact lists, and pictures—from a wireless device through a Bluetooth connection without the owner's knowledge.

2. Explain the primary vulnerability of the PIN method in Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS).

The PIN method in WPS is vulnerable because it has significant design flaws. An attacker can make unlimited PIN attempts, the last digit is just a checksum, and the router validates the PIN in two separate halves, effectively requiring an attacker to crack two much shorter PINs instead of one long one.

3. What is a captive portal AP, and what is its primary function in a public WLAN?

A captive portal AP is a system used in public WLANs that requires user interaction via a standard web browser before granting broader network access. Its primary function is to present information, require agreement to an acceptable use policy (AUP), or ask for valid login credentials, providing a higher degree of security and control.

4. Describe how Near Field Communication (NFC) establishes a connection and identify one major security risk associated with its use.

NFC establishes a two-way connection when devices are brought within 4 centimeters of each other, using magnetic induction between the internal antennas of an interrogator (like a smartphone) and a tag. A major security risk is data theft, where an attacker can "bump" a portable reader against a user's phone in a crowd to steal stored payment information.

5. What is a rogue access point (AP), and why does it pose a significant security threat to an enterprise network?

A rogue AP is an unauthorized access point installed on a network, often by an employee, that bypasses the organization's primary security controls. It is a significant threat because an attacker who connects to the rogue AP is effectively behind the corporate firewall, giving them access to inject malware or steal data from the internal network.

6. Explain the concept of MAC address filtering and describe its main security weakness.

MAC address filtering is a wireless access control method that permits or denies a device from connecting to a network based on its unique hardware MAC address. Its main security weakness is that MAC addresses are initially exchanged between devices and the AP in an unencrypted format, allowing an attacker to monitor the transmission, see an approved MAC address, and spoof it on their own device.

7. What are the three most common types of wireless denial-of-service attacks mentioned in the material?

The three most common types of wireless denial attacks are jamming, a disassociation attack, and a duration field values attack. Jamming floods the RF spectrum with interference, a disassociation attack uses spoofed management frames to force clients off the network, and a duration field values attack uses a high value in the RTS/CTS protocol to prevent other devices from transmitting.

8. How does the WPA2-Enterprise security model handle authentication, and what framework is used to transport authentication protocols?

The WPA2-Enterprise model handles authentication using the IEEE 802.1x standard, which provides port-based authentication where a client (supplicant) must be verified by an authentication server. The Extensible Authentication Protocol (EAP) is the framework used to transport the various authentication protocols between the supplicant, authenticator, and authentication server.

9. What is an "evil twin" attack, and how does it compromise user data?

An evil twin is a fraudulent access point set up by an attacker that is designed to mimic a legitimate, authorized AP. Unsuspecting users' devices connect to the evil twin, allowing the attacker to intercept the connection and capture all wireless transmissions, including sensitive data like usernames and passwords.

10. Identify two key security improvements introduced with WPA3 compared to its predecessors.

WPA3 introduces Simultaneous Authentication of Equals (SAE), which increases security during the initial key exchange handshake, providing stronger protection even if weak passwords are used. WPA3 also mandates Protected Management Frames (PMFs) to mitigate against disassociation attacks, which were a known weakness in the IEEE 802.11 standard.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Access Point (AP) A centrally located WLAN connection device that acts as a base station for the wireless network and as a bridge between the wireless and wired networks.
Bluejacking An attack that sends unsolicited messages (text, images, or sounds) to Bluetooth-enabled devices. It is considered more annoying than harmful as no data is stolen.
Bluesnarfing An attack that accesses unauthorized information (emails, contacts, pictures, etc.) from a wireless device through a Bluetooth connection without the owner's knowledge.
Bluetooth A wireless technology using short-range radio frequency (RF) transmissions for data communication over short distances, enabling rapid device pairings.
Captive Portal AP An access point that uses a standard web browser to provide information and requires a user to agree to a policy or present valid login credentials before granting network access.
CBC-MAC A component of the CCMP protocol used in WPA2 that provides data integrity and authentication.
Controller AP An access point that is managed through a dedicated wireless LAN controller (WLC), which centralizes configuration and management.
CCMP The cryptographic wireless protocol used for encryption in WPA2, which specifies the use of AES.
Disassociation Attack A wireless denial attack where an attacker sends spoofed deauthentication or disassociation management frames to a client, causing it to disconnect from the AP.
Duration Field Values Attack A wireless denial attack where an attacker sends a frame with the duration field set to a high value, reserving the wireless medium and preventing other devices from transmitting.
EAP-FAST An EAP protocol that securely tunnels any credential form for authentication (such as a password or a token) using TLS.
EAP-TLS An EAP protocol that uses digital certificates for authentication.
EAP-TTLS An EAP protocol that securely tunnels client password authentication within Transport Layer Security (TLS) records.
Evil Twin An AP set up by an attacker designed to mimic an authorized AP, tricking users into connecting so the attacker can capture their data transmissions.
Extensible Authentication Protocol (EAP) A framework for transporting authentication protocols, defining the message format used in standards like IEEE 802.1x.
Heat Map A visual representation of wireless signal coverage and strength, typically overlaid on a building floorplan to identify problem areas during a site survey.
IEEE 802.1x A standard that provides port-based authentication, blocking all traffic on a port until a client is authenticated by an authentication server. It is used by WPA2-Enterprise.
Jamming A wireless denial attack where an attacker uses intentional RF interference to flood the spectrum with noise, preventing devices from communicating with the AP.
MAC Address Filtering A type of wireless access control that permits or blocks a device from connecting to a network based on its unique Media Access Control (MAC) address.
Near Field Communication (NFC) A set of standards used to establish two-way communication between devices in very close proximity (within 4 cm).
Piconet The primary type of Bluetooth network, established when two devices come within range. One device acts as the "broadcaster" (leader) and the other as the "observer" (follower).
Protected EAP (PEAP) A common EAP protocol designed to simplify 802.1x deployment by using Microsoft Windows logins and passwords, creating an encrypted channel between the client and authentication server.
RFID A wireless technology used to transmit information between tags (often paper-based) and a proximity reader. Passive RFID tags are powered by the reader's signal.
RFID Cloning An attack that involves capturing data transmitted from an RFID tag and transferring it to another tag for nefarious purposes.
Rogue AP An unauthorized access point installed on a network (often by an insider) that allows an attacker to bypass network security configurations.
SAE A feature of WPA3 designed to increase security during the key exchange handshake, providing stronger security even if weak passwords are used.
Site Survey An in-depth examination and analysis of a WLAN site, conducted to determine the optimal placement of APs for adequate coverage and minimal signal bleed.
WEP An early IEEE 802.11 security protocol designed to encrypt wireless transmissions. It is now considered highly insecure due to several vulnerabilities.
WPA A wireless security protocol introduced to address the flaws in WEP. It has since been superseded by more secure standards.
WPA2 The second generation of WPA, based on the IEEE 802.11i standard. It uses CCMP with AES for encryption and supports IEEE 802.1x for enterprise authentication.
WPA3 The next generation of WPA, offering enhanced security features such as SAE for stronger handshakes and PMF to protect against disassociation attacks.
WPS An optional means of configuring security on WLANs, designed for ease of use. The PIN method is known to have significant security flaws.
WLAN Commonly called Wi-Fi, a network designed to replace or supplement a wired Local Area Network (LAN).
WLC A dedicated, centralized device used to configure and manage multiple controller APs on a network.
