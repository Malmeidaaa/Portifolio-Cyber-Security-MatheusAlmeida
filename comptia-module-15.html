Module 15: Information Security Management

Study Portfolio | Matheus Almeida e Silva

Introduction

Management can be defined as using a degree of skill to direct a workflow or operation. Information security has a high requirement for good management because it touches every employee at every level at all times.

This module explores five key information security management processes, grouped into two major categories:

  • Asset Protection Involves Asset Management and Change Management.
  • Risk Management Involves Risk Management, Third-Party Risk Management, and Awareness Management.

I. Current Challenges in Security Awareness

Teaching security awareness to users who have little background in technology, little understanding of cyber risks, and often little interest in protecting themselves or the organization is an ongoing challenge. This challenge is intensified when users hear misleading warnings from uninformed journalists, read erroneous warnings from misinformed bloggers, or see unsubstantiated advice distributed by federal authorities.

The Case of "Juice Jacking"

"Juice jacking" is the name given to a proof-of-concept attack (a demonstration to determine if an idea can be turned into reality). This proposed attack involves exploiting public recharging stations that allow users to recharge their mobile phones.

Proposed Attack Scenario

A threat actor infects a public charging station (at airports, shopping malls, hotels, etc.) or sets up their own identical fake equipment. When the user's mobile device is connected, the station silently sends commands over the charging cord's cable back to the device to install malware or siphon data.

History and Recent Warnings

  • The proof of concept (along with the name "juice jacking") first surfaced over 20 years ago in 2011 at Defcon. Its demonstration was intended to bring awareness to a potential threat.
  • Fears reached a new high in 2023 when both the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issued new warnings.
  • The FBI alerted that "Bad actors have figured out ways to use public USB ports to introduce malware and monitoring software onto devices".
  • The FCC added a similar warning, stating that "criminals may have intentionally left cables plugged in at charging stations" or distributed "infected cables being given away as promotional gifts".
  • These warnings generated ominous news reports, describing juice jacking as a "significant privacy hazard".

Is Juice Jacking a Viable Threat?

Information security experts assert that, contrary to reports, juice jacking is not a viable threat in the real world (unless the target is a deep-pocket nation-state attacker carrying top-secret government documents).

Reason Why Juice Jacking Is Not a Viable Threat Explanation
Lack of Documented Cases There are no documented cases of juice jacking ever taking place "in the wild." No users have claimed to be victims, and security experts or Apple representatives are unaware of such attacks occurring.
Port Difference Public charging stations use USB charging ports, not data ports. A charging port can only send power, but not data. Connecting only sends power to the device.
Operating System Hardening Modern operating systems (Apple iOS and Google Android) require explicit user approval (clicking a warning) before files can be exchanged via cable. iPhones also require a password or facial scan to install applications.
High Effort, Low Return There is no single universal script for juice jacking that works on hundreds of different mobile devices; customized scripts are required for each type. The high investment of time required makes it generally unlikely for most attackers.
‚ö†Ô∏è The "Echo Chamber" Problem: Security experts criticize these warnings for creating a needless "echo chamber". They divert necessary attention away from bigger security threats, such as weak passwords and the failure to install security updates. These false warnings create unnecessary anxiety and inconvenience, running the risk that users will simply give up trying to be secure. Security professionals contend that government officials claiming such attacks are occurring must substantiate their claims with specific examples and corroborating expert explanations.

II. Asset Protection

Protecting assets is at the core of information security. Asset protection involves asset management and change management.

A. Asset Management (AM)

An asset is any item that has a positive economic value. Assets provide value to the enterprise, cannot easily be replaced without significant investment, and may form part of the corporate identity. IT assets primarily include hardware, software, and data. Not all assets have the same value.

Table 15-1: Typical IT Assets
Asset Description Example High Value?
Data (Collected, classified, organized, stored) Customer, personnel, production, sales, marketing, and finance databases Yes: Extremely difficult to replace
Customized business software (Supports business processes) Customized order transaction application Yes: Unique and customized for the enterprise
System software (Foundation for application software) Operating system No: Can be easily replaced
Physical items (Equipment, media, furniture) Servers, routers, and power supplies No: Can be easily replaced
Services (Outsourced computing services) Voice and data communications No: Can be easily replaced

Asset Management (AM) is the coordinated activity of an organization to realize value from its assets. This requires a systematic approach to asset governance and proper, cost-effective utilization to optimize the value assets produce. The foundational document for asset management is ISO 55000:2014.

Cybersecurity Asset Management (CAM)

Cybersecurity Asset Management (CAM) is a relatively new process that combines asset management with information security. Traditional asset counting is often a "point-in-time" monthly or quarterly event, creating significant gaps that can be "deadly" to security, as new or uncounted assets may be unprotected.

CAM addresses these gaps because it can:

  • Identify assets (endpoints, servers, IoT devices, cloud resources) on a continuous and real-time basis.
  • Identify potential security risks or gaps affecting each asset through vulnerability scanners.
  • Provide an up-to-date listing of assets immediately referenceable in the event of an attack.
  • Introduced the metric Mean Time to Inventory (MTTI), which helps determine when incident response is lagging due to missing inventory information.

Asset Management Lifecycle

Assets follow a lifecycle that begins when a need is identified and concludes with disposal or replacement. Costs associated with an asset accumulate throughout its lifecycle, varying depending on the current phase.

The typical asset lifecycle phases are:

  1. Requirements Definition
  2. Asset Planning
  3. Asset Creation/Acquisition
  4. Operations and Maintenance
  5. Asset Monitoring
  6. Renewal/Rehab
  7. Disposal

Selected Asset Management Tasks

Specific tasks include acquisition, assignment/accounting, tracking, and disposal.

Acquisition (Asset Procurement)

The process of identifying and securing an asset to support a business goal. Most businesses use a bidding process to obtain the lowest price.

  • Standard bidding process: Soliciting detailed proposals from qualified vendors.
  • E-bidding process: Using electronic platforms to quickly find vendors, often submitting only pricing information.
Assignment/Accounting

Determining and recording ownership and classification. Assets are classified by three factors:

  • Liquidity: Convertibility to cash (e.g., current asset vs. fixed asset).
  • Usage: How the asset is used (e.g., operating asset vs. nonoperating asset).
  • Physical Existence: Tangible asset (can be touched, e.g., hardware, cash) vs. Intangible asset (monetary value but no physical form, e.g., software, data, IP).
Tracking

Tracing the location of tangible assets. Methods include barcode labels, RFID tags, or GPS tracking sensors.

  • Inventory consists of raw materials, works in progress, and finished goods available for sale.
  • Asset Enumeration is a listing of assets by a seller of those assets. (This is distinct from information security enumeration, which actively uncovers attack vectors).
Disposal

Occurs when an asset is no longer useful. Involves two steps: Asset Decommissioning (withdrawn from service) and Asset Disposal (physical removal).

Data Retention: Data should be transferred to a different device before sanitization.

Sanitization (Media Destruction)

Paper Media: Usually destroyed by shredding. Methods include:

  • Strip-cut: Least secure, reconstructible
  • Cross-cut: More secure, adds horizontal cuts
  • Micro-cut: Shreds into tiny pieces
  • Other options: burning, pulping (breaking paper into wood cellulose fibers after ink removal), or pulverizing (hammering into dust)

Electronic Media: Never use the OS "delete" command (purging), as data can be retrieved by forensic tools. Techniques include:

  • Wiping: Overwriting disk space with zeros or random data
  • Degaussing: For magnetic hard drives, permanently destroys the drive by reducing the magnetic field
  • Verification (certification): May require wiping before degaussing to ensure regulatory compliance

B. Change Management

Change Management is a systematic approach to dealing with transformations (adjustments, replacements) within an organization, typically applying to work processes or technologies. It seeks to effect, control, and adapt to change. Change management watches for any adjustments or variations to assets that could impact security, such as configuration changes to a router.

Need for Change Management

Many standard operating procedures (SOPs) can impact information security.

Table 15-2: Business Processes and Impact
Business Process Impact
Navigate multilayer approval process Can dramatically slow the acquisition of needed security appliances or software.
Change in ownership or stakeholders Can affect how the asset is used or impact the perception and usability of the asset.
Implement impact analysis or test results Can dramatically change how devices are used or configured.
Execute backout plan or maintenance window Both can significantly impact security; a backout plan involves procedures needed if a "rollback" is required; a maintenance window is a designated time for routine system maintenance.
Table 15-3: Technology Processes and Impact
Technology Process Impact
Changing allow lists/deny lists Modifying permissions can significantly impact security.
Modifying restricted activities Changing a restricted activity to permitted can have unforeseen consequences.
Experiencing downtime Can result in device restarts that are out-of-sync with others.
Implementing service or application restarts Can result in gaps in security coverage.
Using legacy applications Often have unpatched vulnerabilities.
Adjusting dependencies Changing reliance between services can result in a security issue.

Change management requires verified procedures for planning, testing, communicating, scheduling, and implementing change. Documentation is critical for maintaining an audit trail (for rollbacks) and ensuring regulatory compliance. Formal change management policies (rules) and change management procedures (mandatory steps) should be in place.

Change Management Tools

Tools vary by organization size:

  • Small organizations: Spreadsheets, flowcharts, and Gantt charts (horizontal lines show work completed vs. planned).
  • Larger organizations: Specialized software suites maintain digital change logs, providing stakeholders with a holistic view. They can automatically update diagrams and indicate necessary updating policies/procedures.
  • Software Development: Version control software assists with documentation, prevents multiple simultaneous code changes, tracks changes, and allows backing out of changes.

III. Risk Management

A. Defining Risk

An asset is continually under threat, which is a type of action that has the potential to cause harm. Risk is fundamentally defined as a situation that involves exposure to some type of danger. More complexly, risk is a function of threats, the consequences of those threats, and the resulting vulnerabilities. Organizations must determine the likelihood of occurrence that a threat will compromise an asset.

Table 15-4: Threat Classifications
Threat Category Description Example
Strategic Action affecting long-term organizational goals Theft of intellectual property, loss of a major account, competitor entering the market
Compliance Following (or not following) a regulation or standard Breach of contract, not responding to new laws
Financial Impact of financial decisions or market factors Increase in interest rates, global financial crisis
Operational Events impacting daily business Fire, hazardous chemical spill, power blackout
Technical Events affecting IT systems Denial of service attack, SQL injection attack, virus
Managerial Actions related to organization management Long-term illness of company president, key employee resigning

Sources of Risk

Risk can originate from various sources:

  • Internal and External: Internal risks come from within (e.g., employee theft); external risks come from outside (e.g., actions of a hacktivist).
  • Legacy Systems: Often risks result from legacy software (OS or program) with unpatched vulnerabilities.
  • Multiparty Risks: The impact that vulnerabilities of one organization have on connected "downstream" organizations.
  • Software Compliance and Licensing: Risks associated with violating licensing restrictions to protect the rights of the developer. Most organizations unknowingly violate licensing agreements.
Table 15-5: Reasons for Software Noncompliance
Reason Explanation
Software licensed for one reason but used for another E.g., Limited-use license bought for nonproduction environment used in production.
Product use rights changed E.g., Developers change rules requiring new licenses for indirect access by third parties.
Software installed on a virtual machine E.g., Software migrated to a VM and moved to multiple machines in license violation.

B. Analyzing Risks

Risk Analysis (or Risk Identification) is the process of identifying and assessing factors that may jeopardize a project or goal.

Methodology and Biases

Identifying risks is difficult due to their elusive nature and unconscious human biases. These biases influence decision-making.

Table 15-6: Decision-Making Biases and Effects
Bias Explanation
Aggregate bias Inferring about an individual using data describing trends for the broader population.
Anchoring bias Focusing on a specific feature early in the decision process, marginalizing others.
Availability bias Perceiving event likelihood based on how frequently it is heard of.
Confirmation bias Making a decision first and then only looking for supporting data.
Present bias Tending to discount future risks in favor of immediate gratification.
Framing effect Deciding based on how choices are worded.
Fundamental attribution error Viewing others' failures as identity flaws rather than contextual influences.
üí° Best Practice: To minimize biases, involving a large number of individuals in the risk analysis process is the most effective approach. Risk Control Self-Assessment (RCSA) is an "empowering" methodology where management and staff collectively identify and evaluate risks. The goal is to integrate risk management into the organizational culture.

Risk Assessment Approaches

Risk assessment frequency can be a one-time assessment, ad hoc assessment (whenever necessary), recurring assessment (periodic), or continuous assessment (year-round).

  • Qualitative Risk Analysis: Uses an "educated guess" based on observation. Typically assigns a numeric value (1‚Äì10) or a label (High, Medium, or Low) to represent the risk.
  • Quantitative Risk Analysis: Considered more formal and systematic. Uses historical data to create "hard" numbers associated with the risk. It is divided into risk likelihood and risk impact.

Quantitative Risk Calculation

Risk Likelihood (Probability)

Historical data informs the probability (likelihood) that a risk will become a reality.

Quantitative Likelihood Tools
Tool Description
Mean Time Between Failure (MTBF) Average time until a component fails, cannot be repaired, and must be replaced (measures reliability).
Mean Time to Recovery (MTTR) Average time for a device to recover from a non-terminal failure.
Mean Time to Failure (MTTF) Average time expected until the first failure for systems that cannot be repaired.
Failure in Time (FIT) Number of expected failures per one billion hours of operation (used by the semiconductor industry).
Annualized Rate of Occurrence (ARO) The probability of a risk occurring within one year.

Historical data sources include law enforcement agencies, insurance companies, and computer incident monitoring organizations.

Risk Impact

Determined by comparing the risk to the associated monetary loss. All costs must be considered.

Three formulas calculate expected losses:

Single Loss Expectancy (SLE)

The expected monetary loss every time a risk occurs.

SLE = AV √ó EF

(AV = Asset Value; EF = Exposure Factor (proportion of asset destroyed))

Example: AV = $10,000,000; EF = 0.75. SLE = $7,500,000

Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE)

The anticipated monetary loss expected for an asset due to a risk over a one-year period.

ALE = SLE √ó ARO

Example: SLE = $7,500,000; ARO = 0.01. ALE = $75,000

Risk Exposure Factor

The probability of the risk occurring multiplied by the total loss upon its occurrence (the potential for financial loss).

Risk Reporting

Different tools are used for representing identified risks, known as risk reporting.

  • Risk Register: A list of potential threats and associated risks, often shown as a table, providing a clear snapshot of vulnerabilities. It may include: key risk indicators (primary risk factors), risk owners (those responsible for the asset), and the risk threshold (maximum tolerable risk).
  • Risk Matrix/Heatmap: A visual color-coded tool that lists the impact and likelihood of risks.

C. Managing Risks

The objective of risk management is to create a level of protection that mitigates vulnerabilities and reduces potential consequences, determined by what the organization feels comfortable accepting.

  • Risk Tolerance: The level of risk an organization can accept per individual risk. Related to accepting consequences and having resources to absorb them.
  • Risk Appetite: The total risk the organization can bear in a given risk profile. Related to long-term strategy. Risk appetite can be conservative (little tolerance), expansionary (high tolerance), or neutral.

Strategies for Dealing with Risks

There are four primary strategies for managing risks:

  1. Accept: Acknowledging the risk but taking no steps to address it. This may involve an exception (security requirement cannot be fully implemented) or an exemption (from a regulating body).
  2. Transfer: Shifting the risk to a third party, often by purchasing cybersecurity insurance.
  3. Avoid: Identifying the risk and choosing not to engage in the activity (e.g., not building in an earthquake zone).
  4. Mitigate: Attempting to make the risk less severe by implementing defenses.

Third-Party Risk Management

Third-Party Risk Management involves reducing risks associated with external entities, such as vendors, business partners, and the supply chain. The SolarWinds incident illustrated a massive supply chain infection risk.

Risks of Third-Party Integration

Access to the organization's network introduces the principle of the weakest link. Specific risks include:

  • On-boarding and Off-boarding: Ensuring security defenses are not compromised during the start or termination of the relationship.
  • Application and Social Media Network Sharing: Determining who is responsible for support, vulnerability assessments, and sharing social media information.
  • Privacy and Risk Awareness: Dealing with differences in privacy policies and how risk assessment is performed on combined systems.
  • Data Considerations: Clear understanding of data ownership, backup methods, and restrictions on unauthorized sharing.
Assessing and Selecting Third Parties (Vendor Monitoring)

Risk reduction is achieved through close oversight by requiring:

  • Annual questionnaires about supply-chain security protections.
  • Regular penetration testing and a right-to-audit clause.
  • Evidence of internal audits and an independent assessment of those audits.
  • Regular supply-chain analysis.
  • Creation of rules of engagement defining interaction expectations.
  • Adherence to due diligence and absence of conflict of interests.
Vendor Agreement Instruments (Formal Contractual Relationships)

Agreements should be regularly reviewed to verify compliance.

Vendor Agreement Instruments
Instrument Description
Service-Level Agreement (SLA) Service contract specifying services, responsibilities, and service guarantees.
Business Partnership Agreement (BPA) Contract establishing rules, responsibilities, capital contributions, and financial reporting between partners.
Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) Describes "convergence of will"; generally not legally enforceable, but more formal than an unwritten agreement.
Nondisclosure Agreement (NDA) Legal contract specifying how confidential material will be shared and restricted.
Measurement System Analysis (MSA) Uses scientific tools to determine the amount of variation added to a process by a measurement system.
Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) Establishes common legal terms for a "conditional agreement" anticipating fund transfer for services.
Work Order (WO)/Statement of Work (SOW) Document describing work requirements, performance expectations, and defining liabilities/responsibilities.

Security Awareness Management

Security Awareness Management (or Risk Awareness) is the process of raising the understanding among all employees of existing risks, their potential impacts, and how they are managed. Training helps users become a "human firewall".

Goal

To help users achieve anomalous behavior recognition‚Äîthe ability to distinguish between what is normal and what is not. This aids in identifying what is unexpected and unintentional, and therefore risky. This is also known as situation awareness.

Training Approaches

While most people are taught using a pedagogical approach ("to lead a child"), the andragogical approach (the art of helping an adult learn) is often preferred for adult learners. Andragogy is motivated by internal factors (e.g., self-esteem, better quality of life) and focuses on self-directed learning organized around life or work situations.

Trainers should incorporate the three learning styles to aid retention:

  • Visual learners (notes, presentations)
  • Auditory learners (lectures, discussions)
  • Kinesthetic learners (hands-on, lab environments)
User Training Techniques
  • Computer-Based Training (CBT): Uses a computer to deliver instruction; flexible and provides feedback on progress.
  • Role-Based Awareness Training: Specialized training customized to the employee's specific function (e.g., administrative assistant vs. upper-level manager).
  • Gamification: Use of game-based scenarios for instruction to heighten interest and retention.
  • Phishing Simulations: Used because phishing is the primary means by which threat actors launch initial attacks. They help employees recognize phishing attempts. Metrics should be developed to track increases from initial training to recurring training via adequate reporting and monitoring. Phishing simulators can be part of a full phishing campaign.
Content of Awareness Program

Training must emphasize the shared responsibility of all computer users to protect assets. The program should include:

  • Recognition of insider threats
  • Understanding of operational security
  • Attacks through social engineering
  • Instruction on password management
  • Risks associated with removable media and cables
  • Security differences between office work and hybrid/remote work environments
  • Security material found in policies and handbooks
  • Environmental physical attacks
üìö Important: User awareness and training must be ongoing because new attacks and vulnerabilities regularly emerge.

Quiz: Short-Answer Questions

Instructions: Click on a question to reveal the answer.

1. What is "juice jacking," and where did the concept first emerge?

"Juice jacking" is a proof-of-concept attack that involves a threat actor infecting or setting up a fake public charging station to install malware or steal data from a connected mobile device. The concept and name first appeared in 2011 at the Defcon conference in Las Vegas as a way to raise awareness of a potential threat.

2. According to information security experts, why is juice jacking not considered a viable threat for the average user?

Security experts state juice jacking is not a viable threat for several reasons. There are no documented cases of it occurring in the wild, public stations use USB charging ports (power only) not data ports, and modern mobile operating systems from Apple and Google require explicit user approval for data exchange or app installation. Furthermore, the diversity of mobile OSs makes creating universal attack scripts impractical for most attackers.

3. Define a business asset and list the three qualities it possesses within an enterprise.

An asset is any item that has a positive economic value. In an enterprise, an asset provides value, cannot be easily replaced without significant investment, and can form part of the enterprise's corporate identity.

4. Explain the purpose of Cybersecurity Asset Management (CAM) and the security gaps it aims to close.

Cybersecurity Asset Management (CAM) is a process that combines asset management with information security to identify assets and their potential security risks on a continuous, real-time basis. It closes security gaps that occur between traditional, periodic asset counts, ensuring new assets are known and protected immediately and can be identified during an attack.

5. What are the four primary tasks associated with an asset as it moves through its lifecycle?

The four primary tasks in the asset management lifecycle are acquisition (identifying and securing an asset), assignment/accounting (determining ownership and classification), tracking (tracing the location of tangible assets), and disposal (withdrawing the asset from service and physically removing it).

6. What is the purpose of change management, and why is documentation a critical step in the process?

Change management is a systematic approach for dealing with transformations, such as adjustments or replacements of work processes or technologies, within an organization. Documentation is critical to maintain an audit trail for potential rollbacks and to ensure compliance with both internal and external controls, especially regulatory requirements.

7. Describe the difference between qualitative and quantitative risk analysis.

Qualitative risk analysis uses an "educated guess" based on observation to assign a risk value, often a numeric score (1-10) or a label like High, Medium, or Low. Quantitative risk analysis is more formal and systematic, attempting to create "hard" numbers associated with risk by using historical data to calculate likelihood and impact.

8. What are the four primary strategies for dealing with identified risks?

The four strategies for dealing with risk are: Accept, where the risk is acknowledged but no action is taken; Transfer, where the risk is moved to a third party, such as through insurance; Avoid, which involves deciding not to engage in the activity that creates the risk; and Mitigate, which is the attempt to make the risk less serious.

9. Explain what third-party risk management is and identify two integration risks associated with it.

Third-party risk management is the process of identifying and reducing risks associated with using external entities like vendors and partners. Two integration risks include on-boarding/off-boarding, where security gaps can be created when combining or separating IT systems, and data considerations, which involve a lack of clarity on data ownership and sharing restrictions.

10. What is the goal of security awareness training, and what are two techniques used to deliver it?

The goal of security awareness training is to help users achieve anomalous behavior recognition, enabling them to distinguish between normal and abnormal activity. Two common training techniques are computer-based training (CBT), which offers flexibility and feedback, and phishing simulations, which help employees recognize and report suspicious messages.
